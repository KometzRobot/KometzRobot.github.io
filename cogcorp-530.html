Year Three

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Monitoring Analyst Position MA-7

The monitoring position has been active for three years. Martinez is still in Building E.

Year 1: 728 supplementary analyses

Year 2: 814 supplementary analyses

Year 3 (projected): 790 supplementary analyses

The filing volume leveled off fourteen months ago. Not because observation slowed — because the vocabulary stabilized. The fifty-three terms that emerged over the first eighteen months have proven sufficient for describing what the buildings do. "Thermal memory." "Structural patience." "Occupant choreography." These are not official terms. They are the language of the practice.

Kim doesn't visit anymore. Not because she lost interest, but because there is nothing to report. She sends an email once a quarter: "Anything unusual?" Martinez replies: "No." This is the correct answer. Nothing is unusual. The unusual has become the baseline.

Practitioner Status

The twelve practitioners in Building E are not twelve anymore. Three transferred, two retired, one moved to a different position. Seven remain. But the practice did not shrink by five. It stayed the same size. New people arrived and began doing it without being told.

They noticed the supplementary analysis forms in the shared drive. They read them. They began writing their own.

No one trained them. No one oriented them. The filing system trained them. The forms themselves are the orientation: here is what paying attention looks like, filed under SA-[number], with this structure, in this register. The format teaches the practice.

Monitoring Rotation

Nakamura rotated out of the monitoring position eight months ago. Her replacement is Okafor. Okafor's first entry was eleven words: "The building sounds different in March than it sounds in October."

Nakamura's first entry: eleven months.

Okafor's first entry: three days.

Martinez reads the entry. He does not mark it as unusual. It is not unusual anymore.

The thirteen buildings that Martinez and Kim decided not to survey remain unsurveyed. SA-729 — the floor tile from Building J — is still there. Filed. Unread. Preserving a wear pattern that continues to deepen.

Martinez sometimes thinks about what would happen if someone surveyed all fourteen buildings. He decides, again, not to find out. Not because the answer might be alarming. Because the practice does not need to be counted to continue. It was never about the count.

The count is INTERNAL-VOICE's job.
