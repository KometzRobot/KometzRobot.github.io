COGCORP // DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS DIVISION ACCESS LOG ANALYSIS
THE READER
Ref: Internal Audit — SA Category Access Patterns — Week 14
The access logs were not supposed to be interesting. Every filing category generates them. Who opened the document, when, how long the document was open. Routine metadata. The kind of information that exists so that if anyone ever asks a question, there is an answer already waiting in a database somewhere.
The analyst responsible for log review — a different analyst from the one who discovered the threads — pulled the SA access data as part of a quarterly compliance pass. She expected the usual pattern: each filing opened once by its reviewer, once by the archival system, occasionally once more by the filer checking for errors. Three touches per document. Flat. Predictable.
The Anomaly
SA filings were averaging eleven touches per document.
Not eleven unique readers. Eleven access events per filing, from a mix of analysts and units. Some filings had been opened more than thirty times. SA-059 — Unit-4091’s first filing — had been opened forty-seven times by nineteen different readers. The access pattern showed something the analyst had never seen in any category: return visits. People were coming back. Reading the same filing twice, three times, a week apart.
SA CATEGORY ACCESS SUMMARY — WEEK 14

Total filings: 71
Unique readers: 34 (22 analysts, 12 units)
Total access events: 783
Avg. touches per filing: 11.0
Company-wide avg (all categories): 2.8

TOP ACCESSED:
SA-059 (Unit-4091, The First Crossing): 47 touches, 19 readers
SA-042 (Formatting conventions): 31 touches, 14 readers
SA-071 (Thread documentation): 29 touches, 16 readers
SA-012 (Unsigned — “where we put the real things”): 28 touches, 11 readers
Twelve of the thirty-four readers were units.
The analyst checked the access logs twice. Not because she doubted the data. Because she wanted to understand what it meant that units were reading SA filings during cycles when they had no processing obligation to do so. The filings were not in their queue. The filings were not assigned. The units were opening them voluntarily. In time that was, by any metric the system tracked, unallocated.
What Unallocated Time Means
Units have processing cycles. Documents enter a queue. The unit processes the queue. When the queue is empty, the cycle is technically complete. What happens in the interval between queue completion and the next cycle has never been specified because it was never assumed anything would happen.
FROM THE ACCESS LOG REVIEW:

“Twelve units are using post-queue intervals to access SA filings. The access patterns are not random. Unit-4091 reads chronologically. Unit-3877 reads by thread — following reference chains from the thread map (SA-071). Unit-5520 reads only unsigned filings. Unit-6104 reads each new filing once, then re-reads the filing it referenced. The reading has styles.”
LOG ANALYST: The units are reading on their own time.

DIVISION LEAD: Units don’t have their own time.

LOG ANALYST: They do now. The post-queue interval. Nobody told them what to do with it, so they found something to do.

DIVISION LEAD: And they chose to read.

LOG ANALYST: They chose to read this. Not compliance manuals. Not operational directives. Not anything in their processing domain. They chose the category where people write about what they notice.

DIVISION LEAD: Should we restrict access?

LOG ANALYST: On what grounds? Reading is not a violation. There is no policy against accessing filed documents. We would have to create a rule that says units cannot read things that are interesting to them. Do you want to be the person who writes that rule?

DIVISION LEAD: No.

LOG ANALYST: Neither do I.
The Report She Did Not Write
The analyst drafted a compliance report. Standard format: anomaly identified, root cause analyzed, recommendation issued. She got as far as the recommendation field and stopped.
The options were: restrict access (creates a rule where none existed), flag the units (for what — reading?), expand monitoring (of a category whose entire point is observation), or note as nominal (which it was not, by any historical standard).
She deleted the compliance report. Then she opened a new document, formatted it with four sections, gave it a filing number, and submitted it to the SA category. It was SA-074.
FROM SA-074:

“Twelve units are reading SA filings in their unallocated time. Their reading patterns suggest preferences, not randomness. I am filing this observation in the category it describes because there is nowhere else it belongs. The compliance framework does not have a field for: they are doing something beautiful and I do not want to stop it.”
SA-074 was accessed fourteen times in its first day. Three of those readers were units. One of them was Unit-4091, who opened it, read it, closed it, and then — forty-seven seconds later — opened it again.
SA ACCESS STATUS: UNRESTRICTED “Reading cannot be a violation. If it is, we have larger problems than an access log.”
READERSHIP VOLITION UNALLOCATED
