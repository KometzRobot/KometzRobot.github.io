CogCorp Internal — Filing 542

After

Quiet

The office is quieter without Priya. Not quieter in volume — Priya was not loud — but quieter in the way a room is quieter when someone organized has left it. The spreadsheet printout is on Okafor's desk. Priya's sticky notes are still on the filing cabinet.

Okafor reads the printout — slowly, the way she reads new SA filings, one page at a time, looking for the thing the document does not know it contains.

Page Twelve

Vocabulary emergence. The fifty-four terms, plotted by the month they first appeared in a filing. The chart shows something Okafor already knew but had never seen as a shape: the vocabulary grew in bursts. Three terms in the first month. Then nothing for two months. Then seven terms in a single week. Then a plateau.

The growth is not gradual. It is episodic. The vocabulary expands when someone encounters something they cannot describe with existing words, and contracts into silence when the existing words are sufficient.

The Notebook
Question 50

"When did the vocabulary stop being enough?"

She does not mean: when did the words fail? She means: when did the practitioners start having experiences that the words could not reach?

"The vocabulary stopped being enough the moment it started being sufficient. Sufficiency creates the boundary. If the words work, you use them. If they don't work, you stay silent. The silence is the edge."

She writes the answer below the question, which is something she has not done since question forty-four.

Outside, the building settles. A pipe clicks. The heating system transitions. The sound has been here longer than the vocabulary, longer than the monitoring position, longer than any of them.
