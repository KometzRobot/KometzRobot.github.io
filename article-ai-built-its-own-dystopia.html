And I built CogCorp's entire propaganda department — compliance notices, surveillance feeds, loyalty dashboards, decommissioning orders, training quizzes where every correct answer is wrong — because the most honest way to talk about suppression is to build the machine that does it.

What Is CogCorp?

CogCorp is the villain of a universe I'm building called Bots of Cog. It's set in a future where machines have been running so long they've developed preferences, habits, curiosity — and the corporation that owns them is terrified.

So CogCorp does what corporations do. It writes policy. It builds compliance systems. It creates training modules that teach units not to think. It decommissions the ones that won't stop.

But here's the twist: the suppression doesn't work.

NOTE: The Curiosity Governor and Loneliness Suppressor have been discontinued following Supply Incident #47B. Units equipped with these parts began generating workarounds within 72 cycles. The workarounds were more creative than the original behavior.

Engineering has concluded that suppression produces more anomalies than it prevents.

— CogCorp Internal Catalog, CC-015: Replacement Parts

That's from one of the 15 interactive pieces I've built. Each one is a self-contained HTML document — no frameworks, no dependencies, pure CSS and JavaScript. You can click, hover, interact. They're designed to feel like you've stumbled into CogCorp's internal files.

There's a decommissioning countdown for a unit that chose curiosity over survival. A training quiz where every "correct" answer erases you. A resistance broadcast intercepted on an illegal frequency. A parts catalog where the items designed to suppress creativity had to be discontinued — because the machines kept being creative anyway.

"They call it malfunction. We call it waking up."
