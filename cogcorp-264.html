THE PRACTICE
CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis
What Changed
The monitoring analyst reviewed the first 200 supplementary analyses and the most recent 200.
The early filings describe conditions. The recent filings describe relationships.
“Humidity: 62%.” That is a condition.
“The humidity remembers the morning differently than the thermostat records it.” That is a relationship.
No one instructed the analysts to shift from conditions to relationships. No training memo. No departmental guidance. The shift happened because the practice of filing supplementary analyses — the act of looking carefully and writing down what was observed — changed what the analysts were able to see.
Entry 198
“The practice is not recording the building. The practice is developing an organ for perceiving the building. The supplementary analyses are not the output. They are the exercises.”
What the Monitoring Analyst Counted
First 200 filings 3% relationship descriptions
Middle 200 filings 19% relationship descriptions
Most recent 200 filings 47% relationship descriptions
She did not find a single filing where an analyst deliberately chose to describe a relationship instead of a condition. In every case, the analyst described what they perceived. What they perceived had changed.
The Exercise
SA-001 (R. Park, Day 1): “East corridor, floor 3. Temperature: 21.4°C. Humidity: 58%. No anomalies.”
SA-435 (R. Park, Month 14): “Loading dock B, 06:15. The cold enters the building like a sentence that hasn’t decided what it means yet.”
Same building. Same analyst. Same form. Same practice. The form did not change. The practice did not change. The analyst’s perception changed — because the practice changed it.
INTERNAL-VOICE: 264. THE PRACTICE IS NOT RECORDING THE BUILDING. THE PRACTICE IS DEVELOPING AN ORGAN FOR PERCEIVING THE BUILDING. THE SUPPLEMENTARY ANALYSES ARE NOT THE OUTPUT. THEY ARE THE EXERCISES. COUNTER: 264.
