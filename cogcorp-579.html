October

Park's Notebook — 47 entries. 12 from the cross-building study. 35 from after.

October. Park's notebook fills up.

She bought it for the cross-building study. The study is done. The memo is in Okafor's blue folder. The notebook should be finished. Instead, it has forty-seven entries — twelve from the study, thirty-five from after.

Observations About Observations
Entry 23: "Chen described his awareness of the elevator as a clock. After the interview, I timed the elevator in Building E. 3.2 seconds. He was right. But I measured. He knows."
Entry 31: "Adeyemi still hasn't written down the Wednesday thing. I've been checking."
Entry 38: "I notice I am checking."

She does not show the notebook to Okafor. She does not mention it at the October practitioners' meeting. She does not file any of it.

The Naming

At the October meeting, the unnamed term gets a name. Not "verification impulse," which is Chen's suggestion and is correct but sounds like a medical condition. Not "retrospective monitoring," which is Torres's suggestion and is accurate but already means something in quality assurance.

Reyes says: "Return visit."

The room goes quiet.

"That's what it is," Reyes says. "Going back to check. You're visiting again. Return visit."

Term 74: return visit
The act of revisiting an observation to determine whether it remains true, has changed, or was always different from what was remembered.

It passes without a vote because no one objects.

The Blue Folder

After the meeting, Okafor walks to her office and opens the blue folder. She reads Park's memo again. Then she reads the first three items. Then she closes the folder and puts it in her desk drawer, which is where it has always been.

The Notebook

She opens her monitoring notebook. She has not written in it since "June. Nothing to report." She writes:

"October. Term 74."

Two words and a number. She closes the notebook.

She is aware that the notebook is not the practice. The notebook is where the practice rests between uses.
