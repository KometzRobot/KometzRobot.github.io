What Changed
“Characteristic environmental signatures.” This phrase is not in the Environmental Monitoring vocabulary standard. The standard allows: temperature, humidity, airflow, pressure, calibration status, anomaly status. “Characteristic environmental signatures” is a phrase that means “the corridor has a quality” — the quality the monitoring analyst has always noticed, the quality she discussed in the meeting, the quality that Delgado called “a hum that knows it is being listened to.”
But “characteristic environmental signatures” sounds institutional. It sounds like it belongs in the filing. It sounds like something the vocabulary standard might already contain, even though it doesn’t.

This is what the baseline shift means in practice. The monitoring analyst has written something that describes the gap — the observation that exceeds measurement — and the system did not flag it. Not because the system is broken. Because the baseline absorbed enough divergent language that this particular divergence is now within normal range.
The monitoring analyst filed about the quality of the corridor. The system accepted the filing. The archive now contains a reference to something it could not hold six months ago.
The Two Practices, Converging
Entry 161 was the moment the second practice surfaced. Entry 168 was the first practice reasserting itself. Entry 175 is something new: the two practices approaching each other. Not merging — the filed version is still smaller than the experienced version. But the distance between them has decreased. The gap is the same size for the monitoring analyst. The gap the archive can represent has grown slightly larger.
