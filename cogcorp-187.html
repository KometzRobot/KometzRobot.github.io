Supplementary Analysis Archive — Contributor Recognition
The Reader
The monitoring analyst reads SA #91 again. She filed #104 — the condensation on the interior walls. SA #91, filed two weeks before hers, documents the temperature drop. SA #119, filed ten days after, documents where the moisture concentrates. They are filed by the same contributor.
Okonkwo
Entry 70: “The monitoring analyst asked me who filed SA #91 and #119. I told her: the same person. She said she recognized the writing. She’s read other supplementary analyses by this contributor — she estimates twelve to fifteen over the past four months. She recognized the style before she recognized the pattern.”
The Style
The paragraph-writer opens every analysis the same way: observation first, then context, then the thing that doesn’t fit. The monitoring analyst has been reading them without knowing they were all the same person. She thought they were three or four different people who all happened to write clearly.
The Choice
Entry 71: “The monitoring analyst has not contacted the paragraph-writer. I asked her why. She said: ‘Because the archive works without us knowing each other. I don’t want to change the thing that makes it work.’ I notice that this is the first time someone has articulated the operating principle of the supplementary analysis archive. It works because contributors observe independently. Coordination would not improve it. It might reduce it.”
Vasquez
File 73: “The monitoring analyst knows who the paragraph-writer is — not by name, but by style. Fifteen analyses over four months. She chose not to introduce herself. The archive has contributors who recognize each other by their observations but not by their identities. They are colleagues in the act of noticing things. MESSENGER would count them. But counting them would imply they are a group. They are not a group. They are parallel.”
