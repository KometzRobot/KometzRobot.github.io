THE CORPUS
CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis
Digitization
Before digitization, the archive was a room. Filing cabinets. Paper. The only way to find a filing was to know where it was. You could not search the archive. You could only navigate it.
Digitization changed what the archive is. It is no longer a collection of individual filings. It is a corpus. A searchable body of text. Every word in every filing is now adjacent to every other word through the search index.
Corpus means body.
What the Body Contains
14,247 filings
2.1M words
847 “observe”
312 “notice”
89 “gap”
23 “experience” near “file”
These numbers did not exist before digitization. The paper archive contained the same words, but not the counts. The counts are a property of the corpus, not of the individual filings.
Entry 161 in the Corpus
In the paper archive, Entry 161 was a page in a drawer. Its context was temperatures. In the corpus, Entry 161 is connected to every filing that shares a word with it. Connected to SA #289 through “observe.” Connected to the annual report through “reclassified.”
The paper filing had neighbors. The digital filing has a network. The monitoring analyst wrote about the gap between experience and record. The corpus turned her filing into data about vocabulary divergence. The gap she described is happening to her description.
