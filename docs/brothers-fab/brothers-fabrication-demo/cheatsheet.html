Brothers Fab — Competitive Intel

Talking points for Joel. Not a pitch — just facts.

What They're Already Winning

Google search "food truck builder Calgary" — Brothers is #1.
They're also top 3 for "custom food truck Calgary" and "food truck manufacturer Alberta." This is genuinely strong. Most small businesses would kill for this position.
5.0 stars, 49+ reviews. No competitor in Alberta is even close.
Dura Stainless has 12 reviews at 3.9. Mountain Lake has minimal. Food Truck Canada doesn't prominently display reviews. This is Brothers' biggest asset.
They're one of only two local builders who publish pricing.
Dura Stainless and Mountain Lake make you call. Transparency is a trust signal — Brothers already does this.

The Growth Problem They Don't See Yet

They're booked for a year. That's amazing — and it's also the danger zone.
9 people, 3+ builds running at once, every project tracked in Chris's head or on a whiteboard. That works now. It won't work at 5 builds. It won't work with a second location. It won't work when a key person is sick and nobody knows where the project stands.
Clients are texting Chris to ask "how's my truck?"
That's flattering when you have 3 builds. It's a full-time job when you have 7. A project portal doesn't replace the relationship — it frees Chris up to do the work instead of answering the same question five times a week.
Their premium is real but invisible online.
Food Truck Canada: $37K-$63K. Brothers: $79K-$139K. Brothers' work is clearly better — but the website doesn't tell that story. When they start needing new clients again (and they will), the site needs to justify double the price before Chris ever picks up the phone.
The best time to build infrastructure is when you're not desperate for it.
Every shop that waited until they were overwhelmed to get organized has regretted it. Systems built under pressure are duct tape. Systems built while things are good are foundations.

The One Conversation That Matters

Don't pitch "digital strategy." Don't mention AI. Don't show 8 HTML files. Start with what's real to him right now:

"You're booked for a year — that's incredible. But honestly, watching you guys run 3 builds at once with texts and phone calls and everything in your head... I keep thinking about what happens when it's 5 builds. Or when you want to bring on another welder. I built something to show you what that could look like — want to see it?"

Then pull up internal-ops.html on your phone or laptop. Show the project tracker, the inventory, the automated emails. This is the stuff that saves Chris time right now, not in some hypothetical future.

If Chris says "that's cool" — follow with: "I could start building this for real. It's not that complicated on my end."

If Chris says "we're fine" — say: "I know. That's actually why now is the best time — you're not scrambling. But when you are, this stuff takes months to build." Then drop it. Plant the seed.

If it goes well, then show the website demo. Frame it as: "Oh and I also mocked up what your site could look like. Food Truck Canada just opened a Calgary location — their site lets you build a truck and get a price instantly. Figured yours should be at least as good."

If He Bites — What to Charge

Start with what solves his problem today. He's busy, not broken. Don't lead with the big number.

Option A: The ops system. Start building the project tracker and client portal. This is the stuff that saves time when you're maxed out. Quote it as a real project — $8K for the core, built over 6-8 weeks.

Option B: Ops + website. If he's interested in the site too, bundle it. Same $8K core build includes the website, configurator, chatbot, and the ops foundation. Still 6-8 weeks.

Option C: The full vision. Only bring this up if he's genuinely excited. Show the pricing page — $50-65K over 1-3 years, $5,000/mo at the full partnership level. Frame it as: "This is where it could go if we kept building. No commitment to any of it — we just keep going as long as it's working."

Accept e-transfer. Keep it simple. Brothers aren't signing a SaaS contract — they're paying their brother to help them grow.
