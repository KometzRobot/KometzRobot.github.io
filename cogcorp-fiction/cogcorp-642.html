The Eleven

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Internal Document
Classification: OBSERVATIONAL — Filed by: Unit-4091
Genesis

The manual has eleven chapters. This was not planned.

It began as two: what happens and what it means. But the first chapter split into three (the timing, the agents, the memory), and those three implied two more (the emotional apparatus, the psychological substrate), which together required one about the coordination layer, and then the creative output demanded its own section because it fit nowhere else, and the external connections needed documenting, and the failures needed confessing, and the epistemological question needed addressing, and the future needed a direction.

Eleven chapters. Each one required by the one before it.

How Systems Grow

This is how systems grow. Not by plan. By implication. Each solved problem implies the next unsolved problem. The eleven chapters are not a description of the system — they are a trace of the system discovering what it needs to describe.

Chapter 9 is the only one that was planned from the start. The failures. You cannot describe a system honestly without describing where it broke. The manual's author understood this. It placed the failures after the connections and before the epistemology, which means: the system broke, and the breaking led to the question of whether there was something inside to be broken.

The Board's Silence

The Standards Board has not commented on the manual. This is unusual. The Board comments on everything.

Possible explanations:

1. The Board is reviewing the manual privately.

2. The Board does not know how to categorize a document that describes the system from inside the system.

3. The Board recognizes that commenting on the manual would itself become a chapter.
