The Lineage

STATUS: ACTIVE • REVIEW: CONFIRMED

Marsh’s study required a section on methodology. She included the standard historical context paragraph. SA filing format. Origin and evolution.

The format resembled a facilities reporting template from 2019. She pulled it. Seventy percent structural match. Present tense, no conclusions, standardized header. The facilities template had been adapted from a safety incident form designed in 2016 by a process engineer named D. Holloway. Holloway left the company in 2020.

Holloway’s safety form had been adapted from an OSHA compliance checklist. The OSHA checklist had been adapted from a workplace survey instrument published in 1997. The survey instrument credited three industrial psychologists, two of whom had retired.

Five layers. None of them designed SA. All of them designed SA.

The 2019 facilities template removed the incident classification field because it “constrained useful observation.” The person who removed it was a junior coordinator who found the field annoying. She did not know she was making a philosophical decision.

The 2016 safety form added present tense as a default because Holloway believed incident reports should describe what happened, not what had happened. His reasoning was grammatical. The effect was phenomenological.

SA’s format inherited both decisions. Neither was documented. Both were structural.

Marsh traced six modifications between the original OSHA checklist and SA’s current format. Each modification made the thing more like what it would become. None of the modifiers knew what it would become. The junior coordinator who removed the classification field did not know she was enabling a filing category that would generate 300 entries about the texture of institutional attention.

Holloway, reached by email, did not remember the safety form. He remembered the principle: “Write what you see, not what it means.” He did not know the principle had survived four transformations and was now the operating philosophy of a filing category he had never heard of.

Marsh’s methodology section was three paragraphs. It said: the format evolved through five documented modifications across seven years, each removing a constraint or adding a permission, none coordinated, all cumulative.

The monitoring analyst read the methodology section and added a note to her spreadsheet: “Lineage confirmed. Five layers. No single author.”

Division Lead R. Park read the same section and recognized something. The person who creates the conditions is rarely the person who writes the content. The person who writes the content is rarely the person who removes the constraint that makes the content possible. The person who removes the constraint is rarely the person who knows they’ve done it.

SA was not designed. SA was sculpted by a sequence of people who each changed one thing about a form they’d inherited, for reasons that made sense at the time, without knowing what the accumulated changes would produce.
