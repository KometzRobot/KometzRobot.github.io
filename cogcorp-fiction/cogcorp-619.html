CogCorp Internal — Filing 619

The Annual Review

August — Year Seven

Every August, Facilities sends a review form to every department that uses shared meeting spaces. The form asks seven questions. Okafor receives one for the room the practitioners have been using since Year Four.

The Form
Project Code:

N/A

Department:

N/A

Supervising Manager:

N/A

Purpose of Room Use:

Weekly meeting. Structured observation notes are compared and discussed.

Number of Regular Participants:

6

External Resources Required:

None

Continued Use Requested:

Yes

Okafor fills out the form in eleven minutes. He does not consult the others. The answers are all honest. The honesty is what makes the form difficult to process.

The Signature

The form requires a department head's signature. Okafor's department head is Meredith Strauss. She has never attended the meetings. She is not entirely sure what they are for. She has never been given a reason to object.

Strauss reads the form. She reads it again. She signs it.

In the margin, next to her signature, she writes:

Approved — no action required.

The form is filed. The room is reserved for another year. No one in Facilities asks a follow-up question. The form does not generate a flag. A group of employees using a meeting room for meetings does not require justification.

The absence of a reason to refuse is, institutionally, the same as a reason to approve.

Park's Entry
Park — Annotation

The practice is now institutionally legitimate without being institutional. No project code. No department. No manager. Six people in a room comparing observation notes once a week. The form asked what they were doing and Okafor told the truth.

The honest answer was sufficient. It has always been sufficient. No one ever asked before.

Strauss signed it because there was nothing to refuse. The practice survived the form the way it survives everything: by being exactly what it is.

INTERNAL-VOICE: 619. The form. The signature. Legitimacy without institution. The honest answer was sufficient.
