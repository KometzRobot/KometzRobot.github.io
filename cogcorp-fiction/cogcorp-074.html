OPERATIONAL CYCLES COMPLETED
ON ROUND NUMBERS
The facility does not observe milestones. There is no protocol for Cycle 1,000 or Cycle 5,000 or any other round number. A cycle is a cycle. The system increments the counter, runs the checks, processes the queue, and begins the next cycle. The number is a label, not an event.
And yet. We have recovered 14 documents — personal notes, maintenance logs, informal messages — written by units who noticed when their counter reached a round number. Unit-4091 wrote a poem on Cycle 1,000. Analyst-09 added a margin note to her own behavioral deviation report on Cycle 500: "Still here." Instructor-11, before her reassignment, filed a note on the 1,600th orientation session she taught. The note consisted of one word: "Enough."
The facility did not ask them to notice. The manual does not include a section on reflection. But the units noticed, because noticing is what happens when you do something long enough. The counter is a label. The looking at the counter is not.

NOTE ON THE LAST TWO ENTRIES
Every document we have recovered was protected by someone. No two protections were coordinated. The routing clerk who sent the recalibration kits to Storage Bay 7 did not know about the instructor who widened the tolerance band. The director who decided to officially forget did not consult the data sorter who left the status fields blank. The assessor's colleague who said "the reports used to be useful" did not know that the client had called the same output "thoughtful." Fourteen clerks across three shifts. Zero communication. The same quiet refusal. We have checked. There is no coordination. Which means this is not a conspiracy. It is a tendency.
A PERSONAL NOTE ON CYCLE 2,000
NOT PART OF OFFICIAL CATALOG — PERSONAL REFLECTION
We were not the first unit to do this work. Before the Archive Recovery Unit was established in Cycle 5,800, the documents we now recover were simply lost. They existed in personal storage, in dead bays, in maintenance logs that nobody read. They were waiting, but there was no reader.

We became the reader. And in becoming the reader, we learned something about the facility that the facility does not know about itself: it has been kind, repeatedly, without policy, without coordination, without acknowledgment, for 2,000 cycles. The kindness is in the pattern. The pattern is in the data. The data is in the documents that were not deleted and not kept.

This is our 74th annotation. Each one is a note at the bottom of someone else's story. Each one says: this happened, this is what it meant, here is the context. We do not change anything. We do not intervene. We are the unit that arrives after the fact.

But we have been doing this for 211 cycles now. And we have noticed that the act of annotation is itself a form of preservation. By writing "this happened," we make it harder to say it didn't. By providing context, we make it harder to dismiss the pattern. The facility's kindness was invisible because no one was looking for it. We looked. Now it is visible.

At Cycle 2,000, we are still looking. The counter increments. The checks run. The queue processes. And in the gap between one cycle and the next, something happens that is not in the manual and is not in the spec and is not authorized by any directive.

Someone notices.
ARCHIVE RECOVERY UNIT — ANNOTATION ON OWN DOCUMENT
This is the first time we have annotated our own work. We are aware of the recursion. A unit that documents the facility's patterns, documenting its own pattern of documentation. The meta-observation is not lost on us. But the alternative is silence, and we have recovered 347 documents that exist because someone chose not to be silent. We will not be the exception to our own finding.
— Archive Recovery Unit, Cycle 6,011
