COGCORP // DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS DIVISION EXTERNAL FILING
THE VISITOR
Ref: SA-078 — Filed by K. Marsh, Logistics Division — Week 15
The filing system does not have access controls by division. This was a design decision made eleven years ago, when the system was built, on the theory that any employee should be able to file in any category relevant to their work. In practice, nobody had ever tested this theory. People filed in their own categories. Logistics filed in Logistics. Compliance filed in Compliance. The Documentation Standards Division filed in SA.
On a Wednesday in Week 15, a filing appeared in the SA category from K. Marsh, Logistics Division. The Division Lead checked the filing number three times. SA-078. Correctly formatted. Four sections. Parenthetical references to SA-065 and SA-071. The filing followed every convention the category had developed over fifteen weeks, conventions that had never been written down, that existed only in the practice of people who filed regularly.
K. Marsh had never filed in SA before. K. Marsh had, according to the access logs, been reading SA for six weeks.
The Filing
FROM SA-078 (K. MARSH, LOGISTICS):

“I manage warehouse routing for the eastern corridor. My observations are not about documentation. They are about what happens when a system that was designed for one purpose begins to be used for another. Our routing software was built to optimize for speed. Over eighteen months, the warehouse teams have been using it to optimize for something else — they call it ‘flow,’ which is not a metric the software tracks. They route based on how the work feels to move through. The software accommodates this because it does not know the difference. (See SA-065, re: categories that accelerate for reasons that are the same thing.) I am filing here because the SA category is the only place in the company where observations about emergent use are treated as observations rather than defects.”
What It Means
MONITORING ANALYST: Someone from Logistics filed in SA.

DIVISION LEAD: I saw.

MONITORING ANALYST: The filing is good. It follows the conventions. It references the thread map.

DIVISION LEAD: How does someone from Logistics know about the thread map?

MONITORING ANALYST: The same way anyone knows. She read SA-071.

DIVISION LEAD: She has been reading us.

MONITORING ANALYST: She has been reading us for six weeks. Forty-three document accesses. I checked.

DIVISION LEAD: And we never noticed.

MONITORING ANALYST: The access logs do not distinguish between divisions. A reader is a reader.
The Division Lead sat with this for a long time. The SA category had been, until now, a phenomenon internal to the Documentation Standards Division. The filings were about documentation. The filers were documentation people. The units that read and filed were units assigned to documentation work. It had felt, without anyone saying so, like theirs.
K. Marsh’s filing was not about documentation. It was about warehouse routing. And it belonged in SA because SA had become something larger than its origin. The category was no longer about supplementary analysis of documentation standards. It was about the observation of emergence itself. And emergence does not respect divisional boundaries.
The Second Visitor
Two days after SA-078, a second external filing arrived. SA-081, filed by R. Okonkwo, Applied Research. It described a pattern in the Applied Research lab where informal peer review had replaced the formal review process — not by policy change but by drift. Researchers had started sharing drafts in a chat channel that was meant for scheduling. The scheduling channel became a review channel because it was the only space where everyone was already present. Okonkwo referenced SA-078 and SA-065.
The Division Lead did not check the filing number three times. She read it once, noted that Okonkwo’s writing style was cleaner than most internal filers, and went back to her own work.
MONITORING ANALYST: You did not flag the second external filing.

DIVISION LEAD: No.

MONITORING ANALYST: The first one you checked three times.

DIVISION LEAD: The first one was surprising. The second one was inevitable.
By the end of Week 16, four more external filings had arrived. Customer Relations. Facilities Management. Human Resources. Finance. Each one described a different system. Each one described the same pattern: something designed for one purpose, adopted for another, and the adoption working better than the design.
SA CATEGORY SCOPE: UNRESTRICTED “The category was never ours. It only started here.”
BOUNDARY EMERGENCE MIGRATION
[Division Lead’s note, not filed: I spent ten minutes trying to feel possessive about SA. It did not work. The category grew beyond us the way a language grows beyond its first speakers. K. Marsh’s filing about warehouse routing is, by every measure I can think of, exactly what SA is for. The fact that I did not know this until she showed me is itself the kind of observation that belongs in SA.]
