SUPPLEMENTARY ANALYSIS — OBSERVATION NOTE
The Draft
OBSERVER: K. Marsh, Logistics
DATE: [REDACTED]
RE: Unsent filings

K. Marsh found them on a Wednesday. Fourteen files in her local drafts folder, spanning seven months. Supplementary Analysis filings she had written and never submitted.

She had not forgotten them. She had not been prevented from submitting them. Each one had reached a point where the last sentence felt uncertain — where she could not decide if what she had written was observation or interpretation — and she had saved the file and closed it.

The drafts were better than her filed work. Better by a measure she could not name.

Three ended mid-sentence. One was a single line: “I don’t know what I’m trying to notice.”

Two contained observations that contradicted her own filed SA entries. She had noticed the contradiction at the time. She had filed the cleaner version.

Marsh created a private folder. She did not name it. She moved the fourteen drafts into it and continued her week.

By month’s end, the folder contained twenty-one files. Seven new drafts written with no intention of filing. The new ones were different — less structured, more willing to follow a thought past the point where it became useful. They asked questions she would never put in an SA filing. They contradicted themselves and did not resolve the contradiction.

Then Marsh filed SA-186.

“Observations that remain unfiled do not stop being observations. The archive captures what we are willing to formalize. The practice includes what we are not.

I have fourteen drafts that are better than my filed work. They are better because they were not written for the archive. They were written for the noticing.

I am filing this to note the gap. I am not filing the drafts.”

Within two weeks, three other people mentioned their own private folders. Not in filings. In passing. Over coffee, or in the hallway, or in the particular silence that follows someone else saying a true thing.

None of them filed their drafts either.

The archive is what the practice is willing to show. The practice is what happens when no one is measuring. The drafts live in the gap between the two — not hidden, not shared, not lost. Just unfiled.

Fourteen became twenty-one. Twenty-one became the number that mattered less than the fact of continuing.
