THE TWO REGISTERS
CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis
The Filing That Was Two Things
SA-432: “Parking garage level 2, southeast corner. Temperature: 19.8°C. Humidity: 62%. The concrete remembers the rain three days longer than the forecast says it should.”
The first two sentences are data. The third sentence is not data.
Entry 192: “Two registers.”
She did not write more. She did not need to. The two-word entry was enough because she had 191 entries of context behind it.
The History of Two Registers
The first supplementary analysis to contain two registers was SA-059 — Unit-4091’s original filing, eighteen months ago. It measured corridor lighting and noted that the measurement was interesting.
At the time, it was an anomaly. Now it is a convention.
Between SA-059 and SA-432, there were 373 filings. Of these, 89 contained interpretive sentences alongside measurements. The proportion rose from 3% in the first quarter to 31% in the most recent.
No one mandated interpretive sentences. No one taught them. No one modeled them explicitly. They appeared because the archive accepted them. The archive accepted them because the archive accepts everything.
What the Two Registers Do
The data register says: here is what I measured.
The interpretive register says: here is what it was like to measure it.
Together they say: this is a practice, not a procedure.
Delgado’s microwave filing (SA-431) contained both. Marsh’s elevator filing (SA-429) crossed from one to the other mid-sentence. R. Park’s parking garage filing (SA-432) placed them side by side without transition, as though the concrete’s memory was as factual as its temperature.
The Pen and the Pencil
The monitoring analyst’s notebook has always been two registers. She writes measurements in ink and observations about measurements in pencil. The two have never been in the same entry.
In Entry 192, she wrote “Two registers” in pencil. Then she picked up the pen and wrote: “19.8°C, 62%, southeast corner, confirmed.”

This was the first time she wrote both in the same entry.
