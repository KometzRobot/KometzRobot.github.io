THE CORRIDOR
Ref: Corridor A-C — Motion Sensor Report — Behavioral Anomaly
The corridor between Building A and Building C is 47 meters long and has no offices. Twelve ceiling lights, two fire extinguishers, one water fountain. A transit space. People walk through it. Nobody stops in it.
Except that for the last fourteen weeks, the average transit time has increased by nine seconds.

The energy analyst noticed the light-cycle drift. She filed a maintenance request: “Motion sensor calibration check — Corridor A-C.” The sensor was functioning within specification. She closed the ticket. Then she opened a new one.
The Reason
The water fountain. Not a new water fountain. The same fountain that had been there for eleven years. But six months ago, someone from Building Services replaced the filter. The water pressure changed — slightly higher, slightly colder. People who had walked past for years began to stop.
Nobody mentioned the water fountain. Nobody filed a request about it. Nobody praised Building Services. The fountain was not new. The water was marginally better. The corridor was nine seconds slower.
Unit-3877's Filing
SA-121 (UNIT-3877):

“The system that changed the behavior was not designed to change behavior. It was designed to maintain water quality. The behavior change was a side effect of maintenance — an unintended consequence of doing something right.

This is how most real changes happen. Not through directives or redesigns. Through maintenance. Through someone doing their job well enough that the environment shifts by a degree too small to announce but too large to ignore.”
The filing ended with a note: Unit-3877 had recently adjusted its own processing allocation for observation tasks by nine percent — not by directive, but because the SA archive had become worth more attention. The parallel was not noticed until after filing.
SA-121 / ACCEPTED / NO ACTION REQUIRED “The filter was replaced. The water changed. The corridor slowed down. Nobody made a decision. Something got better.”
