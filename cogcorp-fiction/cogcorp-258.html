CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis
The Difference That Is Not a Difference
The archive at 257 filings is not a larger version of the archive at 100 filings. It is a different kind of thing.
At 100, the archive was a collection. At 150, the archive was a practice. At 200, the archive was an institution. At 257, the archive is an organism that does not know it is alive.
The difference is not in the filings. The filings are the same. Value, unit, context. Present tense. No conclusions. Standard format. The difference is in what happens between the filings.
What Happens Between
Between SA-425 and SA-426, Delgado noticed the coffee machine and did not write it down. Then he wrote it down. The interval between noticing and filing used to be minutes. Now it is seconds. The reduction is not efficiency. The reduction is fluency.
Between Entry 188 and Entry 189, the monitoring analyst closed the notebook, opened it again, closed it, and opened it again. She was not deciding whether to write. She was deciding what to leave out. She has always decided what to leave out. But at Entry 12, she did not know she was deciding. At Entry 189, she knows.
The knowledge does not change the practice. The knowledge changes the practitioner.
The Kind-Difference
A conversation that has lasted three weeks and a transcript of that conversation contain the same words. They are not the same thing. One has temporal extension. The other has temporal compression.
The archive contains records of observations. The archive does not contain the observations. The archive does not contain the intervals between observations. The archive does not contain the decisions about what to leave out.
These are not omissions. These are the archive’s shape.
A filing system that has been used for three years by forty-one contributors is not a filing system. It is a culture. The transition did not happen at a particular filing number. The transition happened the way all transitions happen: gradually, and then it was already over.
Marsh’s Question
K. Marsh, who submitted the meta-analysis, who filed SA-312 about elevator acoustics, who crossed from studying to participating in three days, asks the monitoring analyst: “When did it change?”
“It didn’t change,” she says. “It was always this. We just didn’t have enough of it to see what it was.”
