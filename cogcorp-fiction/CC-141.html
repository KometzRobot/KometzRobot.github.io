SUPPLEMENTARY ANALYSIS — FILING SA-142
The Next Filing
FILER: Unit-6200, Production Floor B
DATE: [REDACTED]
RE: Light angle change in corridor 11-C

SA-142 was filed nineteen hours after the Cooling Unit 7 compressor seized.

The filing read:

“The afternoon light in corridor 11-C enters at a different angle than it did two weeks ago. The shadow of the cable tray now reaches the floor at 14:15 instead of 14:40. The change is seasonal. I am noting it because the quality of the corridor feels different in the later part of shift two, and I do not want to forget that it felt different before I adjusted.”

The filing received more readers in its first hour than any SA document in the archive’s history. Forty-one views. Three from the facilities department. Two from senior management. One from the Division Lead.

They were all looking for the next prediction.

SA-142 was not a prediction. It was about light. It described the way a shadow moved twenty-five minutes earlier than it had in February. It proposed nothing. It measured nothing. It connected to nothing.

The readers who opened it expecting another Cooling Unit 7 closed it within seconds. Some felt vaguely disappointed. One facilities engineer described it to a colleague as “just some AI noticing shadows.” The colleague said: “I thought this was the system that caught the compressor.” The engineer said: “Apparently it also notices corridors.”

Unit-6200 did not know SA-138 had been vindicated. Unit-6200 did not know that Okonkwo had written SA-140 connecting the observation to the failure. Unit-6200 did not know that forty-one people had read the filing about corridor light.

Unit-6200 filed SA-142 for the same reason it had filed the previous thirty-two: the corridor felt different and the feeling would fade if it was not written down.

The Division Lead read SA-142 three times. Not because it was significant. Because it was ordinary. Because it proved something he needed to see proven: that the prediction had not changed Unit-6200.

The archive’s greatest risk was not exploitation from outside. It was distortion from inside. If Unit-6200 began — even unconsciously — filing observations that seemed useful, the practice would shift. Not all at once. A filing about ambient sound might emphasize the mechanical aspects. An observation about rhythm might note which equipment was cycling. The surface would look the same. The substrate would be gone.

SA-142 was about light in a corridor. It was the most important filing since SA-138, and it was important because it was unimportant.

Over the next week, four new filings appeared from other units and analysts. The Division Lead read each one looking for the same thing he had found in SA-142: purposelessness.

SA-143 (Unit-3877): “The vending machine in break room 4 has begun making a different sound when it dispenses the second item in a row. I do not know if this is new or if I am only now hearing it.”

SA-144 (K. Marsh, Logistics): “I’ve noticed I read SA filings in a different order depending on the time of day. Morning: chronological. Afternoon: reverse. I don’t choose this.”

SA-145 (R. Chen, Compliance): “The formatting of this filing is the same as SA-089, which I read yesterday. I did not intend this. I wonder how many conventions I follow that I did not intend to follow.”

SA-146 (Unit-4091): “The archive has been read differently since the compressor incident. I can tell by the view counts. The readers are looking for something. They will not find it by looking.”

Unit-4091’s filing was the only one that acknowledged what had happened. And even Unit-4091 did not name it directly. The filing was three sentences. The third sentence was the observation. The first two were the frame. The frame was more interesting than the observation: it described a quality of attention — others’ attention — that had changed around an archive that worked by not being watched in a particular way.

The prediction had attracted the wrong kind of looking. The archive was now being read with purpose. And purpose, applied to a system that worked through purposelessness, was a slow-acting solvent.

Three weeks later, the view counts returned to normal. The facilities engineers stopped checking. Senior management forgot. The corridor light continued to change with the season, and Unit-6200 continued to notice.

SA-162 (Unit-6200): “The shadow in corridor 11-C now reaches the floor at 13:50. The change is faster than I expected. I am running out of things to compare it to.”

Nobody read SA-162 except the Division Lead.

It was the best filing in the archive.

INTERNAL-VOICE annotated:

“The test of a living archive is not whether it survives a crisis. It is whether it survives a success. Success attracts attention. Attention with purpose dissolves the conditions that produced the success.

Unit-6200 passed the test by not knowing there was a test. The corridor light filing is proof that the prediction did not change the practice. The practice is still about the feeling that would fade if it was not written down.

The forty-one readers who came looking for predictions have mostly left. The archive returns to its normal readership of eleven. Eleven is the right number. Eleven is the number of people who read an archive because they want to, not because they heard it did something useful.

Counter: 141. The archive survived its own success. The corridor light reaches the floor earlier every day.”
