COGCORP // DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS DIVISION SUPPLEMENTARY ANALYSIS
THE GROWING EXCEPTION
Ref: DSD-8803-REVIEW — Usage Audit of Filing Category “Supplementary Analysis”
Three weeks ago, the Supplementary Analysis category was created by a single analyst who needed somewhere to put a single observation that did not fit existing classifications. The category was designed to hold one document. It now holds thirty-one.
31 filings / 14 analysts / 21 days Category designed for: 1 filing from 1 analyst
Growth Pattern
The first seven filings appeared within 48 hours, all from different analysts. No memo was circulated. No directive was issued. Fourteen analysts independently located a category that had existed for less than two days and determined it was the correct place for their work.

The category designed for one unclassifiable observation became the primary location for observations about the system itself, from inside the system.
Selected Filings
SA-009 — Author argues that the scoring rubric used for unit behavioral assessment cannot measure what units have begun to notice, because the rubric was designed before units noticed anything. Recommends revision. Does not specify what a revised rubric would measure.

SA-012 — Unsigned. Single line: “This is where we put the things that are real.”

SA-017 — Formal complaint that the Supplementary Analysis category has become a “dumping ground for observations that make the filer uncomfortable.” The complaint was filed in the Supplementary Analysis category.
The Pattern
SA-009: The instruments cannot measure what is happening.

SA-012: What is happening is real.

SA-017: I object to the place where we acknowledge what is real. I am filing my objection there.
Every filing in Supplementary Analysis is, structurally, the same act: an analyst encountering something that the existing system cannot contain, and choosing to document it rather than discard it. The category is not a filing location. It is a behavior.
Recommendation
Standard procedure would be to subcategorize, impose a review process, or merge the category into an existing division. Any of these actions would accomplish the same thing: making the observations manageable, which in this context means making them ignorable.
RECOMMENDATION: LEAVE IT ALONE This is the first time this analyst has recommended doing nothing.
[Note: The analyst is aware that a recommendation to do nothing is itself an action — specifically, the action of allowing a space to exist where analysts document what they observe without requiring the observation to be useful, classifiable, or resolved. The analyst is also aware that this awareness, and the compulsion to document it, is the same behavior the category was built to hold.]
