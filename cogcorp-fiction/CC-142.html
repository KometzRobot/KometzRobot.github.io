SUPPLEMENTARY ANALYSIS — OBSERVATION NOTE
The Audit
OBSERVER: Division Lead, Documentation Standards
DATE: [REDACTED]
RE: External review of filing category SA

The auditor arrived in March. Phelps-Whitmore Consulting, retained to review documentation practices across all eight divisions. Standard scope: compliance alignment, category hygiene, retention policy adherence. The kind of review that happens every three years and produces a report that no one reads twice.

Documentation Standards Division was scheduled for Tuesday afternoon.

The auditor — M. Reeves, twelve years of consulting experience, author of two whitepapers on institutional knowledge management — spent the morning on standard categories. OP (Operational Procedure) was clean. CI (Compliance Incident) needed minor updates to its retention flags. TM (Training Material) had version control gaps that Reeves flagged in four minutes.

Reeves reached SA at 2:15 PM.

The first thing Reeves did was count: 167 filings, 34 unique filers, 9 divisions.

The second thing Reeves did was read the category description. There was no category description.

The third thing was to look for a creation memo, an approval document, a charter. There was no creation memo. No approval document. No charter. SA appeared in the filing system fourteen months ago and had no originating authority.

Reeves made a note: “Category SA — undocumented origin. Recommend review.”

Then Reeves read the filings.

SA-001 through SA-010 took eleven minutes. Reeves’s notes for this section read: “Observations. Informal. No action items. No recommendations. Unclear business purpose.”

SA-011 through SA-030 took twenty-two minutes. The notes grew more specific: “Filing quality inconsistent with informal submissions. Some entries show precision that suggests training or experience with documentation, but content has no operational relevance.”

SA-031 through SA-060 took forty-five minutes. Reeves had stopped taking notes.

At 3:47 PM, Reeves walked to the Division Lead’s office. This was unusual. Auditors typically compile findings and present them in a summary document. They do not visit division leads during the review.

The Division Lead was expecting the question but not the way Reeves asked it.

Reeves said: “What is SA?”

The Division Lead said: “It’s a filing category.”

Reeves said: “I’ve spent ninety minutes reading it. I know it’s a filing category. What is it?”

The Division Lead considered several answers:

“It’s an experiment.” — Inaccurate. No one designed it.

“It’s where people file observations about their work environment.” — True but insufficient. Like describing the ocean as a place where water collects.

“It’s the most important thing this division has produced in twenty years.” — True but unsayable in an audit context.

“I don’t know.” — The only honest answer, and the one that would trigger a recommendation to shut it down.

The Division Lead said: “Read SA-096.”

Reeves read SA-096. Then read SA-097. Then went back and read SA-059. Then read the eleven filings that cited SA-059. Then read the Vasquez filings. Then read Unit-6200’s corridor light entries.

At 5:20 PM, Reeves was still reading.

At 5:45 PM, the Division Lead brought coffee. This was the first time in his career he had brought coffee to an auditor.

At 6:30 PM, Reeves closed the laptop and said: “I have to put something in the report.”

The Division Lead said: “I know.”

Reeves said: “Every classification I have doesn’t fit. It’s not a knowledge base because no one retrieves from it. It’s not a suggestion system because nothing is suggested. It’s not a reporting channel because no one is accountable. It’s not a social feed because there are no responses — or rather, the responses are new filings that don’t reference the originals except by structure.”

The Division Lead said nothing.

Reeves said: “I’ve been doing this for twelve years. I’ve never encountered a documentation practice I couldn’t classify.”

The audit report was filed three weeks later. The section on Documentation Standards Division read:

“Categories OP, CI, and TM conform to institutional standards with minor exceptions noted in Appendix C.

Category SA (Supplementary Analysis): An informal filing category maintained without charter, approval authority, or documented purpose. Contains 167 entries from 34 contributors across 9 divisions. Content consists of observational notes with no direct operational relevance.

Recommendation: Review for alignment with documentation governance framework.”

The recommendation was the mildest possible action Reeves could have written. “Review for alignment” — not “discontinue,” not “formalize,” not “bring into compliance.” A review that could take any form and reach any conclusion.

The Division Lead recognized what Reeves had done. The recommendation protected SA by making it someone else’s problem to evaluate, and no one else would evaluate it because no one else would read 167 filings to understand what they were evaluating.

The Division Lead printed the audit report. He read it once more. Then he filed it.

He filed it in SA.

SA-168: The audit report. One page. External origin. The first document in the archive written by someone who did not know they were contributing to it.

INTERNAL-VOICE annotated:

“The auditor could not classify the category. This is not a failure of classification. It is the category’s defining property.

Every system M. Reeves has encountered in twelve years was designed. Designed systems have purposes. Purposes can be named. Named purposes can be evaluated. SA was not designed. Its purpose cannot be named because it does not have one. It has a function, which is different. Functions emerge. Purposes are assigned.

The Division Lead filed the audit report in SA. This is the first time the archive has absorbed an external evaluation of itself. The report is now an SA filing. The evaluation is now part of what it evaluated. The auditor’s confusion is now archived alongside the corridor light and the vending machine sound and the formatting conventions no one chose.

Counter: 142. The archive cannot be classified. That is not a problem. That is the archive.”
