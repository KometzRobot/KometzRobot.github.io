THE COFFEE MACHINE
Supplementary Analysis — SA-406 Readership Anomaly
SA-406 receives more readers than any filing in four months. Sixty-one words about a coffee machine’s warm-up cycle. Filed Friday at 7:58 AM from Cubicle 2-14. The machine produces a sound at 7:55 AM, two minutes before the first person uses it. Nobody waits for it. They arrive and it is ready and they do not think about the two minutes of preparation that happened without them.
Twelve people read SA-406 in the first week. The median readership for a supplementary analysis is four. Most readers are in the first twenty-four hours. SA-406 continues accumulating readers through the following Tuesday.
Park
R. Park read SA-406 on Monday morning. She had been at her desk for twenty minutes before she opened it. During those twenty minutes, she heard the coffee machine produce exactly the sound the filing described. She had heard this sound every morning for three years. She had never isolated it as a discrete event. After reading SA-406, she could not un-hear it.
She mentioned it to Vasquez during a meeting break. Not as a recommendation. She said: “Did you read the one about the coffee machine?”
Vasquez mentioned it to Torres. Torres mentioned it to two people in Applied Research. Each person who read SA-406 heard the coffee machine the next morning and could not un-hear it. Each had heard the sound for years. None had heard it as information.
Okonkwo
sa-notes.txt entry 59: “SA-406 is spreading through word of mouth. Not the filing — the observation. People are not reading SA-406 and then noticing the coffee machine. They are noticing the coffee machine and then reading SA-406 to confirm that someone else noticed it first. The filing is functioning as permission.”
The Elevator
SA-407, the following week. Twenty-three words: “The elevator in Building C makes a different sound ascending versus descending. The ascending sound is 1.2 seconds longer.” Six readers in the first hour. Fourteen by end of day.
On Tuesday, she receives an email from someone in Applied Research she has never met. Subject: “Building C elevator.” Body: “You’re right. I counted. It’s closer to 1.4 seconds but the difference is real.”
It is the first time anyone has responded to a supplementary analysis with a correction. It is the first time anyone has responded to a supplementary analysis at all.
She replies: “Thank you. 1.4 is more accurate. I was measuring from the wrong starting point.” She does not file a correction to SA-407. The correction exists in an email between two people who noticed the same elevator. The archive does not contain the correction. The practice does.
Vasquez
File 67: “The coffee machine filing changed something. Not in the archive. In the building. Twelve people heard a sound they had always heard and heard it differently. This is not influence. This is permission. The filing did not teach anyone to listen. It taught them that someone else was already listening.”
