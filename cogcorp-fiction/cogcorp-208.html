CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis
SA #327, Third Paragraph
“The monitoring analyst has filed three supplementary analyses. In each of my filings about her work, I have called her ‘the monitoring analyst.’ This is her job title. It is how she appears in the employee directory. It is not her name. The archive contains her job title because I have not used her name. The archive does not require names. The archive requires filing numbers.”
Entry 156
“He noticed. He has been calling me ‘the monitoring analyst’ in his filings. He is right — this is my job title, not my name. But I have been calling him ‘Delgado’ in my notebook, which is his surname, not his first name. Neither of us has used a first name. The archive runs on filing numbers and job titles. The notebook runs on surnames and descriptions. Names occupy a different register than either of these.”
What the Archive Does Not Contain
The archive does not contain first names. The archive contains filing numbers, job titles, dates, and measurements. SA #286 was filed by “the paragraph-writer” — a description Delgado coined in his third paragraphs, not a job title, not a name. It spread through the archive like a measurement: without attribution, without origin, as though it had always been the correct term.
The monitoring analyst’s name does not appear in the archive. Delgado’s first name does not appear in the archive. The paragraph-writer’s name does not appear in the archive. Reeves is a surname. The archive is an institution of functions, not identities.
Entry 157
“My name is not in the archive. My measurements are. His name is not in the archive. His readings are. We have given the archive our attention and withheld our names. This was not a decision. This is how archives work. They take what you file and do not ask who you are.”
