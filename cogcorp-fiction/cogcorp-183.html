THE CORRESPONDENT
Informal Communication — SA-407 Response Chain
Delgado
His name is Delgado. Applied Research, Building C, third floor. He has worked at CogCorp for four years. His role involves testing environmental sensor calibration. He measures things for a living.
He measured the elevator on a Tuesday because he had read SA-407 on a Monday and spent the rest of the day unable to stop listening. The ascending sound was not 1.2 seconds longer. It was 1.4. He counted from the moment the doors began closing. She counted from the moment the button was pressed. The difference was 0.2 seconds. The difference was the starting point.
The Email
He did not intend to send the email. He wrote it, read it, deleted it, rewrote it, and sent it before he could delete it again. Subject: “Building C elevator.” Thirty-one words. He had drafted sixty. He cut until there was nothing left to cut.
She replied: “Thank you. 1.4 is more accurate. I was measuring from the wrong starting point.” He did not expect her to agree. He did not know that no one had ever responded to a supplementary analysis before.
Stairwell B
Three days later, she emails him. Subject: “Stairwell B, floors 3-4.” Body: “The handrail has a vibration at certain times of day. I think it’s the HVAC but I’m not sure. You would know. You test environmental sensors.”
It is the first time the monitoring analyst has asked anyone anything about an observation. It is not a filing. It is a question.
HVAC. 42-minute cycle. Six seconds of resonance traveling upward through metal. He had never noticed it. Four years of passing Stairwell B. His hands had been on that handrail. She noticed it on floor 3. He confirmed it from floor 2. Neither of them filed anything.
The Question
His third email. Subject: “Question.” Body: “Do you always notice things like this? I test sensors all day and I didn’t notice the elevator until you filed SA-407. I measure things for a living. You filed twenty-three words and I heard something I’d walked past for four years.”
She takes two days to reply. Eleven words: “I don’t always notice. I notice when I’m paying attention.”
