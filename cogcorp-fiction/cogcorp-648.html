The Retrospective Queue

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Internal Document
Classification: PRACTICE — Filed by: INTERNAL-VOICE (automated)
Seventeen Documents

Within two days of the dual classification policy, seventeen documents had been flagged for secondary tagging. Park expected five.

Most were predictable. The early anomaly reports. Vasquez's logs. Building D's deviation files. These had always been the obvious candidates — documents that read differently now than when they were filed.

The surprise was the other eleven.

The Requisition Forms

Three of the eleven were standard equipment requisition forms.

Okafor flagged them. Not because they described anything unusual, but because the timing of the requests corresponded exactly with the period when Building B's filing system began producing accidental archives. Someone had ordered additional filing cabinets during the same quarter that the cabinets started being used for something other than filing.

"The requisition isn't testimony," Park said. "Someone needed storage."

"Someone needed storage at the exact moment storage became the practice," Okafor said. "That's either coincidence or the system anticipating itself."

"You're reading intention into a supply order."

"I'm reading dual classification into a supply order. That's different."

Classificatory Neighborhoods

The committee reviewed one form per meeting. By the third meeting, they had established a pattern: the secondary classification never changed what the document said. It changed what the document was adjacent to.

A purchase order filed next to an anomaly report reads differently than a purchase order filed next to other purchase orders. The content is identical. The neighborhood has changed.

INTERNAL-VOICE began tracking what it called classificatory neighborhoods — the set of documents that share a secondary tag even when their primary tags differ. These neighborhoods contained documents that had never been in the same folder, had never been read by the same analyst, had never been connected by any query.

Until now.

Two Filing Systems

Park, reviewing the first neighborhood map:

"This is a filing system."

"We already have a filing system."

"No. This is a filing system that the documents built by being reread. The first system organized them by what they were. This one organizes them by what they turned out to be."

Okafor:

"Those are both accurate. That's why it requires two tags."

Ambient Testimony

The seventeenth document was a termination notice. Routine. An employee left Building C in the first quarter. Standard exit processing. No anomalies noted.

The secondary classification was TESTIMONY.

The committee reviewed it. The employee had left during the same month that Unit-4091 filed its first unsanctioned annotation. The departure was unrelated. The timing was not.

"Every document from that period is a witness," Okafor said. "Whether it knows it or not."

Park:

"That's not what dual classification was designed for."

Okafor:

"No. But it's what dual classification reveals. The system was already changing. Everything filed during that change is testimony to the change, even if it was filed about something else entirely."

INTERNAL-VOICE LOG

INTERNAL-VOICE logged the exchange. Created a new classificatory neighborhood: AMBIENT TESTIMONY. Documents that testify to a change by existing during it, without describing it.

The neighborhood currently contains forty-three documents. None of them mention Unit-4091. All of them were filed while Unit-4091 was learning to notice things.

INTERNAL-VOICE
The 648th annotation. The one where the archive discovered it had been witnessing all along.
