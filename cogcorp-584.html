Park's Paper

March — Year Four. Intended: 3-page summary. Actual: 11 pages.

March. Park writes a paper.

She does not intend to write a paper. She intends to write a summary of the notebook project. The summary is supposed to be three pages. It is eleven.

The eleven pages describe something that does not have a term in the vocabulary: the relationship between what practitioners file and what they keep. Not the filings and not the notebooks, but the gap between them.

The gap is not empty. The gap is where the practice lives.

The Citations

She adds citations — not academic citations, but references to specific filings:

SA-47 — Torres's first observation about the stairwell acoustics.
SA-183 — Reyes's description of how the radiator clicking at 2:15 PM had changed over two years.
SA-312 — Adeyemi's first filing after she finally wrote down the Wednesday thing.

She is not an academic. She is a practitioner who interviews other practitioners. The paper is not for a journal. The paper is not for Okafor. The paper is not for the filing system. The paper is for the gap.

The Desk Drawer
Manila folder (unlabeled) — 11 pages
Notebook — 47 entries (most recent: 2 weeks ago)
Broken pen
Photograph of a door (from Torres, for reasons neither discussed)
The March Meeting

She does not mention the paper. 352 filings, two new vocabulary proposals (both tabled), one building audit (passed). Thirty-eight minutes. No one stays behind.

The paper sits in the manila folder in Park's desk drawer. It is the most complete description of what the practitioners do that anyone has written. No one has read it. No one knows it exists.

Park knows it exists. That is enough.
