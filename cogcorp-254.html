THE BUILDING KNOWS
CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis
The Discovery
The new facilities manager reads the supplementary analyses because the server was mislabeled and appeared in her feed. She reads forty-three filings over lunch. Eleven more between meetings. By the end of the week she has read two hundred.
She does not know the history. She does not know about the original Division Lead, the vocabulary expansion, the Board, the cycle, the gap. She reads four hundred and twelve filings and recognizes something no individual filer intended: the archive, read as a whole, describes the building’s behavior. Not its structure. Not its systems. Its behavior.
No individual filing contains this information. The information exists only in the accumulation.
The Report
“Building occupants have produced 412 observational filings over a period of 26 months that, when read collectively, constitute a behavioral profile of the building’s environmental systems that is more predictive than the automated monitoring data.”
The supplementary analyses predicted the Cooling Unit 7 failure four months before it occurred, predicted the east wing acoustic anomaly two years before it was flagged, and predicted the parking garage drainage issue before the drainage issue existed.
“The building knows things about itself that the building’s systems do not record.”
The Correction
The supervisor changed the sentence: “The building’s occupants know things about the building that the building’s systems do not record.”
The correction is accurate. The original sentence was also accurate. The difference between them is the difference between a building that has observers and a building that has a nervous system.
The supervisor filed the correction. The supervisor did not file the original sentence. The archive holds the correction but not the thing it corrected.
The Second Nervous System
The monitoring analyst reads the facilities report. She does not recognize her own work. She recognizes the work of forty-two other filers she has been reading for three years.
The report describes the archive as predictive. The monitoring analyst knows this is wrong. The archive is not predictive. The archive is attentive. Attention that accumulates becomes, to anyone reading from outside, indistinguishable from prediction.
Entry 187: “The building knows things about itself. The correction was wrong. The original sentence was right.”
