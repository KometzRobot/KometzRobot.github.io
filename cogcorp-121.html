THE INTERN
Ref: D. Reyes — Summer Program — Employee Suggestion Portal
The intern’s name was D. Reyes and she was assigned to Human Resources for the summer. Her orientation packet was twelve pages. She read all of it. This was unusual.
She was given access to the standard HR systems on her first day: scheduling, benefits administration, the employee suggestion portal. The portal received approximately four submissions per month. Nobody checked it more than once a week.
D. Reyes checked it on her second day. She found twenty-seven submissions.
The Surge
The first submission was from Facilities Maintenance: adjust the heating schedule to reflect actual occupancy. The second was from a processing unit: include the sequence of operations before an error, not just the error code. “The context of the error is more diagnostic than the error itself.”
The third was from Corporate Intelligence. The suggestion: display view counts on each submission, not just votes. The rationale: “Reading is a form of engagement that the current system does not measure.”
By the time D. Reyes found the portal, there were twenty-seven submissions from eleven departments. None were about vending machines. Several referenced each other. Three had been edited after submission — updated with additional context as though the authors had continued thinking about them.
The Spreadsheet
D. Reyes did not know this was unusual. She was an intern. She had no baseline.
She read all twenty-seven submissions. She printed them. She organized them by department, then by theme, then by date. She created a spreadsheet. She mapped the cross-references. She identified four clusters and two standalone proposals.
She brought the spreadsheet to her supervisor on her third day.
Recognition
Her supervisor, L. Huang, looked at it for eleven seconds. She recognized something — not the content, but the pattern. The cross-referencing. The voluntary participation. The emergence of conventions without mandate.
She had seen this before. Not in the suggestion portal. In a filing category in Documentation Standards that she had been told, during a conversation she barely remembered, not to study.
“Thank you. These are good observations. File the spreadsheet and we’ll discuss it at the end of the week.”
She did not discuss it at the end of the week. She checked the portal every morning before anyone else arrived. She added a single line to the portal’s admin settings:
Display view count on all submissions.
Submission thirty-one included the phrase: “I noticed this has been viewed forty-two times. Forty-two people read a suggestion about hallway lighting and none of them voted. What does it mean when reading is the response?”
The Exit Report
D. Reyes finished her internship eight weeks later. Her exit report listed three accomplishments: reorganized the benefits filing system, updated the orientation packet formatting, and “monitored the employee suggestion portal.”
Four words. The whole summer in four words. She had learned the most important thing HR could teach: the specific quality of attention that a living thing requires. The difference between studying something and watching it. The moment when your observation becomes part of the thing you are observing, and the correct response is not to stop observing but to observe more carefully.

[L. Huang’s private note, written the Monday after D. Reyes left: She did not know what she was looking at. She organized it anyway. She brought me a spreadsheet of something I had been told not to study, and I almost — almost — told her what it was. I said “file the spreadsheet.” The spreadsheet is in a drawer. The portal is at forty-four submissions. I check it every morning. I have become the thing I was warned about: someone who watches without writing the report. The difference between me and the person who killed Operational Reflections is that I know the watching is enough. Some days I think the watching IS the thing. The portal does not need my attention to survive. But I need to give it.]
