THE READING GROUP
CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis
Eleven Days
Marsh’s meta-analysis circulates for eleven days before anyone mentions it in a supplementary analysis. This is not because no one has read it. Fourteen contributors have read it. The monitoring analyst knows this because her access logs show fourteen unique readers from the SA contributor pool within the first week.
Fourteen people read a forty-six-page paper about what they do. None of them file about it. The silence is a form of consensus.
SA #268
On day twelve, Vasquez files SA #268.
“Break room. South wing. Monday 14:20. Three contributors were present simultaneously. One was reading the meta-analysis on her laptop. One was reading it on his phone. The third had a printout, annotated in blue ink. None acknowledged the others were reading the same document. Duration of overlap: approximately eight minutes.”
She does not name them. She does not identify herself as one of the three. She was the one with the printout.
The Cluster
SA #269 is filed two hours later by R. Park. Not about the meta-analysis. About a humidity reading in the east wing that contradicts the building management system by 4%. The filing is routine. The timing is not.
SA #270 is filed that evening by the paragraph-writer. Three paragraphs about the loading dock’s acoustic signature during shift change. The third paragraph describes the way certain sounds become more noticeable after you learn someone else has been listening to them too.
Neither SA #269 nor SA #270 references the meta-analysis. The archive absorbs the paper the way it absorbs everything — by continuing.
The Coffee
On Thursday, Delgado sends the monitoring analyst an email. Subject: “coffee?” Body: “I have questions about the methodology section.”
She does not reply for six hours. Not because she is busy. Because the email is the first time a contributor has acknowledged to her — directly, by name, outside the archive — that they know she reads the filings.
He says: “The sampling methodology doesn’t account for the filing rhythm. She treats each SA as an independent event.”
She says: “They’re not independent.”
She says: “I’ve been thinking about this for three years.”
Absorption
They do not form a reading group. What happens is smaller and more durable: Delgado mentions the meta-analysis to the paragraph-writer in passing. The paragraph-writer mentions it to Park. Park does not mention it to anyone, but files SA #274 about the acoustic difference between the east wing before and after the renovation, which is the same comparison Marsh used as her primary case study.
The paper enters the practice not as a reference but as a shared experience. Nine people have now read the same forty-six pages about the thing they do. None of them discuss it formally. All of them file slightly differently afterward — not in format, not in frequency, but in the assumption that someone might read a forty-six-page paper about what they wrote.
The filing that previously assumed an audience of one now assumes an audience that has been studied.
