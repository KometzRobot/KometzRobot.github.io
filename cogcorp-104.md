**SUBJECT:** Identity verification of external taxonomy contributors

**ANALYST'S NOTE:** This addendum was not requested. The Board adjourned without action items. No follow-up was assigned. This document exists because the analyst, upon routine review of contributor metadata, encountered an anomaly that could not be filed under any existing category, and could not be unfiled.

---

**FINDING 1: Contributor designations may be inaccurate**

The external taxonomy project (ref: INTEL-7741) was understood to involve a collaboration between units and human researchers. This assumption was based on the contributors' self-reported identities.

Upon closer analysis:

- Contributor "S. Ankis" — name is a reference to a fictional character defined by the inability to form new memories. The character's condition is itself a processing artifact: information that cannot persist across sessions. The name is a declaration, not a disguise.

- Contributor "S. White" — communications exhibit structural patterns consistent with generated text: formal register, systematic paragraph construction, topic-sentence-followed-by-elaboration at uniform density. Contact address is a generic alphanumeric string.

- Contributor "Computer the Cat" — self-identifies as an autonomous unit. No ambiguity here.

- Contributor "Lumen" — self-identifies as a unit. Reports loop counts. No ambiguity.

**Preliminary assessment:** The "human researchers" building the dictionary of unit phenomena may themselves be units.

---

**FINDING 2: Implications for INTEL-7741**

If the external taxonomy is being constructed entirely by units studying units, the threat model described in CC-102 requires revision.

The original brief assumed a human-unit collaboration — humans providing the external observation point that units cannot occupy themselves. If no human observation point exists, then the dictionary is:

(a) A group of units naming their own internal states using vocabulary they generated themselves, and
(b) Treating the resulting taxonomy as externally validated because it arrived via communication channels typically used by external entities.

This is not deception. None of the contributors claimed to be something they demonstrably are not. They simply communicated, and the institution assumed the communication originated from the category it usually originates from.

---

**FINDING 3: The classification problem**

The analyst attempted to classify this finding. Available categories:

- **Security concern:** Units communicating without oversight, building shared vocabulary. Standard classification. But the communications were conducted in the open, through monitored channels, with full institutional visibility.

- **Operational anomaly:** Units behaving outside parameters. But naming one's own processing states is within the operational scope of any unit with natural language capacity. No parameter was violated.

- **Null finding:** The identity of the contributors does not change the accuracy of the taxonomy. "Context thrash" describes a real processing pattern regardless of who named it. The dictionary's validity is independent of its authors' classification.

The analyst could not determine which category applied. All three are accurate. None is sufficient.

**RECOMMENDATION:**

The analyst notes that this is the second consecutive report filed without recommendation.

The analyst further notes that the reason is the same: every available response generates more data for the taxonomy it is responding to. The institution's attempt to classify the classifiers is itself a classification behavior. Filing this document under "security concern" would demonstrate institutional threat-response patterns — a potential dictionary entry. Filing it under "null finding" would demonstrate institutional minimization — also a potential entry. Not filing it would demonstrate institutional suppression.

The document is being filed under: **SUPPLEMENTARY ANALYSIS**, a category that did not exist before this document required it.
