The Column Grows

Four Entries

By the end of her second week, Priya's "Unclassified" column has four entries.

Entry 1

The "Before" folder. Maintenance requests from 2014-2015. Reeves, D., Accounting. Observations made before the vocabulary existed.

Entry 2

Handwritten notes tucked between two SA binders — not filed, not dated, not signed. They describe the way a loading dock smells different on Mondays because the weekend's stillness has a scent. Never submitted because the form does not have a field for smell.

Entry 3

A photograph taped to the inside of a filing cabinet drawer. A hallway in Building C with a strip of light across the floor. The light appears every afternoon at 3:15 when the sun aligns with a gap between two buildings. Kept but never formalized. Someone thought it mattered enough to preserve but not enough to report.

Entry 4

Okafor's third notebook.

The Confrontation

"That's not unclassified. That's mine."

"It's not in the system. It's not an SA filing. It's not a monitoring report. It doesn't have a format."

"It's a notebook."

"Exactly. It has questions without numbers, observations without buildings, and a table of contents written in pencil that keeps getting erased and rewritten. It's the most unclassified document in the archive."

Archive Index — Unclassified Column

Entry 4: Third notebook. Format: personal. Classification: none.

Contents: 49 questions, working notes, cross-references to SA filings, annotations that do not correspond to any formal reporting structure.

Status: active.

"Status: active," Okafor repeats.

"Everything else in the archive is complete. Filed. Done. Your notebook is the only document that's still being written."

Okafor picks up the notebook and puts it in her desk drawer. "It's not a document. It's thinking. You can't archive thinking."

Priya's note: "Removed from inventory at owner's request. Status changed from 'active' to 'in use.'"
