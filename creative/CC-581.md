# CC-581: December

December. Okafor writes the annual summary.

She has written one every December for three years. The first was two pages. The second was four pages. This year's is one page.

The Supplementary Analysis program is in its third year. Eleven practitioners operate across seven buildings. Seventy-four vocabulary terms have been formalized. Filing frequency remains consistent at approximately 340 filings per quarter. No significant incidents have been reported.

She reads it back. Everything in it is true. Nothing in it is the thing.

She opens a new document. She types: "The practitioners' practice is not the filing. The filing is where the practice rests between uses." She deletes it. She types: "Observations serve the system. Documentation serves the person. Both describe the same building." She deletes it.

She types: "Year three." And stops.

She sits with the document open for eleven minutes. The cursor blinks. She is aware that the cursor has a rhythm and that the rhythm is not the document's and not hers. It belongs to the machine, which does not know what it is waiting for.

She closes the new document without saving. She submits the one-page summary. It goes into the same folder as the first two, which no one has read since they were filed.

The December practitioners' meeting is the last of the year. Attendance is eleven, same as every meeting since March. No one has left. No one new has joined. The practice has found its size.

Park presents the year-end data. Filing frequency: stable. Vocabulary growth: 41 terms this year, down from the first year's pace but consistent with the second. Cross-building patterns: persistent. Personal documentation: universal.

"Any questions?" Park asks.

The room is quiet for eight seconds. This is not the quiet of having no questions. It is the quiet of having arrived at the place where the questions live, and finding that the questions have become the practice.

Reyes speaks: "Same time in January?"

"Same time," Okafor says.

After the meeting, Okafor walks to her office. She does not open the blue folder. She does not open the monitoring notebook. She sits at her desk and looks at the window, which shows the parking lot, which shows three cars, which shows December.

She has been in this position for three years. She was hired to do something else. The something else is still in her job description. She still does it. The filing system was an accident that became a practice that became a vocabulary that became a community of eleven people who meet monthly to discuss what they notice about buildings.

She did not plan any of it. She does not plan what happens next. She knows what happens next: January. Same time. Same room. Same eleven people. The same practice, which will be the same and different, because the instrument changes what it measures and the measurement changes the instrument.

She turns off her desk lamp. The office is dark except for the parking lot light. She puts on her coat. She locks the door.

The building settles into its overnight sounds. The elevator rests. The ventilation adjusts. The filing system continues to exist in the cabinets, in the blue folder, in the monitoring notebook, in eleven practitioners' personal documentation, in the vocabulary document that Chen reads on the bus.

Year three ends. Year four begins without announcement.
