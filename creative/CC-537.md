# CC-537: Classification

Okafor spreads the four maintenance requests on her desk. Three from Reeves, one from the original find — #4471. She has read each one six times. She is trying to do something she has never done before: apply the vocabulary backward.

SA-834 described acoustic expectation. She looks at Reeves's stairwell observation: "The stairwell between floors 2 and 3 sounds different than the stairwell between floors 3 and 4. I cannot identify the source. People walk quieter on the landing. They do not know they are walking quieter." If this had been filed as a supplementary analysis, it would be SA-classified. The vocabulary fits. "Acoustic expectation" covers the behavioral change. "Implicit navigation" covers the quieter walking. Two of the fifty-four terms apply cleanly.

But Reeves did not have the vocabulary. Reeves called it "sounds different." And Okafor cannot decide whether applying the terms retroactively is accurate or revisionist.

She opens her third notebook.

Question 48: "If the vocabulary fits an observation made before the vocabulary existed, does that validate the vocabulary or distort the observation?"

She writes it and stares at it. It is the kind of question that splits into two questions as soon as you try to answer it. The vocabulary was built from observations like Reeves's — not from theory, not from academic literature, from practitioners noticing things and finding words for them. So the vocabulary should fit. It was designed to fit exactly this kind of observation.

But Reeves's observation includes something the vocabulary does not capture: the experience of noticing without a framework. "I cannot identify the source." That uncertainty is part of the observation. If Okafor classifies it as "acoustic expectation," the uncertainty disappears. The classification makes it legible. The legibility erases the not-knowing that was the actual experience.

She picks up the parking structure request. The night guard's "the building waking up." Thermal expansion. The vocabulary would call it "temporal signature" — the building's daily rhythm made perceptible. Accurate. But the night guard's phrase captures something "temporal signature" does not: the sense that the building is a living thing with sleep and waking. That is not in the vocabulary because the vocabulary was designed to be precise, and "living thing" is not precise.

Question 49: "What do the pre-vocabulary observations contain that the vocabulary was designed to exclude?"

Okafor closes the notebook. She does not classify the maintenance requests. She puts them back in Kim's blue folder and places the folder on the shelf next to the SA binders — not inside them.

The folder sits between the archive and the practice. It belongs to neither. It belongs to before.
