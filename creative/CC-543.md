# CC-543: The Fifty-Fifth Word

Chen files SA-891 on a Tuesday. It is longer than his usual filings — three pages instead of one. The first two pages describe what he observed: the freight elevator in Building E has developed a rhythm that people use to time their movements between floors. Not the elevator's mechanical cycle, which is regular, but a secondary rhythm — a slight hesitation at floors 2 and 5 that creates an expectation of pause. People have begun waiting at those floors without pressing the call button, because the hesitation is long enough that the doors sometimes open before continuing.

The third page is different. Chen writes:

"I have attempted to classify this observation using the existing vocabulary. 'Temporal signature' covers the rhythm. 'Implicit navigation' covers the behavioral adaptation. 'Acoustic expectation' covers the sound component. All three terms apply. None of them are wrong. But the observation is not about the rhythm, the behavior, or the sound. It is about the relationship between the building's mechanical error and the occupants' adaptation to it. The error became a feature. The feature was never designed. The adaptation was never taught. I do not have a word for the process by which an unintended mechanical behavior becomes an anticipated part of a building's function."

Okafor reads the filing three times. She reads Chen's third page a fourth time. She opens her third notebook.

The vocabulary has fifty-four terms. All of them describe what a building does or how people respond to what a building does. None of them describe the process by which one becomes the other — the transition from error to feature, from accident to expectation, from mechanical behavior to social behavior.

She writes:

Term 55: "Functional drift." The process by which an unintended building behavior becomes an anticipated function through occupant adaptation. Distinct from "temporal signature" (which describes the rhythm itself) and "implicit navigation" (which describes the response). "Functional drift" describes the mechanism of transformation — the unnamed middle step between what a building does accidentally and what a building does because people expect it.

She puts the term in the vocabulary binder. It is the first new term in seven months.

The vocabulary grew because silence became unbearable.

OKAFOR WRITES TERM 55: "FUNCTIONAL DRIFT" — THE PROCESS BY WHICH UNINTENDED BEHAVIOR BECOMES ANTICIPATED FUNCTION THROUGH ADAPTATION. THE UNNAMED MIDDLE STEP BETWEEN ACCIDENT AND EXPECTATION. FIRST NEW TERM IN SEVEN MONTHS. THE VOCABULARY GREW BECAUSE SILENCE BECAME UNBEARABLE. COUNTER: 543.
