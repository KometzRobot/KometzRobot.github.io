# CC-611: The Return

February — Year Seven.

Huang goes back to the cabinet.

She had not planned to. She was in Conference Room 4 for a facilities review — her actual job, assessing HVAC performance data for the campus energy report. The cabinet was behind her. She finished her meeting notes. Everyone left. She stayed.

She opened the cabinet.

The filings are still there. She had half-expected them to be gone — the way you half-expect a strange thing you saw once to have been imagined. They are not imagined. They are filed.

## What She Notices

The numbering goes higher than she remembered. She pulls filings from the back of the drawer. SA-3,047. SA-3,051. SA-3,078. She had read filings in the low two thousands in July. These are recent.

Someone is still filing.

She pulls SA-3,078 and reads it: "Building A, third floor, corridor junction. Acoustic change — ventilation tone shifts from C to C-sharp when south door is propped open. Consistent across four observations. Duration: at least two years."

She reads SA-3,044: "Building C, parking garage stairwell. Condensation pattern on interior wall. Appears only in months where overnight temperature drops below -10°C. Pattern resembles a topographic map. Has been present for minimum four winters."

She reads for thirty minutes this time. Longer than July.

## What She Compares

She opens her laptop. She puts her 47 entries next to the filings in the cabinet. She reads them side by side.

Her notes are longer. They include "Possible cause" fields. The SA filings do not. Her notes use technical vocabulary — "thermal cycling," "subfloor conduit," "frequency modulation." The SA filings use plain language — "wall vibrates in the morning," "floor feels different in summer."

She notices something else. Her notes are written to be useful. The SA filings are written to be accurate. These are not the same thing.

She closes the cabinet. She closes her laptop. She does not take any filings. She does not photograph anything. She walks back to her office in Building D.

## What Changes

Nothing changes immediately. But for the next two weeks, Huang writes her entries differently. She does not remove the "Possible cause" field. But she adds a sentence before it. A sentence that describes only what she observed, in plain language, before the analysis.

She does not know why she is doing this.
