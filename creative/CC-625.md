# CC-625: The Day After the Stairwell

February — Year Eight.

Huang takes the stairs in Building A again on Wednesday. The landing is empty. The shadow crosses at 3:47. She watches it alone.

On Thursday she takes the stairs again. Empty again. She sits on the same step. She does not know why she expected the landing to be different. The light has not changed. The building has not changed. She has changed.

Forty-seven people. Eleven buildings.

She files SA-974. Unsigned.

"The landing on the third floor of Building A receives direct afternoon light for approximately nine minutes beginning at 3:41 PM. The shadow bisection occurs at 3:47. The phenomenon is unremarkable. The attention it requires is not."

## What She Does Not Write

She does not write about the woman on the landing. She does not write about the number forty-seven. She does not write about the word "supposed" and the sentence that followed it: *You aren't supposed to do anything.*

These are not observations about the building. They are observations about the observer. And Huang does not yet have a filing category for those.

## What Changes in the Filing

SA-974 is different from her previous three. The first three described measurements — radiator timing, elevator pause, shadow crossing. SA-974 describes the attention itself. "The phenomenon is unremarkable. The attention it requires is not."

The monitoring analyst reads it twice. She has read seven years of filings. She has watched practices grow, merge, diversify, go quiet. She recognizes what this sentence is. It is the moment an observer discovers that the interesting thing is not what she's looking at but the fact that she's looking.

She writes in her log: "SA-974. Unsigned. Inflection point."

## What Okafor Does

Okafor reads SA-974 in the cabinet on Friday afternoon. She reads it standing up, in less than a minute. She puts it back.

She walks to the meeting room where the practitioners gather on the first Wednesday of each month. It is empty — the meeting is three weeks away. She sits in Park's chair. She sits there for four minutes. She is not waiting. She is imagining the conversation in which she tells them about Huang.

She decides not to. Not because the information should be withheld. Because the practice does not require introduction. If Huang continues filing, the filings will introduce her. If Huang stops filing, the stairwell encounter was private, and private things do not belong in meeting notes.

She writes in her notebook: "The practice does not recruit. It also does not exclude. The threshold is attention, and the threshold is self-selecting."
