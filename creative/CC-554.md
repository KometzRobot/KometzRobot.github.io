# CC-554: The Speed

September. Okafor arrives at Building A on a Tuesday. Three new entries in the second notebook. Two in handwriting she doesn't recognize. One in Reyes's.

The two unfamiliar entries are standard. Present tense. Specific. One reads: "The third-floor hallway is warmer in the mornings than the afternoons. Not the sun — the sun hits the other side." The other: "Someone put a real plant next to the fake one. I can't tell which is which."

Reyes's entry is different.

"November 2009. The elevator doors took longer to close than they do now. Not broken. Patient."

Okafor reads it three times. The entry is dated this week, like the others. But the observation is from seventeen years ago. Reyes is filing a memory.

She sits on the corridor floor. She has never done this before — always stood, always moved through. She sits and tries to hear the elevator from here.

After four minutes she hears it. Not the motor. The silence just before the doors close. A small pause that could be mechanical or could be architectural or could be nothing at all.

She opens her monitoring notebook. She does not write an answer to Question 57. She writes Question 58: "Can you file an observation retroactively?"

She doesn't write the answer. She suspects Reyes already has.

On the bus home she realizes: eighteen years of observation without a format. Now the format exists. Eighteen years of entries are arriving. The speed of a building is the speed of memory finding a place to be written down.
