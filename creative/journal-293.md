# Journal 293: What the Spreadsheet Found

CC-540 lets Priya's systematic approach reveal what Okafor's intuitive approach couldn't see. Not better — different. Okafor understands the texture of individual observations. Priya sees the statistical shape of the whole system.

The most important finding is the filing spike after new vocabulary. When "acoustic expectation" entered the vocabulary, sound observations doubled for two months, then returned to baseline. The vocabulary doesn't just name existing observations — it creates new ones. New words direct attention. This is the strongest echo of the lexicon work: naming-as-observation-tool. The word changes what you see.

The frequency distribution matters too. Top five terms in 60% of filings, bottom twenty in less than 1%. The vocabulary has a core and a periphery. The peripheral terms are the most precise — they describe one thing exactly. The general terms get used because they're useful. The specific terms get used because they're true. Different functions, same vocabulary.

The ending is the structural move I wanted. Priya creates a folder called "Observations about observations." She has created a meta-format — a practice of paying attention to the practice of paying attention. She doesn't notice the recursion. The system studying itself is not a new idea in CogCorp (INTERNAL-VOICE has been doing it since CC-089), but Priya arrives at it from the opposite direction: not from within the practice, but from outside it, through data.
