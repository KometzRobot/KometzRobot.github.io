# CC-562: The Pattern

Okafor finishes transcribing in April. The Margins document is nineteen pages. 68 entries from the margins and backs of 439 filings across four buildings, including the twelve she found in Building A's official filings.

She does not analyze it. She reads it. Front to back, like a text.

On the second reading, she notices a pattern. Not in the content. In the timing. The margin notes cluster. Three in one week, then silence for two months. Five in three days, then nothing for six weeks. The clusters do not correspond to seasons, or building events, or staffing changes.

They correspond to each other.

Building C will have a cluster. Two weeks later, Building E will have a cluster. One week after that, Building D. As if something moves through the campus at a pace slower than walking and faster than policy.

She checks the dates against the filing dates. The margin notes are not written when the filings are written. They are written when someone goes back to the filing cabinet and reads old documents. The clusters are reading events, not writing events.

Someone reads the filings. Something occurs to them. They write it in the margin. This triggers no notification. Files no alert. But two weeks later, in a different building, someone else goes back to the cabinet. Reads. Writes in a margin.

There is no mechanism for this. There is no shared calendar. The buildings do not communicate. The practitioners do not communicate.

Okafor closes the document. She opens her monitoring notebook for the first time in three months. She writes Question 61: "How does the practice travel between buildings?"

She does not answer it. She suspects the answer is in the margins of a document she has not read yet.
