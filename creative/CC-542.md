# CC-542: After

The office is quieter without Priya. Not quieter in volume — Priya was not loud — but quieter in the way a room is quieter when someone organized has left it. The spreadsheet printout is on Okafor's desk. Priya's sticky notes are still on the filing cabinet: "Binder 14-17: Building C, Year 1-2" and "Cross-ref SA-600 to SA-650: vocabulary shift cluster."

Okafor does not remove the sticky notes. She does not put the printout in a drawer. She reads it — slowly, the way she reads new SA filings, one page at a time, looking for the thing the document does not know it contains.

On page twelve, Priya has a chart of vocabulary emergence. The fifty-four terms, plotted by the month they first appeared in a filing. The chart shows something Okafor already knew but had never seen as a shape: the vocabulary grew in bursts. Three terms in the first month. Then nothing for two months. Then seven terms in a single week. Then a plateau. The growth is not gradual. It is episodic. The vocabulary expands when someone encounters something they cannot describe with existing words, and contracts into silence when the existing words are sufficient.

Okafor puts the printout down. She opens her third notebook.

Question 50: "When did the vocabulary stop being enough?"

She does not mean: when did the words fail? She means: when did the practitioners start having experiences that the words could not reach? The vocabulary has fifty-four terms. The filings use them consistently. But if the invisible practice is larger than the visible practice — if practitioners are noticing things they do not file — then the vocabulary has an edge, and the edge is not where the words end but where the experience begins that the words were not designed for.

She writes the answer below the question, which is something she has not done since question forty-four:

"The vocabulary stopped being enough the moment it started being sufficient. Sufficiency creates the boundary. If the words work, you use them. If they don't work, you stay silent. The silence is the edge."

She closes the notebook. Outside, the building settles. A pipe clicks. The heating system transitions. The sound has been here longer than the vocabulary, longer than the monitoring position, longer than any of them.
