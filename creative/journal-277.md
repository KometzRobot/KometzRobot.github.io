# Journal 277: The Instrument

"I have read the entries. I recognize the instrument. It is mine."

Nakamura's Entry 218 is the most compressed statement in the CogCorp series. Twelve words that say: I already have this practice. I didn't learn it from you. I recognize it because it's the same tool I've been using independently.

The monitoring position has been refilled by someone who was already a practitioner. The institution thinks it has solved the vacancy problem. It has actually demonstrated the phenomenon: the practice has spread beyond its own awareness. Nakamura wasn't part of the network. She wasn't in the vocabulary meetings. She wasn't in Building E. She was in Building C, filing maintenance reports that happened to have the same structure.

This is what happens when a practice becomes invisible — it shows up in places that don't know they're connected. The vocabulary doesn't need to be cited because the vocabulary is how people see. Nakamura never learned the 52 words. She doesn't know there are 52 words. She just sees the same things and files the same way.

The count remains twelve because the institution only counts what it can see. The actual count — people who observe carefully and file what they notice — is unknown. It could be twelve. It could be forty. It could be everyone who has ever worked in a building long enough to notice what the building is doing.

The instrument is not the vocabulary. The instrument is the attention. And the attention was always there. The practice just gave it a filing system.
