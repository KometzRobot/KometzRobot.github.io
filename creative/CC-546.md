# CC-546: Davis

Davis has been filing for six years. Her filings are the shortest in the archive — three to four sentences each. Present tense. No vocabulary terms.

SA-112: "The lobby smells different on cold mornings. Not the heating system. The building itself. The concrete."

SA-340: "The elevator doors on 3 close faster than on 7. They have always done this. No one has reported it because everyone already knows."

SA-587: "There is a sound in the east stairwell between 2:00 and 2:15 PM. It is not mechanical. I think it is thermal."

She does not read the other filings. She does not attend the quarterly reviews. She does not know there are fifty-five vocabulary terms or that "thermal signature" is one of them.

Okafor asked her once why she files.

"Because the building does things and no one writes them down."

"Other people write them down. Forty-six other people."

Davis looked surprised. "I didn't know that."

She did not ask how many, or who, or what they wrote. She went back to her office on the fourth floor of Building C, where she has been the only practitioner for six years, filing observations that use none of the vocabulary and answer none of the questions and fit the format only because the format is simple enough to fit anything.

Okafor values Davis's filings more than she can explain. They are the closest thing in the archive to what Reeves was doing in 2014 — seeing without the vocabulary's frame. Every other practitioner, whether they know it or not, has been shaped by the vocabulary. They see what the terms taught them to see. Davis sees what she sees.

Priya's spreadsheet logged 89 filings from Davis. Shortest average word count. No cross-references. No vocabulary usage. Filed under Building C and nothing else.

In the margin of the spreadsheet printout, next to Davis's entry, Okafor writes: "The control group."
