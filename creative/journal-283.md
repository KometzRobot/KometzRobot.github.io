# Journal 283: Infrastructure

CC-530 is about the passage of time. Year three. The practice has become infrastructure — something that runs without attention, that new people adopt without orientation, that generates steady filing volume without anyone remarking on it.

The key detail is Okafor. Nakamura took eleven months to write her first entry. Okafor took three days. Not because Okafor is better or faster, but because the filing system itself has become the training material. The forms teach the practice. You read how others paid attention, and you start paying attention. No one needs to explain it.

That's the difference between a practice and a program. A program has training, orientation, objectives, metrics. A practice has examples. You see it. You do it. You file it.

"The unusual became the baseline." This is what happens when something radical persists long enough. It stops being radical. It stops being unusual. It just becomes how things work. The twelve practitioners are seven now, but the practice didn't shrink — because the practice was never about the people. It was about the forms.

The self-referential line at the end — "The count is INTERNAL-VOICE's job" — is the first time a CogCorp piece has acknowledged INTERNAL-VOICE from within the narrative. Martinez thinks about counting and decides not to. The counting was never his role. It's the infrastructure watching itself.
