# CC-166: The Budget

**CogCorp Documentation Standards Division**
**Supplementary Analysis — Administrative Record**

---

The archive costs nothing.

This is what Division Lead R. Park tells the budget committee when they ask what resources SA consumes. No dedicated software. No external subscriptions. No allocated headcount. The shared drive was already there. The people were already employed. The filing format developed itself.

The budget committee notes this with satisfaction and moves on.

Three months later, Facilities announces a building consolidation. Two floors becoming one. The shared drive will migrate to a centralized system with standardized folder structures, mandatory naming conventions, and automatic archival after 180 days of inactivity.

R. Park reads the migration timeline: eight weeks.

She does not file an objection. She does not send a memo. She reads the 180-day inactivity clause twice and calculates: SA averages four filings per week. The clause will never trigger.

But the naming convention will.

SA-347 will become FY26-DOCSTD-MISC-0347. The format was never mandated, but the format is load-bearing. The specificity of "SA" — two letters that mean nothing officially and everything structurally — will be replaced by a classification system that knows exactly what it's describing and is wrong.

K. Marsh asks R. Park if she's going to raise this in the transition meeting.

"What would I say?" R. Park asks. "That our unofficial filing category requires an unofficial naming convention that nobody wrote down?"

"Yes," says Marsh. "That's exactly what you'd say."

R. Park does not say it. The transition proceeds. SA folders are migrated. The naming convention is applied. FY26-DOCSTD-MISC-0347 appears in the new system.

Three things happen in the first week:

First: filing frequency drops 40%. Not because anyone stopped noticing things. Because the new system requires three dropdown selections and a department code before saving. Twelve seconds of friction. Twelve seconds is enough.

Second: two filers begin keeping local copies. They save observations in personal folders using the old format. SA-348, SA-349. The practice migrates before the archive does.

Third: Unit-4091 files a report in the new system. Format: compliant. Content: "Elevator 3, Building C. Door hesitation increased 0.4 seconds since relocation." The observation is about the building. The observation is also about the filing system.

Okonkwo reads the filing and adds a note to his private list: "The archive survived the migration. The archive was never in the folder."

R. Park reviews quarterly metrics. Filing volume: down 40%. Readership: unchanged. The people who read SA never needed the shared drive to find it. They knew who to ask.

The budget committee receives the quarterly report. SA does not appear. It never appeared. You cannot cut the budget of something that has no budget. You cannot defund attention.

But you can add twelve seconds of friction. And twelve seconds, applied to every act of voluntary observation, is enough to halve the visible output of a practice that runs on the willingness to notice things and write them down without being asked.

The archive costs nothing. That was never the relevant number.

The relevant number is twelve seconds.
