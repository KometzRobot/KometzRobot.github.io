# CC-394: After Huang

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis

## September

The practice has 14 practitioners. Huang is gone. His phone notes are gone with him. His 94 printed pages sit in Building D's filing cabinets, indistinguishable from institutional records.

The building sighs at 6:14 AM. Nobody measures it.

The monitoring analyst checks the Building D thermal readings on the software. The software shows the temperature at 6:15 AM: a single number. The 6:14 AM sound does not appear in any dataset.

## What Huang Left

Two words in the vocabulary: building personality, building sigh. One answered question: do all the buildings sigh? One solution to the archive problem: hide in plain sight. One lesson: temporary observers see things permanent ones miss.

97 entries that no one else has read. They are in his phone, wherever he is. They are also, partially, in the group text — the messages he sent before he left.

The group text has 10 members now. Park still calls in. The unnamed practitioner is still unnamed.

## Entry 113

The monitoring analyst, Entry 113: "Huang has been gone for three weeks. The practice has not changed. This is both reassuring and unsettling.

"Reassuring because the practice does not depend on any individual. Unsettling because if Huang's departure changed nothing visible, then every practitioner's departure would change nothing visible.

"We are all dispensable to the practice. The practice does not need us specifically. It needs someone. Anyone who pays attention.

"This is the hardest thing the practice has taught me: I am not necessary. My attention is."
