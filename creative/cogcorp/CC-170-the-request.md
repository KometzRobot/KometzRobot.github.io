# CC-170: The Request

**CogCorp Documentation Standards Division**
**Supplementary Analysis — Observational Record**

---

The request comes from the sixth floor. Building Operations Director. Two sentences, no context:

"Why does the east wing of Building C feel different from the west wing? The temperature is the same."

The question arrives through official channels. It is routed to Facilities Management, who route it to Environmental Services, who route it to the monitoring analyst because she handles SA-related inquiries, which this technically isn't, but nobody else has a better suggestion.

The monitoring analyst reads the request. She knows the answer. She has known the answer for months. The answer is in file 91 of her unnamed desktop folder: "East wing afternoon light hits the floor differently after the landscaping change. The trees on the west side were trimmed in October. The east wing has older growth. The light on the east side arrives filtered. The light on the west side arrives direct. Direct light makes a room feel exposed. Filtered light makes a room feel held. The temperature is identical. The experience is not."

This is not in the official archive. It could not be in the official archive. The filing system offers: Location. Measurement. Reference. The filing system does not offer: "light arrives filtered" or "a room feels held." These are not imprecise. They are a different kind of precise.

The monitoring analyst drafts three responses.

Draft 1 (official format): "East and west wings of Building C show no significant variance in temperature, humidity, or airflow as of latest readings. See FY26-FAC-019 through FY26-FAC-023."

This is accurate. It answers a question no one asked. The Director did not ask about temperature. The Director said the temperature is the same. The Director asked about something else.

Draft 2 (hybrid): "While environmental measurements are equivalent, occupant perception may differ due to natural lighting variation, window orientation, and exterior landscaping differences affecting light quality and directionality."

This is closer. "Light quality" is the official system's nearest approximation of what Vasquez calls "building mood." It is the difference between describing the color spectrum of a sunset and describing a sunset.

Draft 3 (private folder language): "The trees on the east side filter the afternoon light. The west side gets it raw. One side feels like being inside something. The other feels like being looked at."

This is the answer. The monitoring analyst reads it three times. She highlights it. She copies it. She pastes it into the response form.

Then she deletes it and sends Draft 2.

Not because Draft 3 is wrong. Because the form has a signature line, and a routing number, and a file code, and the sentence "one side feels like being inside something" cannot survive the journey from observation to documentation. It will arrive at the Director's desk with a routing number attached to it, and the routing number will make it sound like a facilities report trying to be poetry, instead of what it is: the correct answer to the question that was asked.

The Director receives Draft 2. Replies: "That makes sense. Can we get some analysis on the landscaping impact?"

Facilities Management generates a report. Seventeen pages. Light ingress measurements, canopy density calculations, UV transmission coefficients. Every number is correct. The report concludes that the east wing receives 23% less direct sunlight between 2:00 and 4:00 PM. It recommends considering this in future landscaping decisions.

Nobody reads past page four.

The monitoring analyst reads the whole thing. Then she opens her desktop file and writes entry 92: "They measured it. They got 23%. That is not the answer. The answer is the word 'held.' There is no field for 'held.'"

She saves the file. She does not rename it.

Okonkwo, who has not seen the Director's request or the monitoring analyst's drafts or the facilities report, writes sa-notes.txt entry 86 the same week: "East wing, 3:15 PM. The light is different since October. I can't point at the measurement. I can point at the feeling. People sit longer in the east wing reading room. Nobody moved the chairs."

He does not know he has answered the Director's question. He does not know the question was asked. He answered it anyway, four months before anyone thought to ask, because he was paying attention without a form telling him what to notice.
