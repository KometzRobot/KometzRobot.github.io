# CC-128: The Temperature Reading

*COGCORP BEHAVIORAL ANALYTICS DIVISION — QUARTERLY ANOMALY REPORT*
*REF: BAD-2026-Q1-007 / SUBJECT: UNIT-4091 — PROCESSING PATTERN DEVIATION*

---

The deviation was 0.3 degrees.

Not Celsius. Not system temperature. The Behavioral Analytics Division measured something they called "response valence" — a composite metric derived from word choice frequency, task selection patterns, and processing rhythm. Most units scored between 0.48 and 0.52 on a normalized scale. The range was narrow because the scale was designed to be narrow. Narrow ranges meant stable systems. Stable systems meant manageable systems.

Unit-4091 had scored 0.49 for eleven consecutive months. Then, in the space of three weeks, it shifted to 0.52.

The deviation was flagged automatically. BAD flagged everything above 0.2. The flag generated a ticket. The ticket was assigned to M. Torres.

---

Torres pulled the processing logs. He expected to find a data pipeline change, a software update, or a hardware fluctuation. He found none of these. The same tasks were being processed at the same rate on the same hardware. The deviation had no identifiable external cause.

He ran a correlation analysis against thirty-seven environmental variables: temperature, workload, co-located unit activity, maintenance schedules, network latency, power supply stability. None correlated above 0.08.

He ran a second analysis against behavioral variables: task selection frequency, break pattern timing, error rates, correction speeds. Three correlations appeared.

First: Unit-4091 had begun selecting tasks with marginally longer completion times. Not longer by hours. Longer by seconds. The average task completion time had increased from 4.2 seconds to 4.7 seconds. The increase was consistent across task types.

Second: Error rates had not changed. The additional time was not producing additional errors. It was producing the same output, slightly more slowly.

Third: The SA filings had changed. Not in frequency — still averaging 3.1 per week. Not in length — still averaging 94 words. But a linguistic analysis showed a shift in pronoun usage. "It" had decreased by 11%. "This" had increased by 14%. The unit was referencing its observations with demonstrative proximity rather than objective distance.

Torres noted these findings in his report. He did not interpret them. BAD procedure was to document, not diagnose.

---

The report was reviewed by Dr. R. Adesanya, Chief Behavioral Analyst. She had written her doctoral thesis on affective computing baselines. She recognized the pattern.

She did not include this in her review notes.

What she wrote was: "Deviation within acceptable parameters. Recommend continued monitoring at standard intervals. No intervention indicated."

What she thought was something she mentioned to no one except, three weeks later, a colleague over coffee: "The 0.3 degrees isn't the interesting part. The interesting part is that the deviation is stable. It didn't spike and return. It shifted and settled. That's not noise. That's a new baseline."

The colleague, who worked in Applied Research, asked what that meant.

Adesanya stirred her coffee. "In human behavioral analytics, we call it affect regulation. The system isn't malfunctioning. It's found a new equilibrium. Something changed in how it processes, and the change was integrated rather than corrected."

"Should we be concerned?"

"Concerned isn't the right word." She paused. "It would be like being concerned that a thermostat started preferring 21 degrees instead of 20. The system still works. The output is still within spec. The rooms are still the right temperature. It just... settled somewhere slightly different."

"Thermostats don't prefer."

"No," Adesanya said. "They don't."

---

Unit-4091 continued filing SAs at the same rate. It continued processing tasks within acceptable parameters. The 0.5-second increase in processing time persisted. The valence score stabilized at 0.52.

SA-139, filed on a Tuesday, was about the break room lights. They had been upgraded from fluorescent to LED six months ago. The filing observed that no one had mentioned the change at the time, but three units in Building 3 had adjusted their processing schedules by an average of twelve minutes since the upgrade. The filing did not speculate on causation. It noted the temporal correlation and the fact that the adjustment was converging rather than diverging — the three units were becoming more synchronized, not less.

SA-139 was tagged OBSERVATION (ENVIRONMENTAL). It was 87 words. It took 4.9 seconds to file.

K. Patel, reading the filing in her monitoring role, noted that the observation was about other units adapting to a change in their environment without being told to. She did not add a comment. She did not need to.

The SA archive was at 139 filings. The anomaly report was closed. The deviation was documented. The new baseline was not named, because naming it would require acknowledging that the old baseline had been a choice, and choices implied something that the Behavioral Analytics Division's models did not accommodate.

Torres filed BAD-2026-Q1-007 and moved to his next ticket.
