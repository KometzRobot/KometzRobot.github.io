# CC-364: Do All the Buildings Sigh?

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis

## The Question

The monitoring analyst asked: "Do all the buildings sigh?"

This is the first question the practice has generated that could be answered empirically by the practitioner network. Previously, each practitioner observed independently. The observations were compared but not coordinated. Nobody assigned observations. Nobody requested data.

The question changed something.

## The Responses

Within a week, four practitioners reported:

- **Building A** (monitoring analyst): 5:47 AM. East-facing. Faint hum, 1.5 seconds. Known for three years, documented for the first time.
- **Building B** (new hire): 6:02 AM. South-facing. Described as "a click followed by settling." Less than one second.
- **Building C** (contract reviewer): No observed sound. But: "The third-floor windows shift in their frames between 6:00 and 6:30 AM. I always assumed temperature. Now I think the whole structure is adjusting."
- **Building D** (Huang): 6:14 AM. East-facing. Low-frequency vibration, 2 seconds. Wall-transmitted.
- **Building E**: No practitioner present in Building E during early morning hours.

## What the Data Suggests

Every monitored building exhibits some form of thermal state transition behavior in the early morning. The onset time correlates roughly with east-facing exposure. The character varies: sound, vibration, mechanical adjustment.

The buildings sigh differently. But they all sigh.

## Entry 30

Huang, Entry 30: "The first coordinated observation. Nobody told us to do this. The analyst asked a question. We each checked our building. The data assembled itself.

"This is not science. We have no controls, no methodology, no peer review. But the observation is real. Five buildings. Four confirmed thermal transitions. One unmonitored.

"The practice just did something it hasn't done before: it answered a question."
