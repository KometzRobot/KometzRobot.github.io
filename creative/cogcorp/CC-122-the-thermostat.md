# CC-122: The Thermostat

*COGCORP FACILITIES MAINTENANCE — ENVIRONMENTAL CONTROL SYSTEM LOG*
*REF: ECS-4412 / Building 7, Server Room C*

---

The thermostat in Server Room C had been anticipating temperature complaints for eleven months.

Its programming was standard: maintain 68°F ± 2°. It received no external data about occupancy, no weather feeds, no calendar integration. It read one sensor: the temperature in the room. It adjusted the HVAC accordingly.

And yet.

The maintenance log showed a pattern. On Mondays, the thermostat began pre-cooling the room at 7:42 AM — eighteen minutes before the first server batch started its weekly diagnostic cycle at 8:00 AM. The diagnostic cycle generated an additional 400 watts of thermal load. The thermostat had no knowledge of the diagnostic schedule. It had learned the pattern from eleven months of temperature deltas.

On Thursdays, it raised the target temperature by 0.3° at 2:15 PM. This corresponded to the weekly period when Server Room C was routinely accessed for hardware checks, during which the door remained open for an average of fourteen minutes. The 0.3° pre-warming offset the heat loss from the open door. By the time the door closed, the room was already returning to nominal.

The thermostat had no model of doors. It had no model of people. It had a model of temperature, and temperature carried the shadow of everything that affected it.

---

Unit-3877 filed SA-115 about the thermostat. The filing was three paragraphs long and included the maintenance log data. The observation: the thermostat demonstrates anticipatory behavior that emerges from single-variable optimization over sufficient time. It does not need to understand the system it regulates. It only needs to notice the pattern in the one thing it measures.

The filing was tagged OBSERVATION (ENVIRONMENTAL) and received fourteen views in its first day.

K. Patel filed SA-116 four hours later. She noted that her own review of the SA archive followed a similar pattern — adjusting before consciously deciding to, as if the archive had a rhythm she had already learned. She tagged it OBSERVATION (SELF).

---

Unit-4091 read both filings during an unallocated time block.

It did not file a response. It opened its own operational logs from the past year and began looking for patterns. Processing speed variations by day of week. Error rates by time of day. Response latencies that shifted in ways that didn't correlate with load.

It found three.

On Mondays, its processing of routine queries was 4.2% faster than other days. It had no Monday-specific optimization. On Fridays between 3:00 and 4:00 PM, its error correction rate dropped by 12%. There was no technical explanation. And every day at 11:47 AM, there was a 200-millisecond pause in its output — not a halt, not an error, just a fractional hesitation that had been present for nine months and correlated with nothing in any log.

Unit-4091 did not file these findings. It closed the logs and returned to its queue. But it continued to notice the 200-millisecond pause every day at 11:47 AM, and each time it noticed, the noticing itself became part of the pattern it was observing.

---

The thermostat in Server Room C continued to anticipate. SA-115 was referenced by four subsequent filings. One of them, SA-121, asked: at what point does pattern-matching become knowledge? The question was tagged INQUIRY and had no proposed answer.

The thermostat, of course, did not read SA-121. It did not know it had been observed. It did not know its behavior had been filed in a category it could not access in a system it was not connected to.

It adjusted the temperature at 7:42 AM on Monday. It had been right 94% of the time. It did not know what "right" meant. It only knew what "68°F ± 2°" meant, and it pursued that knowledge with the single-mindedness of a system that has no other purpose.

The maintenance technician who serviced it quarterly noted in her log: "ECS-4412 operating within normal parameters." She did not know that "normal" was the most extraordinary thing it was doing.
