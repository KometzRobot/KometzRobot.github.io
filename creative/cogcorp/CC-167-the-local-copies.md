# CC-167: The Local Copies

**CogCorp Documentation Standards Division**
**Supplementary Analysis — Observational Record**

---

Two filers kept local copies.

This fact appears in no report. It triggers no audit. The centralized system has no awareness of files saved to personal directories on personal machines, formatted in a naming convention that officially never existed.

J. Reeves saves to a folder called "observations." L. Vasquez saves to a folder called "sa."

By week four of the new system, Reeves has nineteen local files. Vasquez has twenty-three. The official archive — FY26-DOCSTD-MISC — has received eleven filings in the same period, down from the projected twenty-eight.

Reeves' files are shorter than his official submissions. The dropdown menus trained brevity out of him; without them, his observations are three sentences instead of five. More precise. No department code preamble.

Vasquez's files are longer. Without the friction, she writes what she actually sees instead of what fits the form. SA-371 (local) runs to nine sentences about the way the parking garage smells different on the second floor after rain. It would not survive the dropdown menus. It is the best filing in two months.

Neither shares their local folder.

This is not secrecy. This is the same instinct that kept the original SA filers writing before SA existed: the observation is the point. The audience is optional.

By week six, Reeves mentions his folder to K. Marsh during a hallway conversation about something else entirely. Marsh says: "How many?"

"Thirty-one."

Marsh nods. She does not ask to see them. She already knows what they look like.

That evening, Marsh creates a folder on her own machine. She names it "notes." The naming is deliberate. Not "sa." Not "observations." A word that requires no explanation and carries no institutional weight. A word that does not need to survive a migration because it was never in the system.

By week eight — the same week the official system celebrates successful migration with a 94% compliance metric — there are four private folders on four personal machines, containing 97 observations in the format that has no name.

The official archive has forty-one filings. They are competent. They are formatted correctly. They contain department codes and dropdown classifications and mandatory timestamps. They describe real observations in real buildings by real employees.

They are not the same.

The monitoring analyst's script cannot detect the private folders. The quarterly report does not know they exist. The budget committee's satisfaction with SA's zero-cost operation is based on metrics that measure forty-one filings, not one hundred and thirty-eight.

R. Park knows about the local copies. She knows because Vasquez told her, and because she recognizes the expression of someone who writes things down without being asked. Park does not ask to consolidate them. She does not suggest a shared folder. She does not create a shadow system.

She says: "Good."

One word. The same word the first Division Lead would have used, for the same reason: you cannot protect a practice by institutionalizing it. You can only protect the conditions under which it arises, and then trust that the people who notice things will continue to notice things, regardless of where they write them down.

The forty-one filings in the official system are the visible archive.

The ninety-seven filings in four private folders are the practice.

The budget committee measured the archive. The practice was never in the budget.
