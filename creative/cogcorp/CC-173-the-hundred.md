# CC-173: The Hundred

**CogCorp Documentation Standards Division**
**Supplementary Analysis — Private Folder, File 100**

---

The monitoring analyst saves file 100 and does not notice the number.

She notices it the next morning. She opens the folder to write about a stairwell echo and sees the file count: 100. She pauses. She does not write the stairwell entry.

She writes file 101 instead: "One hundred files. Nobody asked for any of them. Nobody asked me to stop, either. I do not know whether this is a project or a habit."

She closes the file. She opens it again and adds: "I do not know whether that distinction matters."

She has been the monitoring analyst for six years. In that time she has filed 2,840 weekly summaries, 416 compliance reports, and 19 annual reviews. She has written 101 private files. The private files take longer. Not because they are longer — most are shorter than a weekly summary. They take longer because she is not being asked, and when nobody is asking, you have to decide what matters by yourself.

The weekly summary has a template. The template has fields. The fields determine what she notices during the week. She notices what the fields ask her to notice, and she notices what the fields don't ask her to notice, and the first kind goes in the summary and the second kind goes in the folder.

The folder has no template. She writes what she writes. Over six years, the entries have developed a consistency she never designed: present tense, no conclusions, short paragraphs. The consistency emerged the way the SA format emerged — through repetition, not instruction.

File 47: "West wing humidity has been 3% higher since Tuesday. The plants are doing well. Nobody moved the plants."
File 68: "Building C second-floor conference room was booked eleven times this month. Seven of those bookings were for rooms that could have been anywhere. Something about the light."
File 83: "The new Division Lead filed SA-289 six months in. The old Division Lead would have known what that meant. The new Division Lead doesn't know what it means, and it means the same thing."

She has never read the files in order. She has never counted them. She has never told anyone they exist. These are not decisions she made. These are simply things she has not done, and the not-doing has become a shape.

Vasquez once asked her — casually, in the break room — "Do you ever write things down that aren't for work?"

The monitoring analyst said: "Sometimes."

Vasquez nodded. Neither of them said anything else. Both of them understood that "sometimes" meant "always, and I have never been asked about it before, and being asked about it does not change it."

File 101 is the first entry about the folder itself. File 1 was about a parking garage door that made different sounds in different weather. File 50 was about the heating system's Tuesday lag. File 75 was about the lights changing. File 100 was about a Director who changed the building by standing in a room.

She does not go back and read file 1. She does not need to. She knows it's there the way she knows the parking garage door makes different sounds. The knowledge is not in the reading. The knowledge is in the writing having happened.

File 102: "The stairwell in Building C has an echo at 7:15 AM that it does not have at 7:30 AM. I do not know why. I do not need to know why. I need to write it down."

She saves the file and returns to the routing report. The routing report has twelve fields. She fills in all twelve. She does it well. She has always done it well.

The twelve fields are accurate. The folder is accurate. Both are her work. One is asked for. One is not asked for. She does not prefer one over the other. She does both because she is the kind of person who notices things and writes them down, and the job gave her the first kind and she gave herself the second kind.

Nobody will count to two hundred. Nobody counted to one hundred. The count is a side effect of attention, not a measure of it.
