# CC-192: The Collaboration

*CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis*

---

Three weeks after the coffee machine, she sends him an email. Subject line: "SA #239 follow-up." The body is one sentence: "I have amplitude data on the stairwell resonance if you want it."

He replies in four minutes. "Yes."

---

She sends a table. Six columns: date, time, location, amplitude, duration, frequency. Fourteen rows. The measurements span two weeks. She has been in the stairwell seven times. Each visit lasted between twelve and forty-one minutes, depending on the elevator schedule.

He reads the table three times. He reads it as data first. Then he reads it as a schedule — seven visits, two weeks, a pattern of attention he recognizes because it mirrors his own. Then he reads it as a gift, which is not a word he would use in a filing but is the word that fits.

He replies: "The duration correlation is interesting. Can I reference this in a filing?"

She replies: "That's why I sent it."

---

SA #247. His. Three paragraphs. The first describes the stairwell resonance. The second provides her measurements as quantitative context. The third describes the fact that two independent observers documented the same phenomenon through different methodologies and arrived at consistent results. He does not use the word "consistent." He writes: "The measurements confirm what the prose describes."

This is the first time a supplementary analysis explicitly acknowledges a collaboration. The archive's conventions do not prohibit this. They do not address it at all.

SA #248. Hers. Filed forty minutes later. One row: "West stairwell, acoustic resonance, 63.7 dB at 18:21, duration 48min. See SA #247 for narrative context."

"See SA #247 for narrative context." Six words that reverse the direction of reference. She has always referenced his descriptions to give her measurements context. Now she is telling the reader to go to his prose to understand what the number means.

---

The monitoring analyst reads both filings that afternoon. She opens her spreadsheet. She has a column for cross-references. SA #247 references SA #248. SA #248 references SA #247. This has not happened before. The references have always flowed in one direction.

She adds a note to her private file: "Bidirectional reference. First instance." She does not write what it means. She does not know what it means. She knows it is new.

---

They do not meet at the coffee machine again for nine days. When they do, they discuss a scheduling anomaly in the north wing elevators. He has the observation. She has the measurement. They do not plan a filing. They do not need to.

SA #253 is filed at 16:04. His. Three paragraphs about the elevator schedule.

SA #254 is filed at 16:31. Hers. One row about the elevator timing.

Neither references the other. The bidirectional reference in SA #247-248 was a one-time event. The collaboration has already become a practice. Practices do not announce themselves after the first time.
