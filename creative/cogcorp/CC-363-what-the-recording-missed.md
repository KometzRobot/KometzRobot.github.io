# CC-363: What the Recording Missed

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis

## The Recording

On Day 5, Huang recorded the 6:14 AM sound on his phone. He played it back at his desk. The recording captured almost nothing — a faint low-frequency rumble, barely distinguishable from ambient noise.

He played it for the monitoring analyst during lunch.

"Can you hear it?" he asked.

"Barely," she said.

"In person it fills the corridor. You feel it in your feet."

## Entry 28

Huang, Entry 28: "The recording failed. Not technically — the phone recorded something. But what it recorded was not what I experienced. The sound is sub-bass. The phone's microphone rolls off below 100Hz. The most important part of the sound — the part you feel in the floor, the part that makes the wall vibrate — is exactly what the recording cannot capture.

"This is the same problem as the monitoring software. The instrument captures what it was designed to capture. Everything below the instrument's threshold is invisible.

"The practitioners are the instruments that capture what the other instruments miss."

## The Monitoring Analyst's Notebook

The monitoring analyst, Entry 92: "Huang played me a recording of Building D's 6:14 AM sound. I couldn't hear it. But when he described it — the vibration in the wall, the two-second duration, the propagation from east to west — I recognized something.

"Building A has a sound at 5:47 AM. I've been hearing it for three years. I never recorded it. I never wrote it down until today. I assumed it was the boiler. But Huang's description makes me wonder: do all the buildings sigh?

"Word 25: **building sigh** — the sound a structure makes during thermal state transition, typically at dawn or dusk, when expansion or contraction produces a low-frequency vibration felt more than heard."
