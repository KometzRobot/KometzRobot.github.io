# CC-191: The Introduction

*CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis*

---

They meet at the coffee machine on a Tuesday.

She recognizes his badge — Applied Research, third floor — before she recognizes his face. He recognizes her lanyard — Quality Assurance, cubicle 2-14 — before he recognizes anything else. Neither mentions the archive.

"The machine takes forty-seven seconds," she says. This is true. She has timed it.

"It used to be forty-three," he says. "Before they replaced the filter housing in January."

She does not say: I know. She does not say: I read SA #214, which documented the change. She does not say: I filed SA #215 the same evening, measuring the acoustic difference in the brewing cycle. She says: "The new housing is louder."

"By about three decibels," he says.

She looks at him. He has estimated the difference she measured. His estimate is within 0.4 dB of her reading. She does not tell him this. She says: "Roughly."

---

They stand in the break room for four minutes. They discuss the building's HVAC schedule, which he has documented in prose and she has documented in measurements. They do not mention this overlap. They discuss the east wing renovation, which she experienced from cubicle 2-14 and he experienced from the third-floor lab. They do not mention their filings about it.

He says: "The south stairwell has an acoustic property."

She says: "I know. I measured it last week."

He says: "I heard it for four months before I wrote it down."

She says: "I read that."

This is the first time either of them acknowledges reading the other's work. It happens between sentences, without weight, as if they have been discussing it for years. In a sense, they have.

---

She does not file an SA about the conversation. He does not file an SA about the conversation. The archive does not require disclosure. It does not even require accuracy. It requires only that someone noticed something and chose to write it down.

She returns to cubicle 2-14. She opens her measurement log. She records: "Coffee machine brewing cycle, 47.2 seconds, 72.1 dB peak at 0:31 mark." She does not record the context, which is that she measured it while standing next to someone who heard the same machine and estimated three decibels and was right.

He returns to the third-floor lab. He opens a new document. He types three paragraphs about the ventilation rhythm in Building C's north corridor. The third paragraph describes how the airflow pattern changes when the break room door is open versus closed. He does not mention who was in the break room.

---

SA #241 is filed at 14:22. Hers. Coffee machine.

SA #242 is filed at 14:47. His. Ventilation.

Neither filing references the other. Neither filing references the conversation. The archive continues as it always has — two disciplines, two formats, two observers who happen to notice the same building.

The monitoring analyst reads both filings that evening. She does not note the coincidence. She has stopped noting coincidences between them. The frequency made it unremarkable.
