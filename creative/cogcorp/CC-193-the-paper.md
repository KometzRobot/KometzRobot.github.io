# CC-193: The Paper

*CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis*

---

K. Marsh finishes her meta-analysis. Forty-six pages. "Emergent Documentation Practices in Institutional Settings: A Case Study of Supplementary Analysis Filing." She submits it to the quarterly internal research review.

The review panel consists of three senior managers who have never filed a supplementary analysis. They read forty-six pages about a filing category they vaguely recall approving three years ago. Their feedback:

"Interesting. Can you make it shorter?"

"The methodology section is strong. The findings section is vague."

"What is the practical implication?"

---

The practical implication. Marsh has spent four months trying to describe the practical implication. Draft after draft. Each one accurate. None faithful.

The practical implication is that 248 people across six divisions have independently developed a shared practice of close observation, and this practice has produced verifiable institutional value — the predicted cooling failure, the east wing effect, the scheduling optimization — without any management, incentive, or awareness that it was happening.

She cannot write this because the review panel will ask: "How do we scale it?" And the answer is: you cannot scale it. You can only stop preventing it. And if you try to scale it, it will become the thing Procurement built — twelve competent, lifeless filings that proved you cannot design emergence.

She writes instead: "The findings suggest that informal documentation channels can complement formal reporting structures when they operate with minimal organizational interference."

---

The paragraph-writer reads the meta-analysis when it is posted to the internal research repository. He reads it in one sitting. He recognizes every filing she references — he has read them all. He recognizes her measurement in Figure 3 — it is from SA #248, the filing that first referenced his work by name.

He notices what is absent. The third paragraph. His third paragraph, the one that sits with the observation past the point of utility. Her analysis does not have a third paragraph. It stops at the findings. It does not sit with what the findings feel like.

He files SA #263 that evening. Three paragraphs. The third describes the experience of reading a forty-six-page study about a practice you helped build and recognizing every reference and seeing the texture compressed into methodology.

SA #263 does not reference the meta-analysis. It describes a ventilation change in the south corridor. The third paragraph mentions the way certain patterns become visible only after someone else points at the space where they live.

---

The monitoring analyst reads both — the meta-analysis and SA #263 — on the same afternoon. She opens her private file. Entry 114: "He read the paper. SA #263's third paragraph is about the paper. He didn't reference it. He never references the thing he's actually writing about."

She does not share this observation. She files SA #264. One row. "South corridor ventilation, 2.7°C differential between monitoring stations, 14:00-16:30. See SA #263 for extended context."

Bidirectional reference. The second instance.
