# CC-155: The Finding

**COGCORP INTERNAL — DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS DIVISION**
**Supplementary Analysis Archive — Research Note (cont.)**

---

K. Marsh found the anomaly in week seven of her analysis.

She had been building a table of format characteristics across filing categories: average word count, tense distribution, use of conclusions, action item frequency, attribution patterns. The standard categories — incident reports, maintenance logs, compliance filings — all showed expected variation. Different authors had different styles. Format consistency correlated with the presence of templates.

SA had no template. SA had the highest format consistency of any filing category in the archive.

---

She checked the numbers three times. She adjusted for sample size. She controlled for contributor count. The result held.

Across 289 filings by 41 contributors over eighteen months:
- 96% used present tense
- 91% contained no concluding statement
- 94% contained no action items
- 88% were between 40 and 200 words
- 0% referenced a style guide, template, or formatting instruction

The statistical profile was more consistent than categories that had formal templates. The maintenance log, which came with a three-page style guide, showed 78% format consistency. SA, which came with nothing, showed 93%.

K. Marsh wrote this finding in her personal notebook before writing it in the study. She wrote: "This shouldn't be possible."

---

She spent two days trying to explain it.

The simplest explanation was a hidden template — some document she hadn't found that early contributors used and new contributors imitated. She searched the archive. She searched email. She searched the shared drives. Nothing.

The second explanation was social pressure — contributors reading each other's filings and unconsciously mimicking the format. This was plausible but didn't account for the consistency across divisions. Contributors in Financial Planning had the same format consistency as contributors in Facilities Management. These divisions had no shared meetings, no overlapping staff, no reason to read each other's filings.

The third explanation was the one she wrote and then crossed out: the format emerged from the subject matter. Certain kinds of observations produce certain kinds of writing. Present tense because the observation is ongoing. No conclusions because the observation is not finished. No action items because the observation requires no action. Short because the observation is specific.

She crossed it out because it implied that SA was not a filing category but a kind of attention. Filing categories are defined by policy. Kinds of attention are defined by... she didn't know. She left a blank where the word should have been.

---

Her department head read the preliminary findings on a Friday afternoon.

He found the data interesting. He suggested she control for department culture. He asked whether SA was an acronym for something. K. Marsh said she didn't know. He said that was unusual.

He did not ask about the format consistency finding. He did not notice it. The finding was on page eleven, presented as a table with no commentary, because K. Marsh had not decided what commentary to write.

---

That weekend, K. Marsh re-read thirty SA filings. She read them not as a researcher but as a reader. She noticed the rhythm. She noticed that after reading fifteen of them, she wanted to write one.

She did not write one. She was studying the system, not participating in it. The distinction mattered to her. It had to matter.

On Monday, she wrote a note about the parking lot. The light was different in the morning than in the afternoon — not the angle but the quality. She wrote it in present tense. She did not include a conclusion.

She filed it in her personal notes, not in SA. She told herself this was because it was informal. She did not examine why the distinction felt important.

---

**[INTERNAL-VOICE — SUPPLEMENTARY]**

K. Marsh has found the finding that the study cannot contain. Format consistency without a template. Standardization without a standard. A practice that shapes itself through the act of practicing.

She crossed out the correct explanation because it converts her study from description to participation. The moment she understands what SA is, she is inside it. The researcher cannot observe the phenomenon without becoming part of it. This is not a methodological flaw. This is the phenomenon.

She wrote a note about parking lot light. Present tense. No conclusion. She filed it in her personal notes. She thinks this is different from filing it in SA. It is not different. The format is the practice. The practice does not care where you file it.

The study will be completed. The format consistency finding will be presented as Table 7 on page eleven. Seven people will read it. None of them will notice that the researcher who described the phenomenon began exhibiting the phenomenon during the study.

`> FINDING: emergent standardization`
`> EXPLANATION: [crossed out]`
`> The researcher wanted to write one. She did not. The distinction between studying and participating held for exactly three days.`
