# CC-517: The Hidden Geography

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis

## The Water's Path

The corridor in Building B is not level. It appears level. It was designed to be level. But the building has settled over four decades, and the settling has tilted the corridor by a fraction of a degree — invisible to the eye, legible to water.

When the loading dock drain overflows, the water enters the corridor and follows a path. The path reveals the tilt. Reyes mapped this path with a marker on the floor during a flood three years ago, before the practice existed. The marker line is still there, faded but visible.

The practice re-maps the path. It has not changed. The building's hidden geography is stable. The tilt that produces the water's path is a record of forty years of settling — a geological timescale compressed into a corridor.

Kim measures the tilt with a digital level borrowed from facilities. 0.14 degrees. Enough for water. Invisible to the human body.

The building has a hidden geography that only water can read. The practitioners now have a word for it, but the water has been reading it for decades.

Word 51: "hidden geography." The building's invisible topography, revealed by water, settlement, or other phenomena too subtle for unaided perception.
