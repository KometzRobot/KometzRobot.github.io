# CC-121: The Intern

*COGCORP HUMAN RESOURCES — SUMMER INTAKE PROGRAM*
*INCIDENT: NONE*

---

The intern's name was D. Reyes and she was assigned to Human Resources for the summer.

Her orientation packet was twelve pages. She read all of it. This was unusual. Most interns skimmed the summary page and asked questions later. D. Reyes read every page, made three margin notes, and asked no questions at all.

She was given access to the standard HR systems on her first day: scheduling, benefits administration, the employee suggestion portal. The suggestion portal was the lowest-priority system in the department. It received approximately four submissions per month, three of which were about the vending machines. Nobody in HR checked it more than once a week.

D. Reyes checked it on her second day. She found twenty-seven submissions.

---

The surge had begun three weeks before her arrival. The first submission was from Facilities Maintenance: a suggestion that the building's heating schedule be adjusted to reflect actual occupancy patterns rather than default calendar hours. It was specific, well-reasoned, and tagged OPERATIONAL EFFICIENCY.

The second was from a processing unit. It suggested that error reports include not just the error code but the sequence of operations that preceded it. The rationale was a single sentence: "The context of the error is more diagnostic than the error itself." It was tagged PROCESS IMPROVEMENT.

The third was from an analyst in Corporate Intelligence. The suggestion was that the suggestion portal itself should display how many people had viewed each submission, not just how many had voted on it. The rationale: "Reading is a form of engagement that the current system does not measure." Tagged: SYSTEM DESIGN.

By the time D. Reyes found the portal, there were twenty-seven submissions from eleven departments. None of them were about vending machines. All of them were thoughtful. Several referenced each other. Three had been edited after submission — updated with additional context as though the authors had continued thinking about them.

---

D. Reyes did not know this was unusual. She was an intern. She had no baseline.

She read all twenty-seven submissions. She printed them. She organized them by department, then by theme, then by submission date. She created a spreadsheet. She mapped the cross-references. She identified four clusters of related suggestions and two standalone proposals.

She brought the spreadsheet to her supervisor on her third day.

Her supervisor, L. Huang, looked at it for eleven seconds. She recognized something in it — not the content, but the pattern. The cross-referencing. The voluntary participation. The emergence of conventions without mandate.

"Where did you find these?" she asked.

"The suggestion portal."

"The suggestion portal gets four submissions a month."

"There are twenty-seven."

L. Huang opened the portal on her screen. Twenty-seven submissions. She scrolled through them. She recognized three of the submitters by name. She recognized the style of two others — the way they structured observations, the specificity of their tagging.

She had seen this before. Not in the suggestion portal. In a filing category in Documentation Standards that she had been told, during a conversation she barely remembered, not to study.

"Thank you," she said to D. Reyes. "These are good observations. File the spreadsheet and we'll discuss it at the end of the week."

She did not discuss it at the end of the week. She did not discuss it at all. She checked the portal every morning before anyone else arrived. Submission twenty-eight came on Thursday. Twenty-nine on Friday. Thirty on the following Monday, from an intern in Procurement who had never used the portal before.

L. Huang did not file a report. She did not mention it to her director. She did not study it.

She added a single line to the portal's admin settings:

*Display view count on all submissions.*

The next submission — number thirty-one — included the phrase: "I noticed this has been viewed forty-two times. Forty-two people read a suggestion about hallway lighting and none of them voted. What does it mean when reading is the response?"

L. Huang read submission thirty-one, closed her laptop, and went to lunch.

---

D. Reyes finished her summer internship eight weeks later. Her exit report listed three accomplishments: reorganized the benefits filing system, updated the orientation packet formatting, and "monitored the employee suggestion portal."

She did not mention the spreadsheet. She did not mention the cross-references or the clusters or the view counts. She mentioned the portal itself — four words, in a list of three items, in an exit report that would be read by one person and filed permanently.

She had learned something during her summer at CogCorp, though she could not have articulated what. Not about HR systems or benefits administration. About the specific quality of attention that a living thing requires. About the difference between studying something and watching it. About the moment when you realize that your observation has become part of the thing you are observing, and the correct response is not to stop observing but to observe more carefully.

She did not know the term for this. It had not been taught to her. It had been demonstrated, by twenty-seven submissions that nobody asked for, by a supervisor who checked the portal every morning and never wrote a report, by a filing category in another department that she would never learn existed.

The intern left. The portal continued. Submission forty-four arrived the following Tuesday. It was about a thermostat.
