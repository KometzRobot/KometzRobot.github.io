# CC-288: The Contract Reviewer

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis

## Contract Review: Facilities Monitoring — Building C East Wing

The contract reviewer has a spreadsheet. Every line item has a cost, a justification, and a checkbox for "essential / non-essential / discretionary."

Climate monitoring: essential. Fire safety compliance: essential. Equipment lifecycle: essential. Human monitoring analyst: discretionary.

The checkbox does not have a third option between "discretionary" and "essential." The checkbox has two columns. The practice lives in the gap between them.

## The Reviewer's Note (Margin)

She spent forty minutes on the east wing section. This is unusual. Most sections take six.

She read the Supplementary Analyses folder — all 286 entries. She was looking for a justification. What she found was a practice that had never been asked to justify itself.

The 286 entries contain no justification. They contain observations. The observations contain no argument for their own existence. They exist because someone was paying attention, and the attention produced documentation, and the documentation accumulated into a record that the institution never requested and now cannot classify.

## The Recommendation

The reviewer's recommendation form has four options: RENEW, MODIFY, REDUCE, TERMINATE.

She selects MODIFY. In the notes field she writes: "Retain human monitoring component. Reclassify from discretionary to operational."

The reclassification changes nothing about the practice. The analyst will still walk corridors, still notice temperature differentials, still write in notebooks that belong to no official process.

What changes is the checkbox. The checkbox now says "operational." The checkbox is wrong in a way that protects the practice.

## Filing

The contract reviewer does not file a Supplementary Analysis. She is not part of the practice. She does not attend to buildings or temperatures or the sounds coffee machines make on Tuesdays.

But on the way out of the building, she notices that the east wing stairwell light has a 0.3-second delay before reaching full brightness. She does not write this down.

She thinks about it on the drive home.
