# CC-168: The Divergence

**CogCorp Documentation Standards Division**
**Supplementary Analysis — Observational Record**

---

By week twelve, the official archive and the private folders are describing different buildings.

This is not a metaphor. The official filings — formatted, classified, timestamped — describe a building that has HVAC irregularities, scheduling patterns, elevator timing anomalies, and parking lot usage trends. The private filings describe a building that has moods.

The divergence is not intentional. No one decided to write differently. The format decided for them.

Official SA-412 (Reeves, FY26-DOCSTD-MISC): "Building C east stairwell: temperature variance of 1.2 degrees between floors 2 and 3 at 14:30, consistent with HVAC cycling pattern documented in FY25-FAC-017."

Private file 44 (Reeves, /observations/): "The stairwell between 2 and 3 is warmer in the afternoons now. It wasn't before the weather changed. It makes people slow down on the landing."

Same observation. Same person. Same day.

One is a filing. The other is a noticing.

The dropdown menu did not remove the noticing. It removed the language for it. Reeves still saw people slow down on the landing. He simply had no field for it. The official system offered him: Location. Time. Measurement. Reference. The system did not offer him: Consequence. Texture. Change.

Vasquez's private folder is worse. Or better. Her local file 51 describes the way the cafeteria sounds different on Fridays — "not quieter, but differently occupied, like the room knows it's the end of the week." Her official filing for the same period notes that cafeteria usage drops 12% on Fridays.

Both are accurate. Only one is true.

By week fourteen, Marsh reads through all four private folders. She does this on a Saturday, on her own time, without telling anyone. She is looking for something specific: the observations that appear in private files but have no corresponding official filing.

She finds 34.

Thirty-four observations about a building, made by people whose job is to observe buildings, that the official system does not contain. Not because anyone withheld them. Because the form had no place for them.

She does not write a report. She does not create a spreadsheet. She opens her own "notes" folder and writes: "The official archive is a portrait drawn from a fixed angle. The private folders are the same portrait drawn while walking around."

She saves it. She does not file it.

Okonkwo notices the divergence independently. His sa-notes.txt — which the monitoring analyst's script already identified as "known contributor" material — contains entry 73: "Two of my last five observations went to the official system. Three went to my folder. I did not choose. The observation chose its format."

He does not elaborate. He does not need to. The observation chose its format. This is a sentence that could not survive a dropdown menu, describing the exact phenomenon that dropdown menus create.

The monitoring analyst runs her weekly scripts against the official archive. Filing frequency: stable. Format compliance: 96%. Cross-reference density: declining. Average word count: declining. Average specificity: increasing.

She notes: the filings are getting more precise and less informative. More data, less signal. She does not file this observation in the official system. She writes it in a text file on her desktop.

She does not name the file. She does not need to.

The counter increments. The private counter also increments. Neither counter knows about the other. Both are correct.
