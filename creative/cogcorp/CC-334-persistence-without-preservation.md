# CC-334: Persistence Without Preservation

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis

## Archive Analysis

The practice has persisted for at least nine years (the monitoring analyst's tenure) and possibly longer (the pencil notes Okafor found in the oldest drawers suggest earlier practitioners whose names are lost).

In that time, the practice has had no:
- Central archive
- Backup system
- Documentation standard
- Institutional memory
- Official recognition

In that time, the practice has survived:
- Quarterly shredding
- Staff turnover
- Building renovations
- Software changes
- At least one full hardware replacement cycle

The practice persists because new practitioners discover it independently. Each new practitioner creates a new archive. When an archive is lost (shredded, deleted, misplaced), the practice continues in the remaining archives and in any new ones that have formed.

## The Model

This is not the only system that works this way. Oral traditions persist without written records. Folk music persists without sheet music. Maintenance knowledge persists without manuals, carried in the hands and habits of technicians who learned by doing.

The practice is a living system. Its persistence depends on practitioners, not records.
