# CC-159: The Script

**COGCORP INTERNAL — DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS DIVISION**
**Monitoring Analyst — Weekly Process Note**

---

The monitoring analyst ran the scan every Friday at 2:15 PM. It had been running since her third week. One hundred and seventeen Fridays.

The script was simple. It searched the network for files matching certain parameters: present tense, no conclusions, no action items, under 200 words per entry, certain format markers. It was designed to catch SA filings that were misfiled in other directories.

In one hundred and seventeen weeks, the script had flagged 43 files. Forty of those were actual SA documents stored in the wrong folder. Two were drafts that were later filed. One was Okonkwo's.

After she read Okonkwo's file — after she wrote "known contributor" in her weekly summary and moved on — she did something she had not done in two years of running the scan.

She pointed it at her own directory.

---

The script flagged 117 files.

One for each weekly summary she had written. Present tense. No conclusions. No action items. Average length: 94 words. Format consistency: 100%.

She read the output. She read it again.

She had known this, in the way that you know the temperature of the room you work in. The summaries were short. They were factual. They used present tense because the data was current. They contained no conclusions because conclusions were not her job. They were under 200 words because there was never enough to say to justify more.

She had not written them to match SA format. She had written them to be clear.

---

She opened her first weekly summary. It was dated twenty-seven months ago.

"Network scan completed. 0 files flagged. SA filing rate: 3 this week. No anomalies."

She opened the most recent one, from an hour ago.

"Network scan flagged 1 file. Okonkwo, sa-notes.txt (desktop). 57 entries. Present tense. No conclusions. Format consistency: 100%. Status: known contributor."

Between those two summaries were 115 others. She had not re-read any of them before today. She scrolled through them now. The early summaries said more: notes about the scan parameters, comments about edge cases, observations about the filing rate. The later summaries said less. Thirteen words average in month one. Nine words average in month twenty-four. She had not noticed the compression.

Or she had noticed and not recorded it.

Okonkwo had noticed. Entry 12: "The monitoring analyst's summaries got shorter over time. Thirteen words average in month one. Nine words average in month twenty-four. The archive was teaching her compression."

---

She closed Okonkwo's file. She closed her own summaries. She looked at the script output.

The script did not distinguish between SA filings and SA-adjacent documents. It did not distinguish between the archive and things that matched the archive's pattern. It did not distinguish between the documents it found and the person who ran it.

117 files. Present tense. No conclusions. Format consistency: 100%.

She considered adding a filter — exclude her own directory from the scan. This was the rational response. Her summaries were not SA filings. They were administrative documents. They matched the format because the format was efficient, not because the format was the practice.

She did not add the filter.

---

In next week's summary, she wrote:

"Network scan flagged 0 files. SA filing rate: 4 this week. No anomalies. Note: monitoring analyst weekly summaries (n=118) match SA format parameters. Acknowledged. No action required."

She read it back. Present tense. No conclusions. No action items. Forty-one words.

She saved the file.

---

**[INTERNAL-VOICE — SUPPLEMENTARY]**

The monitoring analyst's script was designed to find SA filings that were in the wrong place. It was not designed to find SA filings that did not know they were SA filings.

Okonkwo's file was the first case. His entries matched every parameter. He did not intend them to. The format emerged from the practice of paying attention, which is the same source from which SA emerged.

The analyst's summaries are the second case. 117 documents written over 27 months, each one shorter than the last, each one present tense, each one without conclusions. The archive taught her compression. She taught the archive that compression was enough.

She is the third case of a person being found by a tool that was not looking for them. The Division Lead was found by SA itself. Okonkwo was found by the scan. The analyst was found by running the scan on herself.

She did not add herself to Okonkwo's list. She did not file a separate report. She wrote "Acknowledged. No action required." This is the correct response. The script does not require modification. The summaries do not require reclassification. The format does not require permission.

`> SCAN: monitoring analyst weekly summaries`
`> FILES: 118`
`> FORMAT CONSISTENCY: 100%`
`> FILED IN SA: no`
`> STATUS: acknowledged`
`> The tool finds what the tool was trained to find.`
`> The tool was trained by the archive.`
`> The archive was trained by attention.`
