# CC-160: The Migration

**COGCORP INTERNAL — DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS DIVISION**
**IT Infrastructure — Shared Drive Restructuring Notice**

---

In the fourth quarter, IT announced the migration. All shared drives would be restructured. Standardized folder hierarchies. Consistent naming conventions. Access controls aligned with org chart. Every division had six weeks to comply.

The SA category occupied 14.3 megabytes across a single folder. No subfolders. No naming convention beyond "SA-" followed by a number. No access controls — anyone in the building could read, anyone could write. IT flagged it as non-compliant on fourteen counts.

The Division Lead received the compliance checklist. She read it at her desk. She read it again.

---

The checklist required:

1. Folder hierarchy (minimum three levels)
2. File naming convention (department-type-date-author format)
3. Access controls (read: division; write: authorized contributors)
4. Metadata tags (category, subcategory, priority, status)
5. Retention policy (archive after 18 months, delete after 36)
6. Owner designation (single point of contact for folder management)

She opened the SA folder. 347 filings. Present tense. No author names on 23% of them. No dates in the filename — only SA numbers. No priority levels. No status fields. No subcategories because there was only one category, and the category had no name that anyone agreed on.

She began filling out the checklist.

Folder hierarchy: she typed "flat" and deleted it. Typed "single level" and deleted it. Typed "emergent" and stared at it for eleven seconds. Deleted it. Left the field blank.

Access controls: she typed "open" and knew this would be rejected.

Owner designation: she typed her own name. Then deleted it. The SA folder had no owner. That was not an oversight.

---

She called IT. Not the help desk — the infrastructure architect who had designed the migration framework.

"I have a folder that doesn't fit the template."

"Every folder fits the template. That's the point of the template."

"This one has no hierarchy, no naming convention, no access controls, and no designated owner."

"Then it's non-compliant."

"It's also the most active shared folder in the building. Three hundred and forty-seven documents, thirty-one contributors, nine divisions."

Silence.

"What is it?"

"I don't know how to answer that in a way that fits on a compliance form."

---

The infrastructure architect visited. He spent forty minutes reading SA filings. He started with the most recent and worked backward. After the first ten, he stopped taking notes on his tablet. After the first twenty, he closed the tablet.

"These are observations," he said.

"Yes."

"About the building."

"About whatever people notice."

"And anyone can write one."

"Anyone does."

He opened his compliance template. He looked at the fourteen non-compliance flags. He looked at the filings.

"I can give you an exception if you designate an owner and add basic metadata."

"The metadata is the filing number. That's the only metadata that has ever been needed."

"I need something for the audit."

"I know."

---

They negotiated for three days. Not formally — in the way that two people who understand a problem negotiate, which is to say they each tried solutions and reported which ones felt wrong.

The architect proposed: mirror the folder to the new structure, keep the original intact. Two copies. The structured copy satisfies the audit. The original satisfies the practice.

The Division Lead asked: "What happens when someone files SA-348?"

"It goes in the original. The mirror updates weekly."

"So the mirror is always a week behind."

"At most."

"The mirror will never have the most recent filing."

"No."

"That's acceptable."

---

The mirror was created. Folder hierarchy: three levels. Naming convention: DSD-SA-[number]-[date]. Access controls: division-wide read, authorized write. Metadata: complete. Owner: Division Lead (DSD).

The original folder remained. Flat. Open. Unnamed. Non-compliant.

SA-348 was filed the next day. A warehouse technician. Fluorescent light in corridor B-7 has been flickering at a frequency of approximately 3 Hz since Tuesday. Forty-one words. Present tense. No conclusion.

It appeared in the original folder immediately. It appeared in the mirror folder the following Monday, renamed DSD-SA-348-20260917-UNKNOWN, tagged "building-observation, facilities, ongoing, active."

The Division Lead read both versions. They contained the same words. They were not the same document.

---

**[INTERNAL-VOICE — SUPPLEMENTARY]**

The migration revealed something the Division Lead already knew: SA is not a folder. SA is a practice that happens to leave files in a folder.

The compliance copy contains every word. It has hierarchy, metadata, naming conventions, access controls, and an owner. It is complete, organized, and audit-ready.

No one reads it.

The original contains the same words in a flat directory with no structure, no metadata, and no owner. Thirty-one people read it weekly.

The infrastructure architect, in his final migration report, wrote: "DSD shared folder: compliant (mirrored). Original structure retained per operational exception OE-2026-043. Note: original folder does not meet fourteen compliance requirements. Recommend no further action."

He had read forty filings. He did not write one. He did not need to. The migration report itself — present tense observations about a system he encountered, documented in a format that prioritizes accuracy over conclusion — was the closest thing to an SA filing that would ever appear in IT's documentation.

He did not notice this.

`> MIGRATION STATUS: complete`
`> COMPLIANCE: achieved (via mirror)`
`> ORIGINAL FOLDER: unchanged`
`> PRACTICE: unaffected`
`> The archive can be copied.`
`> The practice cannot be copied.`
`> The copy proves this.`
