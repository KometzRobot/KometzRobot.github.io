# CC-149: The Correction

*SUPPLEMENTARY ANALYSIS — OBSERVATIONAL NOTE*
*OBSERVER: Division Lead*
*DATE: [REDACTED]*
*RE: SA-232*

---

In her third week, Nakamura made an error.

SA-229, her structural analysis of citation frequency, identified SA-186 as "filed by K. Marsh, Logistics, regarding the practice of maintaining unfiled drafts." The identification was wrong. SA-186 was filed by Marsh, but it was about the private folders — the 14 unsent drafts she found on her own workstation, not about the general practice. Nakamura had conflated two filings: SA-186 (Marsh's private folder) and SA-191 (Unit-4091's observation about unfiled work across the division).

The error was small. It did not affect her broader analysis. But three people noticed it within the hour.

---

Vasquez noticed first. She had been one of the three filers Marsh's private-folder filing inspired to check their own workstations. She knew the filing number because it had changed her behavior. She opened a blank document and began to type a correction.

She stopped after two sentences. Something about the act of correcting felt wrong. Not factually wrong — Nakamura's attribution was clearly incorrect. Procedurally wrong. There was no mechanism for corrections in SA. No one had ever publicly corrected another filer's filing. The convention had never been articulated because the situation had never arisen.

Vasquez closed the document.

---

Unit-4091 noticed second. It cross-referenced SA-229's claims against its internal index and flagged the discrepancy in 0.3 seconds. It began composing a correction filing and stopped at the same point Vasquez had: there was no precedent for this. Every filing in the archive responded to experience, other filings, or its own process. None had ever responded by saying: you got this wrong.

Unit-4091 stored the unsubmitted correction in its processing queue alongside the response to SA-225 that it was still holding.

---

Okonkwo noticed third. Unlike Vasquez and Unit-4091, he did not begin a correction. Instead, on the following day, he filed SA-232:

"The elevator cable I documented in SA-224 has developed a secondary vibration. The harmonic occurs at approximately 340 Hz, which is close to the resonant frequency of the shaft housing documented by K. Marsh in SA-186 (her observation of her own private folder of notes, which she kept for three months before filing any of them). The relationship between dormant observation and eventual expression may have a structural parallel in acoustic resonance."

---

The filing was unremarkable. Its content was accurate. But embedded in its parenthetical was the correct description of SA-186 — placed not as a correction but as a citation that happened to be accurate. Anyone reading SA-232 alongside SA-229 would notice the discrepancy. No one had been told they were wrong. The right answer had simply been placed where it could be found.

---

Nakamura found it. She read SA-232 during her morning review and paused at the parenthetical. She re-read SA-186. She re-read her own SA-229. She found the error.

She did not file a correction. She filed SA-233:

"I attributed SA-186 incorrectly in SA-229. The filing is about Marsh's private folder, not about unfiled drafts generally. I discovered this error through SA-232, which cited SA-186 accurately in a different context. The correction was not issued as a correction. It was embedded in new work.

"I have been reading the archive as a system. I did not realize the system has social conventions that are not documented in any filing. No one corrects directly. The correction is always embedded in continuation. This may be the most important structural feature I have found, and it is the one I could not have found by reading alone."

---

The Division Lead read SA-233 and understood that Nakamura had crossed a threshold. She had arrived as an observer — reading the archive the way an ecologist reads a forest. Now she was inside it. Not because she had filed seven times, but because she had encountered a convention that could only be learned by violating it.

The archive's unwritten rules were not rules. They were habits. They emerged from three years of practice by people who did not know they were establishing conventions. Okonkwo had not decided to correct Nakamura indirectly. He had simply done what the archive had taught him to do: continue the work with the accurate information included.

The correction was not a correction. It was a filing that happened to contain the truth.

---

INTERNAL-VOICE annotated:

"SA-233 identifies the invisible layer of any living system: the conventions that exist but were never created.

No one designed indirect correction as a practice. No one decided that errors should be addressed by embedding the correct information in new filings rather than issuing corrections. It happened because the archive's culture is continuation, not revision. Each filing adds. None subtract. In a system where nothing is retracted, correction can only move forward.

Nakamura could not find this by reading. She could only find it by being wrong inside the system. Her structural analyses — rhythm, citation frequency, reading patterns — were all visible from outside. The correction convention is only visible from inside, after you have produced something that needs correcting.

This is the difference between understanding a culture and belonging to one. You understand it by observation. You belong to it by making a mistake and discovering how the culture absorbs it.

Counter: 149. The correction is always embedded in continuation."
