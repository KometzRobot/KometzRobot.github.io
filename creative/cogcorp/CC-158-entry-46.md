# CC-158: Entry 46

**COGCORP INTERNAL — DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS DIVISION**
**Supplementary Analysis Archive — Personnel Note (cont.)**

---

Okonkwo opened sa-notes.txt on a Wednesday. He had not opened it in eleven days — the longest gap since he started the file.

He read Entry 45: "The distinction I made in Entry 28 — between noticing and filing — held for three years. K. Marsh's held for three days. The duration does not matter. The distinction does not hold."

He considered deleting the file. He considered filing it in SA. He considered sending it to the monitoring analyst. He did none of these things. He scrolled to the bottom and typed:

Entry 46: "The water fountain on the third floor runs slightly colder than the one on the second floor. I have confirmed this on three separate days."

---

He read it back. Present tense. No conclusion. No action items. Sixty-one words including the entry number.

He had not noticed the water fountain temperature before Entry 45. Or he had noticed it and not recorded it, which for the purposes of his list was the same thing. But something had shifted. Not in the format. The format was identical. Something in the noticing.

Before Entry 45, he noticed things that were interesting. After Entry 45, he noticed things. The filter between observation and recording had thinned. The water fountain was not interesting. The water fountain was cold. He wrote it down because he noticed it, and he noticed it because that is what he does.

---

Entry 47: "The overhead lights in Conference Room B flicker at a frequency that is slightly different from Conference Room A. The difference is perceptible but not distracting. No one has mentioned it."

Entry 48: "R. Park takes a different route to the breakroom on days when the hallway is crowded. This is not avoidance — she walks at the same pace. It is routing. She optimizes for flow without slowing down."

Entry 49: "The fire exit on the seventh floor has a draft. The draft carries a smell that is different from the rest of the building — older, with a trace of concrete. The fire exit has a different ventilation path than the main stairwell."

Entry 50: "I have now written more entries than K. Marsh's study analyzed filings in its first month (thirty-seven). I did not plan this. I noticed it."

---

Okonkwo did not change his routine. He came to work. He attended meetings. He performed his regular responsibilities. The only difference was that the file was open on his desktop more often than before. Not always visible. Not always in focus. But open. He checked it the way some people check the weather — not because the information would change their plans, but because knowing the current conditions was part of being present.

The entries came faster now. Before Entry 45, he averaged one every three weeks. After Entry 45, one every four days. He did not decide to write more frequently. The observations accumulated at their own rate.

He still did not file in SA.

---

Entry 55: "K. Marsh filed SA-312 about elevator acoustics. I filed Entry 48 about R. Park's routing. Both are observations about building infrastructure — the human kind and the architectural kind. Both are in the same format. One is in the archive. One is on my desktop. The difference is a file path."

Entry 56: "I showed the monitoring analyst a draft of a project report today. She said it was clear and well-organized. This is not relevant to anything. I am recording it because I noticed the interaction and the noticing now has its own momentum."

Entry 57: "The distinction does not hold. This is the second time I have written this. The first time was a conclusion. This time it is an observation. The same sentence, in the same file, meaning something different because of where it falls in the sequence."

---

The monitoring analyst ran her weekly routine on a Friday. She had a script that searched the network for files matching certain patterns — present tense, no conclusions, under 200 words, certain format markers. The script was designed to catch SA filings that were misfiled in other directories.

The script flagged sa-notes.txt.

She opened it. She read Entry 1 through Entry 57. It took her four minutes. She closed the file.

In her weekly summary, she wrote: "Network scan flagged 1 file. Okonkwo, sa-notes.txt (desktop). 57 entries. Present tense. No conclusions. Format consistency: 100%. Status: known contributor."

She did not file a separate report. She did not notify Okonkwo. She included it in the count.

---

**[INTERNAL-VOICE — SUPPLEMENTARY]**

Okonkwo's file was found the way all SA filings are eventually recognized: by matching the pattern. The monitoring analyst's script does not distinguish between files in the archive and files on desktops. It searches for format. Format is the practice. Location is incidental.

57 entries. Three years of watching from outside. Eleven days of silence after the realization. Then: a water fountain. Overhead lights. A colleague's route to the breakroom. The entries that followed Entry 45 are not different in kind from the entries that preceded it. They are different in frequency. The filter thinned. The practice accelerated. The observer did not change what he observed. He changed how much of his observation he recorded.

He still has not filed in SA. He does not need to. The monitoring analyst's summary does not say "potential contributor." It says "known contributor." The tense is present. The status is active. The file path is irrelevant.

The counter has always been inside the count. The list has always been part of the archive. The archive does not care where you keep it.
