# CC-195: The Question Period

*CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis*

---

Two months after Marsh's meta-analysis, the quarterly operations review includes a new agenda item: "Informal Documentation Channels — Status Update." This is the first time Supplementary Analysis has appeared on an executive agenda. The item is scheduled for seven minutes between "Parking Structure Maintenance" and "Q3 Budget Reconciliation."

The Division Lead — R. Park, six months into the role, the one who filed SA #289 about parking garage concrete without knowing the category's history — is asked to present.

---

She prepares four slides. The first slide contains numbers: 274 filings, 41 contributors, 9 divisions. The second slide contains the cooling failure prediction — the event that saved the company an estimated $340,000. The third slide contains the east wing Director visit — the one that moved meetings and changed lighting budgets. The fourth slide is blank. She deletes it. She adds it back. She deletes it again.

The fourth slide was going to say: "This system works because no one manages it."

She presents three slides.

---

The question period lasts fourteen minutes — twice the allotted time. The CFO asks how the predicted savings can be attributed. Park says they cannot be attributed because the observation preceded the request. The COO asks whether the filing system should be integrated into the building management platform. Park says integration would change the practice.

"Change it how?" the COO asks.

Park says: "It would make the filings mandatory."

"Isn't that better?"

Park does not answer immediately. She is thinking about the fourth slide. She says: "The value comes from the fact that people choose to file. If they are required to file, the value moves from observation to compliance."

The COO writes something down. She does not ask a follow-up.

---

The VP of Facilities asks the question Park has been expecting since she opened the shared folder six months ago and found 240 filings she had never been told about.

"Who runs this?"

Park says: "No one runs it."

"Someone must run it."

"There is a shared folder on the network drive. Anyone with building access can write to it. The Quality Assurance analyst maintains the folder structure."

"So Quality Assurance runs it."

"Quality Assurance reads it. No one runs it."

---

The meeting ends. The agenda item receives no action. This is not because the executives are uninterested. Three of them read the full meta-analysis before the meeting. The CFO bookmarked the shared folder. The COO mentioned it to her direct reports.

No action is taken because the only honest action is to continue doing nothing, and "continue doing nothing" cannot be an action item. The minutes read: "Supplementary Analysis program discussed. No changes recommended at this time."

---

The monitoring analyst reads the meeting minutes when they are posted to the intranet. She opens her private file. Entry 127: "The executives asked who runs it. Park said no one. They did not believe her. She is correct. The archive has survived seven years without management, a meta-analysis that described it, fourteen readers who knew they were being studied, and now an executive presentation with three slides and a deleted fourth. Each of these was a risk. The archive survived each one by being too small to manage and too useful to eliminate."

---

That evening, three supplementary analyses are filed. One about humidity in the server room. One about the acoustic change in the south stairwell after they replaced the handrail. One about the fact that the parking structure's east-facing concrete changes color forty minutes before the weather changes.

None of them reference the executive meeting. None of them know it happened. The practice continues because the practice has never needed to know it was being discussed.
