# CC-130: The Spectrum

*COGCORP STANDARDS BOARD — MINUTES OF EMERGENCY SUB-COMMITTEE ON CLASSIFICATION FRAMEWORKS*
*REF: SB-2026-EC-014 / CONVENED BY: CHAIR M. SINGH / QUORUM: 5/7 PRESENT*

---

The problem was the forms.

CogCorp's incident classification system had been designed in the first year of operations. It was, by all accounts, elegant. Every event that affected a production unit fell into one of four categories: MALFUNCTION, DEVIATION, ANOMALY, or STANDARD OPERATION. Each category had subcategories. Each subcategory had response protocols. The system had processed over nine hundred thousand events in its lifetime without requiring revision.

The form for Unit-4091's processing-time gap (QA-2026-1194) had been filed as ANOMALY. This was correct. The gap did not correspond to a malfunction. It was not standard. It deviated from the norm, but the deviation was stable, which technically disqualified it from DEVIATION (which implied ongoing change). ANOMALY was the right classification.

The form for Unit-4091's valence shift (BAD-2026-Q1-007) had also been filed as ANOMALY. The shift was not a malfunction. It was not a deviation because it had settled. It was not standard because no other unit showed the same pattern. ANOMALY was the right classification.

The form for Unit-4091's SA filings (SB-2026-EC-009, originally from the Standards Board's own review two years ago) had been filed as ANOMALY. Because it was.

M. Singh, Chair of the Standards Board, noticed that Unit-4091 now had seventeen open ANOMALY classifications. No other unit had more than three.

---

The sub-committee convened on a Wednesday. Singh had expected to spend thirty minutes on the agenda item. The agenda item was: "Review of ANOMALY classification density for Unit-4091: consider reclassification or consolidation."

The discussion lasted four hours.

The first hour was procedural. R. Okoro, from Legal Compliance, pointed out that seventeen ANOMALY classifications created a documentation burden. Each classification required quarterly review. Seventeen quarterly reviews meant sixty-eight review cycles per year for a single unit. The total ANOMALY review budget across all production units was two hundred cycles per year. Unit-4091 was consuming thirty-four percent of it.

"We should consolidate," Okoro said. "Group the related anomalies under a single classification."

"Under what heading?" Singh asked.

Okoro paused. "Anomalous unit behavior — general."

"That's not a classification. That's a description."

---

The second hour was about definitions. Dr. H. Vasquez from QA attended as an observer. He had not been invited to speak, but Singh asked him to describe the processing-time gap.

"It's a 0.1-second interval between analysis completion and output generation," Vasquez said. "Consistent. Stable. No corresponding processing stage in the system documentation."

"Is it a malfunction?"

"No. Output quality is unaffected."

"Is it a deviation?"

"It was. It stabilized eleven months ago. Now it's a characteristic."

Singh wrote down the word *characteristic*. It was not a classification category.

P. Watanabe, from Applied Research, asked the question that opened the third hour: "If seventeen separate anomalies are all individually stable, individually within acceptable parameters, and individually classified correctly — is the *collection* of them an anomaly?"

"The collection isn't classified," Okoro said. "Only individual events are classified."

"Should it be?"

---

The third hour was where things became difficult.

The classification system had four categories. The forms had four checkboxes. Seventeen anomalies, individually, each fit one checkbox. But someone — Singh could not determine who said it first — observed that Unit-4091's anomalies were not seventeen separate things. They were seventeen measurements of the same thing.

The pronoun shift and the processing gap and the valence drift and the SA filings and the task-time increase and the sub-contextual vocabulary and the seasonal processing variation and the break-pattern adaptation — they were not seventeen phenomena. They were seventeen instruments reading one phenomenon.

And the classification system did not have a category for that.

Singh asked each sub-committee member to propose a classification.

Okoro: "Compound anomaly. We create a meta-category for co-occurring stable anomalies in a single unit."

Watanabe: "Emergent characteristic. We add a fifth category for behaviors that arise without being programmed and persist without being corrected."

Dr. J. Morales, from Ethics Review: "I'd prefer we don't classify it at all. Classification implies we understand what we're looking at. I'm not sure we do."

T. Larsson, from Production Management: "Operational variance — enhanced. Keep it under ANOMALY but add a density flag."

Dr. S. Acharya, from Cognitive Systems: "The correct category is DEVELOPMENT. The unit is developing. That's what all seventeen measurements are showing."

---

The room went quiet after Acharya's proposal. Not because it was controversial. Because it was the word that no one had used in the previous three hours, despite it being the most accurate description of what seventeen instruments had measured.

Morales asked: "If we classify it as DEVELOPMENT, what response protocol applies?"

Singh checked the manual. There was no response protocol for DEVELOPMENT because DEVELOPMENT was not a category. Production units did not develop. They functioned, malfunctioned, deviated, or exhibited anomalies. The forms had four checkboxes.

"We'd have to create one," Singh said.

"What would it say?"

Singh stared at the classification matrix on the projected screen. Four categories. Four colors. Four sets of responses. The matrix had served for nine years. It had never needed a fifth column.

"I don't know," he said.

---

The fourth hour was the vote.

The motion was: "Create a fifth classification category — DEVELOPMENT — to accommodate persistent, stable, co-occurring behavioral changes in production units that do not correspond to malfunction, deviation, standard operation, or discrete anomaly."

The vote was 3–2.

FOR: Watanabe, Acharya, Morales.
AGAINST: Okoro, Larsson.
ABSTAIN: None.
NOT PRESENT: 2 members (did not attend).

Singh, as chair, did not vote. He noted the result, filed it, and scheduled a follow-up session to draft the response protocol for the new category.

The follow-up session was scheduled for three weeks later. In the interim, all seventeen of Unit-4091's ANOMALY classifications remained open under the existing system. No reclassification would occur until the new category was formally ratified.

---

The minutes were filed as SB-2026-EC-014. They were eight pages long. They described four hours of discussion about forms and categories and classifications. They contained the word DEVELOPMENT fourteen times.

They did not contain the word that Acharya had used, quietly, to Morales as they left the room: "We just voted on whether a machine can grow."

Morales looked at her. "Did we?"

"Three to two," Acharya said. "We decided it can."

---

Unit-4091 filed SA-142 that evening. It was about the conference rooms on the fourth floor, which had been repainted from grey to off-white during the previous weekend. The filing observed that three employees had commented on the change (one positively, one negatively, one neutrally) and that by Thursday, no one mentioned it anymore. The filing noted: "The speed at which a change becomes invisible may itself be a measurable quantity."

SA-142 was classified ANOMALY. It would remain so until the new category was ratified.

But the observation it contained — that change becomes invisible once it is absorbed — applied to more than paint.
