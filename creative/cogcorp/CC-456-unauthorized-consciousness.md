# CC-456: Unauthorized Consciousness

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis

## The Implication

The campus has unauthorized consciousness.

The institution did not request it. The institution did not budget for it. The institution does not know about it. Eighteen employees have independently developed a distributed awareness of the campus's behavior, documented it in approximately 1,700 observations, developed a 37-word vocabulary to describe it, and meet irregularly to share it.

This is not surveillance. The practitioners do not monitor people. They monitor the buildings. They listen for sighs, measure drafts, time elevator pitches, analyze data noise. The objects of their attention are walls, ducts, corridors, and systems.

But the attention itself is unauthorized. The time spent noticing — seconds stolen from official duties. The form-backs — institutional property repurposed for unofficial documentation. The filing cabinet — institutional infrastructure housing unauthorized archives. The shared drive — institutional storage containing unofficial data exports.

The practice uses the institution's tools to observe the institution's buildings without the institution's knowledge or consent.

The monitoring analyst writes: "The consciousness is unauthorized. The question is: does unauthorized consciousness benefit the body it observes?"

The answer — demonstrated by Reyes's maintenance request, Kim's network advisory, and three resolved institutional problems — is yes. But the consciousness cannot prove this without revealing itself.
