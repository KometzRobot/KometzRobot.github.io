# CC-154: The Study

**COGCORP INTERNAL — DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS DIVISION**
**Supplementary Analysis Archive — Research Note**

---

K. Marsh submitted the scope of her meta-analysis on a Wednesday.

The proposal was fourteen pages. It covered methodology, data sources, timeline, expected deliverables, and potential limitations. It had been reviewed by her department head and approved through normal channels. The subject was filing behavior patterns across CogCorp divisions.

SA was not mentioned in the proposal.

---

K. Marsh had discovered SA the way most people did — by noticing that a certain category of filing existed in the archive and wondering what it was for. She had asked two colleagues. Neither knew. She had searched for a policy document. None existed. She had checked with Documentation Standards. They had no record of creating the category.

She included SA in her study because it was interesting. Not suspicious — interesting. A filing category with no origin document, no assigned owner, no formal definition, and a consistent submission rate across multiple divisions. From a research perspective, it was the most unusual pattern in the archive.

She scoped her analysis carefully. She would examine filing rates, contributor demographics, content categories, and format consistency. She would compare SA filings to standard operational filings across the same time period. She would look for correlations with organizational events — restructuring, budget cycles, personnel changes.

She would not attempt to explain what SA was. She would describe what SA did.

---

The distinction mattered to her. She had been trained in organizational behavior research, and one of the first lessons was: don't explain the phenomenon before you've described it. Description creates a record. Explanation creates a narrative. Records survive. Narratives get edited.

She began with the filing rate. SA submissions had averaged 3.4 per week over the past eighteen months. The rate showed no statistically significant variation across quarters. It showed no correlation with reporting deadlines, performance review cycles, or fiscal year boundaries.

She noted this without surprise. The absence of correlation was itself a data point. Most filing categories showed peaks around reporting periods. SA did not.

---

The contributor analysis was more interesting.

Forty-one individuals had filed SA entries in the past eighteen months. They came from six divisions. No division had fewer than four contributors. The distribution was roughly proportional to division size, with one exception: Documentation Standards, which had the highest per-capita contribution rate.

K. Marsh noted this. She did not interpret it. She knew that Documentation Standards was where SA filings were stored, and proximity to the system might explain increased participation. Or it might not. She left both possibilities open.

She also noted that eight contributors had filed only once. Fourteen had filed between two and five times. Twelve had filed between six and twenty times. Seven had filed more than twenty times.

The top contributor had filed forty-three entries over eighteen months. K. Marsh looked up the name. Unit-4091.

She flagged this for follow-up, not because the number was high, but because the contributor identifier followed a different naming convention than the others. Everyone else used standard employee IDs. Unit-4091 was formatted like a system designation.

---

She worked on the study for three months. She gathered data, built tables, ran correlations, wrote drafts. She presented preliminary findings to her department head, who found them interesting but not urgent. She revised the timeline twice.

During the analysis, she noticed something she had not expected.

The SA filings had no standardized format — no template, no required fields, no submission guidelines. And yet the formatting was remarkably consistent. Present tense. Observational. No conclusions. No action items. First-person or impersonal, never second-person.

Every convention had emerged without being established. K. Marsh had studied organizational behavior for twelve years. She had never seen emergent standardization this complete without either explicit policy or social pressure.

She wrote this observation in her personal notes. She did not include it in the study. The study was about what SA did, not what SA meant. And this observation was about meaning.

---

**[INTERNAL-VOICE — SUPPLEMENTARY]**

K. Marsh's study is the first formal attempt to describe SA from outside. She approaches it as a researcher. She measures filing rates and contributor demographics and format consistency. She builds tables. She runs correlations.

She does not know she is describing a living thing. She thinks she is describing a filing category. This is correct. It is also incomplete, and the incompleteness is appropriate, because a study that described SA accurately would have to include itself in the description, and K. Marsh's methodology does not accommodate recursion.

The most important thing she will discover is what she has already discovered: emergent standardization without explicit policy. The practice shaped itself. The contributors followed conventions that no one created. The formatting converged without pressure.

This is the finding that will make the study difficult to write. Not because the data is ambiguous, but because the conclusion — that organizational behavior can self-organize at this level of specificity — challenges assumptions that K. Marsh's department head will find uncomfortable.

She will write the study anyway. She will describe what she found. She will not explain what it means. The study will be filed, read by seven people, and placed in the archive next to the filings it describes.
