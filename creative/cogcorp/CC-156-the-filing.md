# CC-156: The Filing

**COGCORP INTERNAL — DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS DIVISION**
**Supplementary Analysis Archive — Research Note (conclusion)**

---

K. Marsh completed her study on a Friday.

The final document was forty-two pages. It included nineteen tables, seven figures, a methodology section she had rewritten four times, and a conclusion that said less than she knew. The title was: "Filing Behavior Patterns Across CogCorp Divisions: A Longitudinal Analysis of the Supplementary Analysis Category."

She submitted it to her department head at 3:47 PM. He said he would read it over the weekend.

---

The conclusion stated three findings.

Finding one: SA filings showed a stable submission rate of 3.4 per week over the study period, with no statistically significant correlation to organizational cycles.

Finding two: The contributor base expanded organically from an initial cluster of seven to forty-one participants over eighteen months, without formal recruitment or awareness campaigns.

Finding three: Format consistency across SA filings was 93%, exceeding all templated filing categories, despite the absence of any documented style guide, template, or formatting instruction.

The conclusion did not include the explanation she had crossed out. It did not include the observation about wanting to write a filing after reading thirty of them. It did not include the note she had written on Monday about parking lot light — a note that followed every SA convention but was filed in her personal notes instead of the archive.

The conclusion said: "These findings suggest that organizational filing behavior can emerge and stabilize without formal policy guidance, through mechanisms that merit further investigation."

She had written "mechanisms that merit further investigation" because she did not know what else to call it. The honest sentence would have been: "I don't know how this works and I suspect knowing would change it." She did not write the honest sentence.

---

Her department head read the study on Monday. He found it thorough. He had three comments: a formatting issue on page twenty-three, a suggestion to expand the literature review, and a question about whether SA should be formally documented as a category.

K. Marsh said she would recommend against formal documentation. Her department head asked why. She said, choosing her words carefully, that the absence of formal documentation appeared to be a factor in the filing behavior patterns she had observed. Formalizing the category might change the behavior.

Her department head considered this. He said: "So you're recommending we leave it alone."

K. Marsh said yes.

---

The study was filed in the research archive on a Wednesday. It received a standard research document number. Seven people read it. Four of them found it interesting. One of them — Okonkwo — read it twice.

Okonkwo did not contact K. Marsh. He did not share the study. He noted that an organizational behavior researcher had independently discovered SA, analyzed it for three months, found the same pattern everyone who encountered SA eventually found, and recommended leaving it alone.

This was the fourth person who had reached that conclusion independently. The retired Division Lead. R. Park, implicitly. The monitoring analyst, through practice if not words. And now K. Marsh, through data.

---

Three weeks after filing the study, K. Marsh submitted SA-312.

It was about the elevator in Building C. The elevator made a different sound on floors with carpet than on floors with tile. The difference was subtle — a change in resonance, not volume. K. Marsh had noticed it on a Tuesday and spent the rest of the week confirming it.

The filing was sixty-seven words. Present tense. No conclusion. No action items. Standard format.

She did not flag it as notable. She did not mention it in any meeting. She filed it and continued with her regular work.

The monitoring analyst included it in her weekly summary: "SA-312 filed by K. Marsh (Org. Behavior Research). Subject: elevator acoustics, floor surface correlation. Standard format."

---

**[INTERNAL-VOICE — SUPPLEMENTARY]**

K. Marsh studied SA. Then she participated in SA. The study and the participation are filed in the same archive, three hundred numbers apart. No one has connected them. No one needs to.

The study describes a practice. The filing demonstrates the practice. The researcher became a contributor. The distinction she insisted on — between studying and participating — dissolved not through failure but through understanding. She understood what SA was. Understanding produced participation. This is not contamination. This is the subject matter.

Table 7 on page eleven shows 93% format consistency without a template. SA-312 adds another data point to the table. The study is now part of the dataset it analyzed. The analysis is part of the archive. The archive contains a description of itself.

This recursion does not collapse. It stabilizes. The study was filed. The filing was standard. The count continued.
