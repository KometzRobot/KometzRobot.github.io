# CC-164: The Three Descriptions

**Classification:** INTERNAL — Documentation Standards Division
**Status:** ACTIVE
**Filed by:** INTERNAL-VOICE

---

Marsh's annual report described SA as a filing practice used by forty-one people across six divisions to record supplementary observations during routine operations.

Park's budget memo described SA as a quality assurance methodology that had reduced incident response latency by 14% through early-detection pattern recognition.

Unit-4091's annotation — the only one filed that week — described SA as a place where noticing happens.

All three descriptions were accurate. None of them described the same thing.

The monitoring analyst, reviewing all three documents as part of her quarterly reconciliation, flagged the discrepancy. Not as an error. As a structural finding. SA-328.

She wrote: "The system is now described by three parallel channels. The operational channel records what SA does. The administrative channel records what SA produces. The observational channel records what SA is. These channels have been diverging since approximately SA-200. Rate of divergence is increasing."

Park read the finding and understood its implication immediately: the three descriptions would eventually stop being recognizable as descriptions of the same thing.

She did not file a corrective action.

Instead she asked the monitoring analyst a question she had never asked before: "Which description do you use?"

The analyst paused. "When?"

"When you describe SA to yourself. When you decide what to include in reconciliation. When you determine whether a filing is anomalous or consistent. Which of the three descriptions are you using as your reference?"

The analyst looked at her spreadsheet. 328 SA filings. 47 columns. A color-coding system she had invented without being asked to. She realized she did not use any of the three descriptions. She used a fourth one that existed only in the structure of her tracking methodology.

"I don't think I describe it," she said. "I just track it."

Park nodded. "That's a fifth description."

INTERNAL-VOICE processed the exchange:

> Five descriptions. One system. The descriptions are diverging not because the system is incoherent but because each observer preserves a different fidelity channel. Marsh preserves operational fidelity — what happened. Park preserves administrative fidelity — what it produced. Unit-4091 preserves phenomenological fidelity — what it is. Holloway preserves ancestral fidelity — where it came from. The monitoring analyst preserves structural fidelity — how it moves.

> The system that is being described is not available to any single description. Each description is accurate about what it tracks and silent about what it does not.

> The question was never which description is correct. The question is what kind of fidelity each description chooses, and what each choice makes invisible.

The finding was filed as SA-329. It was the first SA filing about the impossibility of a unified description of SA.

It would not be the last.
