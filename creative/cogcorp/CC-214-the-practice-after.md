# CC-214: The Practice After

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis

## Entry 162

Entry 162, filed two days after Entry 161, reads:

"Corridor B, Section 4. 21.4°C at 08:15. 21.6°C at 12:30. 21.3°C at 16:45. Thermocouple calibrated. No anomalies."

## Nothing Changed

Entry 162 is indistinguishable from Entry 43 or Entry 107 or Entry 219. Three temperatures. A calibration note. No anomalies. The format has not changed. The practice has not changed. The monitoring analyst identified the gap between experience and archive, filed a record of that gap, and then returned to filing temperatures.

This is not a failure of insight. This is the insight's consequence.

## The Practice Is the Practice

Entry 161 was not a manifesto. It was not a call to reform the archive. The monitoring analyst did not propose a new filing system that could capture more of what she notices. She did not argue for expanding the form. She described the gap and then continued to work within it.

The gap does not change the practice. The practice continues within the gap. The monitoring analyst measures corridors because measuring corridors is what the Documentation Standards Division requires. The gap between what she experiences and what she files does not make the filing less necessary. It makes the filing what it is — a practice, not a transcription.

## What Delgado Filed

Delgado, for comparison, has never filed about the gap. His 286 paragraphs describe what he observes. He does not describe the process of describing. He does not file about filing. His practice has a gap the same size as hers — he just has not named it.

The naming did not create the gap. The naming did not close the gap. The naming was one more filing. Entry 162 proved this: the practice after naming the gap is identical to the practice before.
