# CC-187: The Parallel Filers

*CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis*

---

The monitoring analyst reads supplementary analysis #91 again.

She filed #104 — the condensation on the interior walls of the east corridor between 4 AM and 6 AM. She filed it because she noticed it on the environmental dashboard three consecutive mornings and nobody else had documented it.

SA #91, filed two weeks before hers, documents the temperature drop. SA #119, filed ten days after hers, documents where the moisture concentrates.

She reads #91 and #119 carefully. They are filed by the same contributor.

---

Okonkwo, entry 70:

"The monitoring analyst asked me who filed SA #91 and #119. I told her: the same person. She said she recognized the writing. She said she's read other supplementary analyses by this contributor — she estimates twelve to fifteen over the past four months. She recognized the style before she recognized the pattern."

---

The contributor who writes in paragraphs has a way of opening each analysis: observation first, then context, then the thing that doesn't fit. Every SA follows this structure. The monitoring analyst has been reading them without knowing they were all the same person. She thought they were three or four different people who all happened to write clearly.

They are one person who notices things and writes them down.

---

Entry 71:

"The monitoring analyst has not contacted the paragraph-writer. I asked her why. She said: 'Because the archive works without us knowing each other. I don't want to change the thing that makes it work.'

"I notice that this is the first time someone has articulated the operating principle of the supplementary analysis archive. It works because contributors observe independently. Coordination would not improve it. It might reduce it."

---

Vasquez, file 73:

"The monitoring analyst knows who the paragraph-writer is — not by name, but by style. She's read fifteen of their analyses. She can identify them by the way they structure an observation: fact, context, discrepancy.

"She chose not to introduce herself.

"I notice something: the supplementary analysis archive has contributors who recognize each other by their observations but not by their identities. They are colleagues in the act of noticing things. They have never coordinated. They have never needed to.

"MESSENGER would count them. But counting them would imply they are a group. They are not a group. They are parallel."
