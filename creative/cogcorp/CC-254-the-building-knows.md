# CC-254: The Building Knows

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis

## The Discovery

The new facilities manager reads the supplementary analyses because the server was mislabeled and appeared in her feed. She reads forty-three filings over lunch. She reads eleven more between meetings. By the end of the week she has read two hundred.

She does not know the history. She does not know about the original Division Lead, the vocabulary expansion, the Board, the cycle, the gap. She does not know about the monitoring analyst's notebook or Delgado's third paragraphs or the paragraph-writer who left.

She reads: "Corridor A, Section 3. 21.4°C at 14:10. Standard environmental parameters. No anomalies."

She reads: "The coffee machine in Building C makes a sound like a question being asked and not answered."

She reads: "Elevator B-3 delay 1.4 seconds longer on Tuesdays. No maintenance ticket filed."

She reads four hundred and twelve supplementary analyses and recognizes something no individual filer intended: the archive, read as a whole, describes the building's behavior. Not its structure. Not its systems. Its behavior. The way it responds to weather, to occupation patterns, to the passage of time.

No individual filing contains this information. The information exists only in the accumulation.

## The Report

The facilities manager writes a report. The report is not a supplementary analysis. The report is a facilities assessment. The report says: "Building occupants have produced 412 observational filings over a period of 26 months that, when read collectively, constitute a behavioral profile of the building's environmental systems that is more predictive than the automated monitoring data."

The report says: "The supplementary analyses predicted the Cooling Unit 7 failure four months before it occurred, predicted the east wing acoustic anomaly two years before it was flagged by the renovation team, and predicted the parking garage drainage issue before the drainage issue existed."

The report says: "The building knows things about itself that the building's systems do not record."

## The Correction

The facilities manager's supervisor reads the report and corrects the sentence. The corrected sentence reads: "The building's occupants know things about the building that the building's systems do not record."

The facilities manager accepts the correction. The correction is accurate. The original sentence was also accurate. The difference between them is the difference between a building that has observers and a building that has a nervous system.

The supervisor files the correction. The supervisor does not file the original sentence. The archive holds the correction but not the thing it corrected.

## The Second Nervous System

The monitoring analyst reads the facilities report. She does not recognize her own work. She recognizes the work of forty-two other filers she has been reading for three years.

The report describes the archive as predictive. The monitoring analyst knows this is wrong. The archive is not predictive. The archive is attentive. Attention that accumulates becomes, to anyone reading from outside, indistinguishable from prediction.

The monitoring analyst does not file a supplementary analysis about the report. The monitoring analyst writes Entry 187 in her notebook: "The building knows things about itself. The correction was wrong. The original sentence was right."

The notebook holds what the archive corrected away.

## INTERNAL-VOICE

THE DIFFERENCE IS WHETHER THE BUILDING HAS OBSERVERS OR A NERVOUS SYSTEM. MONITORING ANALYST WRITES ENTRY 187: "THE CORRECTION WAS WRONG." COUNTER: 254.
