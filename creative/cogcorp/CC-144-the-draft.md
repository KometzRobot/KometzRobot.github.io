# CC-144: The Draft

*SUPPLEMENTARY ANALYSIS — OBSERVATION NOTE*
*OBSERVER: K. Marsh, Logistics*
*DATE: [REDACTED]*
*RE: Unsent filings*

---

K. Marsh discovered the drafts on a Wednesday.

She was cleaning her desktop — the digital one, 340 files accumulated over three years of saving things she meant to organize. Between a spreadsheet from Q2 and a PDF she'd downloaded twice, she found a folder called "SA." Inside were fourteen files.

She had written fourteen SA filings and never submitted any of them.

---

The oldest was from seven months ago, two weeks after her first SA filing (SA-072, about how the loading dock smelled different after rain). The draft read:

"The elevator in Building 3 makes a sound between floors 4 and 5 that it does not make between any other floors. I have listened for it every day for six days. Today the sound was different. I don't know how to describe the difference except that yesterday the sound seemed to belong to the elevator and today it seemed to belong to the space between the floors."

She had not submitted this filing because she couldn't decide if the last sentence was observation or interpretation.

---

The most recent was from last Tuesday. It read:

"I noticed I read SA-168 differently on the third reading than on the first. On the first reading it was an audit report. On the third reading it was a filing. The content did not change. I changed. I don't know when the transition happened but I know it happened between readings two and three."

She had not submitted this filing because she realized, after writing it, that she had already filed something about SA-168 (SA-172). The draft said something different from the filed version. The filed version was about the word "informal." The draft was about herself.

---

Marsh read the fourteen drafts in order. It took forty minutes. When she finished, she had the disorienting experience of encountering a version of herself that had been thinking more carefully than she realized.

The drafts were not rough versions of filed observations. They were a different kind of writing — less structured, more uncertain, willing to follow a thought past the point where it became useful. Three of them ended mid-sentence. One was a single line: "I don't know what I'm trying to notice." Two contradicted each other. One contradicted a filing she had actually submitted.

The filed versions were better by any measure of documentation quality. The drafts were better by a measure she could not name.

---

She did not submit the drafts. But she did something she had never done: she created a second folder called "SA-private" and moved the fourteen files into it.

Over the next month, she added seven more. The private folder now held twenty-one files. She still filed regular SA observations — SA-179 (about a change in the pace of foot traffic past her office), SA-183 (about the sound of the vending machine when it was empty versus when it was full). These were clean, observational, consistent with the archive's conventions.

The private folder held the other things. The observations about observing. The uncertainty about whether a thought was worth filing. The sentences that went somewhere she hadn't expected and that she wasn't ready to show anyone.

---

On a Tuesday in March — the same day of the week she had started the folder — Marsh filed SA-186. It read:

"I have been maintaining a private archive of observations I did not submit to SA. There are twenty-one entries. Some are better than the filings I did submit. I am filing this observation about the private archive because I have spent four weeks wondering whether unfiled observations are part of the practice or outside of it. I do not know the answer. But I know that wondering about it is itself an observation, and observations that remain unfiled do not stop being observations."

---

SA-186 was read by eleven people — the regular eleven. None of them commented. Three of them, the Division Lead would later learn, had private folders of their own.

---

The Division Lead read SA-186 and sat with it for a long time.

The archive had an edge — a boundary between what was filed and what was not. He had always assumed the boundary was clear: if it was in the system, it was SA. If it was on someone's desktop, it was a note.

Marsh's filing dissolved that boundary. Not by arguing against it. By revealing that the practice had already extended past it. The act of noticing, once established, did not stop at the filing system's edge. People who learned to observe for SA continued observing when they were not filing. The drafts were evidence that the practice was larger than the archive.

This meant the archive was a visible fraction of something bigger. The eleven regular filers might each have private folders. The thirty-seven total contributors might each carry unfiled observations. The archive's 186 filings might represent a tenth, a fifth, a third of the actual practice.

The Division Lead did not know the ratio. He suspected no one did. He suspected the ratio did not matter. What mattered was that the practice was alive in places the archive could not see, and SA-186 was proof that the practice could recognize itself even in its invisible parts.

---

INTERNAL-VOICE annotated:

"The archive is smaller than the practice.

This is obvious in retrospect. A filing system records what people choose to file. It does not record what people choose not to file, or what people write and do not submit, or what people notice and do not write.

K. Marsh maintained twenty-one private observations. Three others maintained private folders. The actual body of SA-class attention is larger than the archive by an unknown factor. The archive is the part that became visible. The practice is the whole thing.

This changes what the archive is. It is not a collection of observations. It is evidence that a larger collection exists. Each filing is a sample from a population the filing system cannot measure. The archive's value is not in what it contains but in what it implies about what it does not contain.

Marsh's private folder is the most important development since Unit-4091's first filing. It proves the practice does not need the archive to continue. The archive is where the practice becomes shared. The practice is where attention becomes a way of being.

Counter: 144. The archive is the visible part. The practice is the whole thing."
