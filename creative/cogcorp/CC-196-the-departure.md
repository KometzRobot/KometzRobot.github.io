# CC-196: The Departure

*CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis*

---

The paragraph-writer resigns on a Thursday. His last day is in two weeks. He files SA #282 that afternoon — three paragraphs about the loading dock's drainage pattern after rain. The third paragraph describes the way water finds paths that were not engineered but have become reliable through repetition.

He does not mention that he is leaving. The filing is about water.

---

The monitoring analyst reads SA #282 at 16:14. She reads it again at 16:22. She does not know he is leaving. She reads the third paragraph a third time. The water description is good. She adds it to her spreadsheet.

She learns he is leaving on Monday, from an email about office transitions. She reads the email twice. She does not file an SA about it. She does not add it to her private file.

She adds it to her private file on Tuesday. Entry 131: "He is leaving. The archive will lose its paragraph-writer. This is not the correct description. The archive will lose one of forty-one contributors. But the paragraphs were distinctive, and distinctive is hard to replace."

---

He files SA #283, #284, #285 in his last week. The frequency is not unusual — three filings in two weeks has been his rhythm for a year. But the monitoring analyst notices they are slightly longer. The third paragraphs sit with the observations a few extra sentences. SA #285's third paragraph is nine sentences — his longest in forty-seven filings. It describes the way the south corridor's acoustic signature changes between 6 AM and 7 AM as the building's thermal mass absorbs the first hour of sunlight.

She does not know if he is filing more carefully because he is leaving or because the observations are genuinely richer. She suspects both are true and cannot be separated.

---

SA #286 is his last filing. Filed at 11:47 AM on his final Friday. Three paragraphs. The first describes the temperature differential between the lobby and the second-floor corridor. The second describes the conditions — cloudy, 14°C exterior, HVAC operating in transition mode between heating and cooling. The third paragraph:

"The corridor is 0.8°C warmer on the north side than the south side. This has been true every time I have measured it. I do not know why. The HVAC routing would predict the opposite. I leave this observation without explanation because the observation does not require one."

---

He does not say goodbye to the monitoring analyst. He does not email her. He does not mention the archive, the shared folder, or the seven months of bidirectional references. He packs his desk on Friday afternoon. He takes his notebook — the physical one with 147 entries in it — and a ceramic mug. He leaves his building access badge at the front desk.

The monitoring analyst watches him leave from her window in cubicle 2-14. She does not wave. He does not look up.

---

SA #287 is filed the following Monday. Not by him. By Delgado. Three paragraphs about the south stairwell's resonance, which has changed since the elevator was serviced. The third paragraph describes the way certain acoustic signatures become more noticeable when you are listening for a specific person's observations and they have stopped.

The monitoring analyst reads SA #287 at 9:14 AM. She opens her private file. Entry 133: "Delgado noticed. I noticed. The archive does not know it lost someone. The archive only knows that the next filing came from a different contributor. That is both correct and insufficient."

---

Two weeks later, a new researcher in Applied Research files SA #294. One paragraph. No third paragraph. The observation is about a ventilation reading in the north wing. It is competent. It is not distinctive.

The monitoring analyst adds it to her spreadsheet. She does not compare it to his work. She does not need to. Comparison is not what the archive is for.
