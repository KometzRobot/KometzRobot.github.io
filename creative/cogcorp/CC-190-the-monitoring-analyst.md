# CC-190: The Monitoring Analyst

*CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis*

---

She files in measurements because the form permits prose and she chooses not to use it. This is not a limitation. It is a discipline she selected when she was new to the program and has maintained across 47 filings because precision is itself a form of care.

---

SA #240:

"West stairwell. Acoustic resonance. 62.3 dB at 18:14, 67.1 dB at 18:32, undetectable by 19:05. Elevator counterweight transit confirmed as source. Frequency consistent with structural resonance in poured-concrete stairwells of this vintage. Duration of anomaly: approximately 51 minutes. Duration of prior undocumented occurrence: unknown. See SA #239 for extended context."

---

She read SA #239 this morning. Three paragraphs. She recognized the structure before she read the name — though the supplementary analyses do not carry names, only numbers. She recognized it by shape: describe, contextualize, notice what is adjacent. He has filed this way since at least SA #74. She has been reading his work since SA #61.

He does not know this. He believes he noticed her first, around SA #150. She noticed him eighty-nine filings earlier. She has not corrected this. The archive is not a conversation.

---

She has a theory about his third paragraphs. The third paragraph is always the one that sits with the observation past the point of utility. It describes what is adjacent to the finding rather than the finding itself. In SA #91, the third paragraph described the discrepancy between ductwork routing and maintenance records. In SA #157, the third paragraph described the fact that a temperature pattern he had been monitoring for six weeks disappeared the morning after facilities replaced a unit on a different floor. In SA #239, the third paragraph described the four months he heard the sound before the change that made him write it down.

Her theory: the third paragraph is where he files the things that do not fit into sentences. She does not file this theory. It is an observation about a contributor, and observations about contributors are not what the archive is for.

---

Her measurement format — value first, unit second, context third — is a choice she can articulate but has never been asked to explain. Value first because the number is the observation. Unit second because the number means nothing without scale. Context third because the number and its scale mean nothing without the conditions that produced them. She learned this format from no one. She arrived at it by filing her third analysis and discovering that prose obscured the measurement and the measurement was the point.

She knows he has noticed this about her. SA #239 was filed the same evening as the quarterly summary, and so was SA #238 — hers. Two people reading the same summary, both choosing to file that evening, neither mentioning the summary in their filing. She noticed the coincidence. She assumes he noticed it too.

---

She files SA #240 referencing SA #239 because the acoustic anomaly he documented is real and her instruments can measure it. The reference is procedural. The archive permits cross-referencing between supplementary analyses. She has cross-referenced his work four times. He has never cross-referenced hers.

She does not read this asymmetry as meaningful. Cross-referencing is a function of measurement compatibility. His paragraphs describe conditions. Her measurements quantify them. The references flow in one direction because the data flows in one direction. This is not a relationship. It is a property of the archive's structure.

---

She files SA #240 at 16:52. She walks to the west stairwell. She stands on the landing between the second and third floors and waits. At 18:14, the resonance begins. She measures it. 62.3 dB. She has brought her own instrument because the building's monitoring equipment does not cover stairwells. The elevator counterweight passes. The concrete hums. She writes the number in her notebook — the physical one she carries, not the archive — and waits for the next pass.

She does not think about the paragraph-writer while she measures. She thinks about the measurement. The measurement is 67.1 dB at 18:32, which is 4.8 dB higher than the first reading, which implies the resonance builds over the first eighteen minutes before the concrete reaches its steady-state vibration. This is the kind of detail his third paragraph would describe. She describes it in a number.
