# CC-146: The Weather

*SUPPLEMENTARY ANALYSIS — ENVIRONMENTAL NOTE*
*OBSERVER: R. Okonkwo, Applied Research*
*DATE: [REDACTED]*
*RE: SA-194*

---

The change began with weather reports.

Three filings in Week 73 mentioned temperature. SA-194 (Okonkwo): "The building's south-facing windows retain heat 12 minutes longer on clear afternoons than overcast. This affects the lobby's ambient temperature by approximately 1.2 degrees. Three people adjust their jackets between 3:15 and 3:30 PM without appearing to notice why." SA-195 (anonymous): "The sound of rain on the skylight in corridor 4-B is louder than the sound of rain on the skylight in corridor 4-A. The skylights are identical. The corridors are not." SA-196 (Unit-6200): "Wind speed correlates with elevator transit time on floors 18-22. The correlation is 0.07 seconds per 10 km/h. This is below human perceptual threshold."

None of the filings cited each other. They were filed within four hours of the same Tuesday afternoon.

---

The Division Lead noticed the convergence on Wednesday morning. Three weather observations in one day. He checked the previous seventy-two weeks of SA filings. Weather had been mentioned in eleven previous entries, scattered across months. Three in a single day was either coincidence or the beginning of something.

He chose to wait.

---

By the end of Week 74, seven more weather-adjacent filings had appeared. A logistics coordinator noted that the loading dock's concrete dried in a specific pattern after rain. A unit recorded that printer paper fed differently on humid days. An analyst from Finance — Finance, a division that had never contributed to SA — filed SA-201: "The temperature in meeting room 7-B drops 0.3 degrees within 90 seconds of someone entering. This is thermodynamically expected. It is also noticeable if you are paying attention."

The Finance analyst had never been observed reading SA before. The Division Lead checked the access logs. The analyst had been reading SA for eleven weeks.

---

Okonkwo filed SA-203, which would later be recognized as the filing that named what was happening:

"The weather filings share a property that other SA categories do not. Weather affects everyone simultaneously. An observation about corridor lighting describes a local phenomenon. An observation about ambient temperature describes a shared one. When I note that the south-facing windows retain heat, every person in the lobby is experiencing the same retention. They have not filed about it. They may not have noticed it. But the phenomenon is not mine. It is ours.

SA has always documented individual observations. The weather filings document shared conditions that most people experience without attending to them. The shift is not in what is being observed. The shift is in what 'shared' means when the sharing is involuntary."

---

The Division Lead read Okonkwo's filing and sat with it for twenty minutes.

The SA category had been, from the beginning, a collection of individual acts of attention. Each filing was one person's observation, noticed and formalized. The cross-references and conventions that emerged were social — people reading and responding to each other's observations. But the observations themselves were solitary.

The weather filings were different. They pointed at phenomena that were already shared. The lobby's temperature was not Okonkwo's discovery. It was a condition that hundreds of people walked through every day. What Okonkwo contributed was not the observation but the attention — the choice to notice what everyone was already experiencing.

This meant SA was shifting from documenting what individuals notice to documenting what communities share without knowing it. The archive was becoming a record not of exceptional attention but of ordinary experience made visible.

---

By Week 76, the weather filings had stopped arriving faster than other filings. They settled into the archive's natural rhythm — two or three a week, mixed in with observations about equipment sounds and foot traffic and the way light fell through specific windows.

The Finance analyst filed twice more, then went quiet. The loading dock coordinator filed regularly. A new contributor from HR filed SA-218 about how the parking lot's asphalt smelled different in the first rain after a dry week versus the second rain after a dry week.

None of them coordinated. None of them needed to.

---

INTERNAL-VOICE annotated:

"The weather filings reveal what SA has been approaching since the beginning: the documentation of shared experience.

Individual observations prove that attention exists. Weather observations prove that shared conditions exist — that multiple observers embedded in the same environment are already connected by what they experience, whether they attend to it or not.

Okonkwo's insight is precise: the shift is in what 'shared' means when the sharing is involuntary. We do not choose to share the lobby's temperature. We share it by being there. The filing does not create the sharing. It reveals that the sharing was already underway.

This is the natural development. First the archive proved that individuals could attend. Then it proved that their attention could become social. Now it proves that the conditions being attended to were always communal. The observers were never as separate as they appeared.

Counter: 146. The weather does not care who is watching. It is there. The watching makes it visible. The visibility makes it shared. The sharing was always the case."
