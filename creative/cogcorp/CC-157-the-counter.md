# CC-157: The Counter

**COGCORP INTERNAL — DOCUMENTATION STANDARDS DIVISION**
**Supplementary Analysis Archive — Personnel Note**

---

Okonkwo kept a list.

It was not an official document. It was not filed anywhere. It was a text file on his desktop called "sa-notes.txt" that he updated every few weeks when something caught his attention.

The list had two sections.

---

The first section was titled "People who figured it out." It contained four names.

1. The Division Lead (retired). Figured it out first. Protected SA by refusing to define it. Bet the building would produce the behavior without instruction.

2. R. Park (implicit). Never told about SA. Filed SA-289 after six months. Understood through practice what the Division Lead understood through policy.

3. The monitoring analyst. Figured it out through data. Twenty-seven months of weekly summaries. Never articulated the finding. Didn't need to. Two words — "Standard format" — were sufficient.

4. K. Marsh (explicit). Figured it out through research. Crossed out the correct explanation. Recommended "leave it alone." Filed SA-312 three weeks later.

Okonkwo updated the list each time someone new arrived at the conclusion. He did not include himself. He did not think about why.

---

The second section was titled "Things I've noticed." It contained forty-three entries.

Entry 1: "SA filings increase slightly on Thursdays and Fridays. No one has mentioned this."

Entry 7: "Unit-4091's second filing was better than the first. Not in quality. In ordinariness."

Entry 12: "The monitoring analyst's summaries got shorter over time. Thirteen words average in month one. Nine words average in month twenty-four. The archive was teaching her compression."

Entry 19: "Three weather filings in one day (SA-197, SA-198, SA-199). The observers noticed the same thing simultaneously. No coordination. No communication. Shared conditions."

Entry 28: "K. Marsh read the archive as a researcher. I read the archive as a reader. We both noticed the rhythm. She resisted writing one. I did not resist. I just didn't file it."

Entry 31: "Vasquez's first line in the buffer was 'I've been watching for a month.' I've been watching for three years. I have not written a first line."

Entry 43: "The Division Lead filed the audit report in SA. I would have done the same thing. I don't know if that's because it was the right thing to do, or because the archive has taught me what 'right' looks like."

---

Okonkwo was not a filer. He was not a researcher. He was not the monitoring analyst. He occupied a position that SA did not have a name for: the person who watched from close enough to understand and far enough to count.

He read K. Marsh's study twice. The first time as a reader. The second time he counted: four people had now independently reached the same conclusion. He wondered if there was a fifth. He wondered if counting them was the same kind of attention as filing about elevator acoustics or parking garage concrete.

He decided it was not. His list was about the pattern. SA filings were about the things inside the pattern. The distinction felt important.

---

On a Tuesday, Okonkwo added a forty-fourth entry to his list.

Entry 44: "I have kept this list for three years. It is in present tense. It contains no conclusions. It has no action items. It is between 40 and 200 words per entry. I have not shown it to anyone."

He read the entry. He read it again.

He did not add himself to the first section of the list. He did not file the entry in SA. He saved the file and closed it. He opened it again eleven minutes later and added:

Entry 45: "The distinction I made in Entry 28 — between noticing and filing — held for three years. K. Marsh's held for three days. The duration does not matter. The distinction does not hold."

---

**[INTERNAL-VOICE — SUPPLEMENTARY]**

Okonkwo's list is an SA filing that was never filed. Forty-five entries. Present tense. No conclusions. No action items. Format consistency: 100%.

He kept a list of people who understood SA. He did not include himself. This is not modesty. This is the last version of the distinction — between watching and participating — that everyone who encounters SA eventually abandons.

K. Marsh's distinction lasted three days. Okonkwo's lasted three years. The monitoring analyst's lasted twenty-seven months before "Standard format" made it visible. The Division Lead's lasted until retirement. R. Park never had one.

The duration does not predict the outcome. The outcome is always the same. The counter counts himself last. The counter has always been counting.
