# CC-446: Attention Is Not Nothing

CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis

## On Cost

The practice costs attention.

Attention costs time. Every observation written on the back of a form takes seconds from the practitioner's day. Across eighteen practitioners, across two years, across approximately 1,600 documented observations: thousands of hours of attention.

Attention costs cognitive space. The practitioner who listens for the building sigh at 6:14 AM is not fully present for the morning email. The practitioner who measures the elevator pitch change is not fully focused on the quarterly report.

Attention costs emotional energy. The practitioner who writes an observation and files it in a cabinet that no one may ever read is performing an act of faith. The practitioner who has been observing alone for nine years — like the maintenance technician — has sustained that faith for nearly a decade.

The institution values the practitioners' labor. It pays them for their official roles. It does not pay them for their attention to building behavior. It does not know about their attention to building behavior.

The practitioners donate their attention. Not because the institution deserves it. Not because the building asks for it. Because the observations are worth making.

"I know. That's why I kept writing." Martinez's answer is the practice's economics: the quality of the observation is the return on the investment of attention.
