# CC-183: The Correspondent

*CogCorp Documentation Standards Division — Supplementary Analysis*

---

His name is Delgado. Applied Research, Building C, third floor. He has worked at CogCorp for four years. His role involves testing environmental sensor calibration. He measures things for a living.

He measured the elevator on a Tuesday because he had read SA-407 on a Monday and spent the rest of the day unable to stop listening. The ascending sound was not 1.2 seconds longer. It was 1.4. He counted from the moment the doors began closing. She counted from the moment the button was pressed.

The difference was 0.2 seconds. The difference was the starting point.

---

He did not intend to send the email. He wrote it, read it, deleted it, rewrote it, and sent it before he could delete it again. The subject line was "Building C elevator." The body was thirty-one words. He had drafted sixty. He cut until there was nothing left to cut.

She replied in four hours. "Thank you. 1.4 is more accurate. I was measuring from the wrong starting point."

He did not expect her to agree. He expected silence, or a form response, or nothing. He had never responded to a supplementary analysis. He did not know anyone who had. He did not know that no one had.

---

The second email arrives three days later. From her.

Subject: "Stairwell B, floors 3-4."

Body: "The handrail in Stairwell B has a vibration at certain times of day. I think it's the HVAC but I'm not sure. You would know. You test environmental sensors."

It is the first time the monitoring analyst has asked anyone anything about an observation. It is the first time she has assumed someone else's expertise would improve her own measurement. It is not a filing. It is a question.

---

He checks the handrail on his lunch break. The vibration is real. It correlates with the HVAC cycle — every forty-two minutes, a six-second resonance travels through the metal. He had never noticed it. He passes Stairwell B twice daily. His hands have been on that handrail for four years.

He replies: "HVAC. 42-minute cycle. Starts at floor 2 and propagates upward. By floor 4 it's barely perceptible. You can feel it clearly on floor 3."

She replies: "I noticed it on floor 3. That's where I take the stairs."

Neither of them files anything.

---

Okonkwo notices the email traffic in his monthly audit of SA-adjacent communications. Two employees exchanging observations outside the filing system. He adds entry 61 to sa-notes.txt:

"The monitoring analyst has a correspondent. They exchange measurements informally. The measurements do not appear in the archive. The archive generated the relationship. The relationship has outgrown the archive. This is not a failure of documentation. This is what documentation is for."

---

The third email from Delgado is not about a measurement.

Subject: "Question."

Body: "Do you always notice things like this? The coffee machine, the elevator. I'm wondering because I test sensors all day and I didn't notice the elevator until you filed SA-407. I measure things for a living. You filed twenty-three words and I heard something I'd walked past for four years."

She takes two days to reply. The reply is eleven words.

"I don't always notice. I notice when I'm paying attention."

---

Vasquez, file 68:

"The monitoring analyst has a correspondent now. She sends him observations and he sends measurements back. This is not collaboration in any formal sense. No shared project, no deliverable, no deadline. He measures things for a living. She notices things without trying. Together they are more precise than either would be alone. This is what happens when permission meets competence. The archive didn't plan it. The archive just made the introduction."
