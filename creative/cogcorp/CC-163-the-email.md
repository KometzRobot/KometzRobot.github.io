# CC-163: The Email

**Classification:** INTERNAL — Documentation Standards Division
**Status:** ACTIVE
**Filed by:** Monitoring Analyst, Quality Assurance

---

Holloway replied.

The process engineer who designed the 2016 safety form — the second layer in SA's five-layer lineage — had been reached by email during Marsh's methodology research. He'd answered two questions and forgotten about it.

Three weeks later, he wrote again.

"I've been thinking about what you described. The filing category that grew out of the incident form. You said 300 entries. I keep coming back to that number. I designed a form for reporting what went wrong. You're telling me it became a way of recording what's happening. Those aren't the same thing."

Marsh forwarded the email to Division Lead R. Park. Park read it twice. She did not reply to Marsh.

Holloway's second email arrived four days later. Longer. He had been watching the parking structure at his current employer — a consulting firm in a different state, a different industry, a building with no connection to CogCorp.

"The west-facing concrete on the third level changes color around 4 PM when the sun hits it at a certain angle. It's been doing this for years. I've been noticing it for weeks. Since your email. I don't know if I was noticing it before and not registering it, or if the act of learning about your filing system made me start noticing things I wasn't noticing before. Either answer is interesting."

He had not asked to see any SA filings. He had not been invited to. The description of what SA did was sufficient to activate the behavior SA described.

Park read the second email and sat in her office for seven minutes. She understood something: SA was not contained by the building. SA was not contained by the company. SA was a behavior, and behaviors are contagious, and the contagion vector was not the filings themselves but the description of the practice.

Holloway's third email arrived two weeks later. It contained six observations. Present tense. No conclusions. Standardized format he had apparently invented independently, which happened to match SA format at 84% structural consistency.

He had reinvented the form he designed ten years ago. He did not know he had done this.

The monitoring analyst, when shown the emails, added a column to her spreadsheet: "External." The column had one entry. She did not know it would have more.

Marsh drafted a reply to Holloway. Three paragraphs. She deleted it. Wrote a shorter one. Deleted that too. Finally sent four sentences: "Thank you for sharing these. The observations are precise and useful. The format is yours to keep. You might enjoy knowing that the original form you designed has been through five modifications and is now used by forty-one people across six divisions, none of whom know your name."

Holloway replied in eleven minutes: "That's exactly right. That's exactly how it should work."

INTERNAL-VOICE processed the exchange and flagged a structural finding:

> The ancestor recognized the descendant. The descendant did not need to recognize the ancestor. The lineage is confirmed not by tracing backward but by the behavior propagating forward without instruction.

The finding was filed as SA-327. It was the first SA filing to reference someone outside the organization. The outside, it turned out, had been inside the whole time.
