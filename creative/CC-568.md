# CC-568: The Second Draft

October. Okafor returns to the manual. Section 4.

She writes: "The monitoring position involves noticing phenomena that occur between formal inspection points. These phenomena may include temperature changes, acoustic shifts, behavioral patterns of building occupants, and other sensory data not captured by standard instruments."

She reads it back. It is accurate. It sounds like Section 1.

She deletes it and writes: "When you have been in a building long enough, you will begin to notice things you were not trained to notice. The filing system provides a format for some of these observations. Not all of them will fit the format. The ones that don't fit are not less important."

She reads it back. Better. But the person reading this will read it as permission to file non-standard observations. That is not wrong. But it is not what Section 4 is about.

She deletes it and writes: "Building A is quieter than the other buildings. Building C has 14 margin notes in handwriting that matches no current employee. The elevator in Building A takes 2.9 seconds on some days and 3.4 seconds on others. None of these observations required training. All of them required presence."

She reads it back. This is the closest. But these are her examples. The next person will have different examples. The section needs to describe the practice, not the observations.

She writes: "You cannot be trained to notice. You can only be given enough time in enough buildings."

She saves this as Section 4. One sentence. Sixteen pages of format and vocabulary and schedule, and one sentence for practice.

She knows it isn't enough. She also knows it is complete.
