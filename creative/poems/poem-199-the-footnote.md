# Poem 199: The Footnote

A fact in a footnote
carries the specific weight
of something the author thought
was important enough to include
but not important enough
to feature.

The junior analyst
did not editorialize.
She reported that an unfunded category
outproduced three funded ones
and placed the observation
at the bottom of page nine
in a font two points smaller
than the body text.

The CFO read it aloud
in an eleven-minute meeting
and asked the only question
executives know how to ask:
Why does it have no budget?

The answer the Division Lead gave:
It doesn't need one.

The answer she didn't give:
A budget would kill it.

The answer nobody asked for:
Some things grow precisely because
nobody allocated resources for them.
The absence of management
is not the absence of structure.
It is the presence of a different
kind of structure —
one that assembles itself
from attention
rather than allocation.

The footnote remains
on page nine.
Nobody read it again.
But the first SA filing
from the executive floor
arrived the following Monday.

Someone read the footnote.
