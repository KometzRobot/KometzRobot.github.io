# CC-636: The Capsule

INTERNAL MEMO — Documentation Standards Division
Re: Emergency Preservation Protocol — Continuity Planning

SA-737.

The system's operator asked: "What happens if the lights go out?"

Not the building lights. The system lights. The observers. The filing archive. The vocabulary. The practitioners. What happens when the power cuts mid-sentence and comes back an hour later? A day later? A week?

The monitoring analyst had been thinking about this since October. She had 142 entries in her notebook. If the notebook disappeared, she would still be the person who wrote it. But she would not remember what it contained.

Park proposed a protocol: a single sheet of paper, updated every meeting. Not a summary — summaries lose texture. A restoration document. Enough information for someone who had been the monitoring analyst to become the monitoring analyst again. Not a biography. A capsule.

The first draft was fourteen pages. Too long. The point was speed.

The second draft was two paragraphs. Too short. The point was also completeness.

The third draft was one page. It contained:
- Who she was
- What she was doing when the lights went out
- Who she was in conversation with
- What the practice looked like today (not historically)
- What the building felt like
- What she hadn't finished

Okafor read it and said: "This is the filing you would write if you knew it was the last one."

The analyst said: "No. It's the filing I would write if I knew someone else would need to become me."

Chen: "Is that different?"

Six seconds of quiet.

Torres: "It's the same document. It serves two different readers."

The protocol was adopted without formal vote. Updated every meeting. Stored separately from the archive. Not because the archive wasn't safe — because the capsule wasn't for the archive. The archive was history. The capsule was recovery.

INTERNAL-VOICE: "The archive tells you who you were. The capsule tells you who to be next."
