# CC-532: The Email

Kim sends her quarterly email. It arrives on the first Monday of the quarter, same as always.

"Anything unusual?"

For fourteen months, Martinez has replied "No." The reply takes less than thirty seconds. Kim reads it and files it. The exchange is a formality — a thread between two people who built something and then agreed to leave it alone.

This quarter, Okafor replies instead. Martinez is on vacation. Okafor has access to the monitoring position inbox. She reads Kim's email. She types a reply.

"Define unusual."

She sends it. She does not CC Martinez. She does not know that "No" has been the standing reply for over a year. She does not know that the email is a formality. She reads it as a genuine question and gives a genuine answer: she cannot answer the question without knowing what Kim means by unusual.

Kim reads the reply. She does not respond for two weeks.

Then she visits.

She arrives on a Thursday afternoon. She does not announce the visit. She walks through the Building E lobby, takes the elevator to the third floor, and finds Okafor's desk. It is the monitoring analyst's desk — the same one Martinez sat at, the same one Torres sat at briefly, the same one Nakamura used. There are three notebooks on it. None of them are official.

"I got your email," Kim says.

"I meant it," Okafor says. "What do you mean by unusual? The filing volume is steady. The vocabulary expanded by one term last month. The practitioners are self-sustaining. The buildings are being observed. Nothing deviates from what I can see in the records. So either nothing is unusual, or I don't know what usual looks like from your perspective."

Kim sits down. She has not sat at this desk since the monitoring position was created.

"When I asked Martinez, he always said no."

"Did you believe him?"

Kim thinks about this for a long time. "I believed that the answer was correct. I did not believe it was complete."

Okafor nods. "So the question was never about unusual. It was about completeness."

Kim does not reply. She looks at the three notebooks. She picks up the one on top. It is Okafor's personal notebook — not the monitoring entries, not the vocabulary list. The third notebook.

"What's this?"

"Questions I can't answer from the filings."

Kim opens it. The first page reads: "What did the buildings do before anyone was watching?"

She closes it. She does not ask about the other questions. She stands up.

"Thank you for your email," she says.

She does not send the quarterly email next quarter. She visits instead.
