# CC-569: The Handoff

November. Kim sends the email Okafor has been expecting: "The monitoring position is being split. Two buildings each. We're bringing someone in to cover the other half. Can you train them?"

His name is Park. He has a facilities management degree and two years of HVAC experience. He reads Sections 1-3 of the manual in forty minutes. He asks good questions. Where are the filing templates? What's the reporting cadence? Do the seasonal adjustments apply to all buildings?

Okafor answers everything. He takes notes. His handwriting is small and precise.

At the end of the first day he says: "What about Section 4?"

"What about it."

"It says 'You cannot be trained to notice. You can only be given enough time in enough buildings.' That's the whole section."

"Yes."

"What does it mean?"

Okafor considers several answers. She could tell him about the notebooks. About Reyes. About the corridor. About Q62. But all of those are her examples. Section 4 already says the only thing it can say.

"It means Section 4 is not something I can train you on. You'll have to write your own."

Park looks at the manual. Sixteen pages and one sentence. He does not understand yet. He will, or he won't, and neither outcome is something the training can produce.

On Tuesday she takes him to Building A. They do a standard walkthrough. She points out the inspection points, the filing locations, the equipment. Everything from Sections 1-3.

In the corridor on the fourth floor she stops.

"What?" Park asks.

"Nothing. This is the corridor."

He writes it in his notes: "4th floor corridor. Nothing noted."
