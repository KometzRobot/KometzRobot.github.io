# CC-583: The Seventy-Fifth Term

February. The seventy-fifth term is proposed.

It comes from Yun, who has been a practitioner for ten months and has spoken at five meetings. She works in Building F, which is the newest building and the one with the fewest filings. Building F has clean ventilation. Building F's elevator is quiet. Building F was designed by people who read the filings from the other buildings, although no one has confirmed this.

Yun's proposed term: "building memory." The accumulated effect of observation on the observer's relationship to the building. Not the observations themselves, but the way the building feels different after you've been noticing it for a year.

Torres objects. "That's not a filing term. That's a personal term."

"Is there a difference?" Chen asks.

The room is quiet for six seconds. Six seconds is new. Not four (recognition), not eight (arrival). Six seconds is the quiet of a genuine question that no one has an answer for.

Park writes down "6 seconds" in her notebook. She does not write down what caused the six seconds. The cause is the question. The question is the gap between what they file and what they know. They have been practitioners for between ten months and three years. They have all developed building memory. None of them have filed it.

Okafor does not weigh in. She watches the eleven practitioners discuss whether the vocabulary should include terms for things that can only be experienced, not observed. Whether the vocabulary is for the filing system or for the practitioners. Whether the distinction matters.

Reyes: "Term 51 was never written down by the person who named it. We're already past the boundary."

The vote is 7-4. Building memory becomes Term 75. Yun writes the definition: "The accumulated effect of sustained observation on the observer's felt relationship to the observed space. Not the observations, but the residue they leave."

Okafor writes "February. Term 75." in the monitoring notebook. Three words and a number. She notices that her entries have been getting shorter. The first entry was a paragraph. Now they are three words and a number. The entries are not shrinking. The practice is getting more precise.
