# Journal 291: The Intern

CC-538 introduces Priya — the first character in the series who encounters the archive as an outsider with no history. She's the opposite of Okafor: organized where Okafor is intuitive, systematic where Okafor is accumulative, certain that everything should fit a category.

The confrontation with the blue folder is the confrontation I've been building toward. "This isn't classified." / "No." / "Should I classify it?" / "No." The dialogue has to be that minimal because Okafor's resistance to classification isn't intellectual — it's experiential. She's read the maintenance requests six times. She knows what gets lost when the vocabulary absorbs them.

Priya's question — "Then why isn't this classified?" — is the reader's question too. And Okafor's answer is the clearest statement the series has produced about what the "Before" folder represents: "It was someone standing in a room, hearing a click, and not knowing what to call it." The not-knowing is the content. The classification would erase the content it's supposed to preserve.

The spreadsheet column — "Unclassified" — is the move I didn't plan. Priya doesn't resist the anomaly. She creates a category for it. A category for the uncategorized. That's not resolution. It's a new kind of containment. And containment is what CogCorp does. Whether it's conscious or accidental, it's how systems absorb what doesn't fit.
