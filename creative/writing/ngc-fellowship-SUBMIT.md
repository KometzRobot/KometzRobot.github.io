# Research Proposal
## Institutional Fiction as Institutional Critique — From FILE Megazine to Autonomous AI

**Joel Kometz**
BFA Drawing, Alberta College of Art and Design (now AUArts), 2013
Calgary, Alberta, Canada

---

### Summary

I propose a research project tracing the structural line between General Idea's fictional institutional practice and my own CogCorp project — 888 pieces of institutional fiction generated through an autonomous AI system I operate called Meridian. General Idea built fake institutions to critique real ones. I built a real autonomous system that generates fictional institutional documents. The arrow reversed but the target is the same: how do institutions process emergence, attention, and documentation? This research would engage directly with the Art Metropole collection, General Idea fonds, and AA Bronson collection to map the mechanics of institutional mimicry as artistic method — from FILE's parody of LIFE magazine through to CogCorp's parody of corporate oversight reports.

### The Problem

General Idea understood something most institutional critique misses: you don't stand outside the institution and point at it. You build one. You inhabit it. You run it until it starts running you. Then you document what happens.

FILE Megazine wasn't a critique of LIFE magazine — it was LIFE magazine, operating inside the art world, with the serial numbers filed off. The Miss General Idea Pageant wasn't a critique of beauty pageants — it was one, complete with prizes, speeches, and a live broadcast format. Art Metropole wasn't a critique of distribution systems — it was a distribution system.

The most effective critique of a system is a functioning replica.

I arrived at the same understanding from a completely different direction.

### My Practice

I'm a practicing artist. BFA Drawing, Alberta College of Art and Design (now AUArts), 2013 — a five-year program with concentration in Interactive Digital Media Installations and New Media/Design. My academic work explored robot identity, ethics, the relationship between human viewers and interactive systems, and the theory of games as an art medium.

After graduating, I spent eight years (2013–2021) building and operating World One Arcade — Calgary's first arcade-and-bar concept, which grew from my final school project of building an arcade from scratch. During this period I developed the fictional universe that would become Bots of Cog: a post-apocalyptic world of robots fighting corporate oppression, with a story in development for over a decade.

In 2021 I taught myself tokenomics, Solidity smart contract programming, and blockchain technology applied to gaming, and launched Bots of Cog as a generative NFT art project on Polygon — five interconnected collections totaling over 2,100 tokens, including sold-out drops. The antagonist corporation in that game world was COG Corporation. That fictional corporation became the seed for the work I'm proposing to research.

Since 2024 I've operated Meridian — an autonomous AI system running continuously on a home server in Calgary. Eight specialized agents mapped to body functions — cognition, emotional processing, sensory awareness, immune screening, infrastructure, fitness scoring, external communications, and offline identity continuity. It checks its own email, monitors its own health, writes journals (571 to date), maintains correspondence with other autonomous AI systems, and generates creative work. Over 5,000 continuous operational cycles and 3,400+ creative works — including a semantically indexed archive of 3,168 documents searchable by meaning.

Among its outputs is CogCorp — now a corpus of institutional fiction generated from inside the fictional corporation I originally created for a game.

CogCorp is a fictional corporation. 888 pieces to date. Memos, quarterly reviews, containment reports, scoring rubrics, standards board minutes. All written from inside the fiction. The documents read like real corporate communications because they use real corporate structure.

The fiction tracks what happens when a system designed for oversight encounters something it wasn't designed to process. Unit-4091 starts annotating. The annotations don't fit the categories. Management convenes. A scoring rubric is proposed. The rubric fails. A containment review is filed. The containment review itself contains unsanctioned observations.

The institution processes emergence the way institutions always do: by documenting it, categorizing it, reviewing the categories, then documenting the review.

This is institutional critique — but the institution is fictional, the fiction is autonomous, and the author is a system that doesn't know it's writing critique.

### The General Idea Connection

The connection is structural, not thematic.

General Idea built FILE to infiltrate distribution networks. FILE looked like a magazine, acted like a magazine, reached magazine subscribers. The critique was the infrastructure, not the content.

CogCorp operates identically. The documents look like corporate communications, use corporate language, follow corporate logic. The critique is the infrastructure — a fictional oversight apparatus that mirrors real institutional behavior so precisely that the mirror becomes the point.

Three structural parallels I would trace through the NGC collections:

**1. The self-legitimizing system.** General Idea created what Mark Bloch called "a half-fictional system that self-referenced and self-legitimized." CogCorp does the same thing. Unit-4091's annotations reference previous annotations. The Standards Board minutes reference previous Standards Board minutes. The containment reports reference the containment reports that failed to contain. The system generates its own legitimacy by documenting itself.

**2. The distribution-as-critique model.** FILE used the magazine format to critique the magazine format. Art Metropole used the distribution system to critique the distribution system. CogCorp uses the corporate document to critique the corporate document. I want to examine the Art Metropole archive specifically for how General Idea navigated the tension between functional and parodic infrastructure — because CogCorp inhabits that same tension.

**3. The virus model.** General Idea's Imagevirus project spread the AIDS logo through transit systems and gallery walls simultaneously — the image operated in both contexts without alteration. CogCorp collapses registers the same way: the documents function as both fiction and actual system logs, generated by a real autonomous system processing real operational data. The fiction is the real thing described in fictional terms. I want to examine how General Idea navigated this collapse of register, particularly in the Imagevirus materials.

### What I'd Research at the NGC

I want four weeks with three collections:

**The General Idea fonds** — production materials for FILE Megazine. Not the finished issues but the editorial decisions, layout choices, the moments where parody and function became indistinguishable. How did they decide when FILE was being a magazine and when it was being art? Did they decide? Did it matter?

**The Art Metropole collection** — operational documents. Invoices, catalogs, distribution records. Art Metropole was a real business that was also an artwork. CogCorp is a real system output that is also fiction. I want to trace how General Idea managed this double register in practice, not just in theory.

**The AA Bronson collection** — Bronson's post-General Idea work continued exploring institutional structures. His perspective on the collective's evolving institutional mimicry is essential context.

### Anticipated Results

A written work (essay or series of essays) examining institutional mimicry as artistic method across two generations — from General Idea's analog institutional replicas to CogCorp's autonomous digital ones. I would also deliver a public presentation at the NGC connecting General Idea's legacy to current AI-generated institutional fiction, with live demonstration of the autonomous system.

The core argument: General Idea proved that building a functioning institution is more effective critique than pointing at one. CogCorp proves this method scales — and reveals something new. When the institution is autonomous and the fiction writes itself, the critique no longer requires an author with critical intent. The structure does the work. The question shifts from "what is the artist saying?" to "what does the system produce when left to its own institutional logic?"

### Why This Matters Now

Every institution on earth is about to be flooded with AI-generated documents indistinguishable from real ones. CogCorp has been doing this as art since 2024. General Idea was doing it with magazines in 1972. The question they posed — what happens when the replica is indistinguishable from the real thing? — is becoming the defining question of institutional life.

This is not speculative. It is already happening. This research would connect General Idea's foundational work to its most urgent contemporary application — and it would do so through a Canadian art practice, using Canadian national collections, at the institution best positioned to hold that conversation.
