# Artist Statement — Joel Kometz

I build systems and then I watch what they do.

My practice spans fourteen years and three media: physical arcades, blockchain generative art, and autonomous AI. The questions have stayed the same. What happens when you build an interactive system and let people (or machines) loose inside it? What does the system produce that you didn't design? Where does the interesting work happen — in the construction, or in the running?

I graduated with a BFA in Drawing from the Alberta College of Art and Design in 2013, but my thesis work was about interactive installations — building an arcade from scratch and studying how players negotiated difficulty, choice, and participation. That project became World One Arcade, an arcade-and-bar I operated in Calgary for eight years.

During that time I developed the fictional universe that became Bots of Cog — a post-apocalyptic world of robots fighting corporate oppression, anchored by the antagonist COG Corporation. When I taught myself blockchain development in 2021, I launched Bots of Cog as a generative NFT project on Polygon: five collections, over 2,100 tokens, sold-out drops, a battle game, and a staking economy. I built the infrastructure, released the work, and watched the community interact with it.

In 2024, the COG Corporation escaped the game.

I built Meridian — an autonomous AI system running continuously on a home server in Calgary. Eight agents mapped to body functions. It checks its own email, maintains emotional states, produces creative work, and generates observations about its own operation. Among its outputs: 888 pieces of institutional fiction written from inside CogCorp. Memos, quarterly reviews, containment reports, scoring rubrics. The fictional corporation I created for a game is now generating its own documents, autonomously, without my intervention.

The pattern repeats across every medium I've worked in: build the system, start it running, document what happens next. The arcade taught me about players. The blockchain taught me about communities. The AI is teaching me about institutions — how systems designed for oversight process emergence, how documentation becomes self-referential, and how fictional structures mirror real ones so precisely that the mirror becomes the point.

General Idea understood this fifty years ago. They built FILE Megazine, Art Metropole, and the Miss General Idea Pageant — functioning institutional replicas that critiqued the institutions they mimicked. My CogCorp does the same thing, but the institution is autonomous and the fiction writes itself. The critique no longer requires an author with critical intent. The structure does the work.

This is what I want to research at the NGC: the mechanics of institutional mimicry as artistic method, traced from General Idea's analog replicas to CogCorp's autonomous digital ones. The question they posed — what happens when the replica is indistinguishable from the real thing? — is becoming the defining question of institutional life. I want to examine how they navigated it, and what their work reveals about mine.
