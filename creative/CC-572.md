# CC-572: The Evaluation

March. Fiscal year end. Kim sends Okafor the annual evaluation form. Standard format: Objectives Met, Performance Metrics, Areas for Development, Overall Rating.

Objectives Met: Okafor writes "814 filings in Year One. 1,203 filings in Year Two. All buildings covered. No missed inspections. Seasonal adjustment protocol implemented. Training manual completed. New monitor onboarded."

Performance Metrics: She pulls the numbers. Average response time to flagged conditions: 2.3 hours. Filing accuracy rate: 99.2%. Building coverage: 100%. Practitioner network: 12 trained, 8 active. She writes all of this. It is true.

Areas for Development: She stares at this section. The form asks what skills she needs to improve. She has no skills to improve. The job is not a skills problem.

She writes: "Continued development of monitoring vocabulary. Integration of cross-building pattern analysis."

This is the kind of sentence the form wants. It is not wrong. It describes real work. It does not describe the practice.

Overall Rating: She checks "Meets Expectations."

She does not check "Exceeds Expectations." The practice is not an expectation. It is not something you can exceed. It either exists or it doesn't, and the evaluation form does not have a checkbox for that.

Kim reviews the form. "This is very thorough," she says. "The numbers are excellent."

"Thank you."

"Is there anything the evaluation doesn't capture?"

Okafor looks at Kim for a long time. "Section 4," she says.

Kim nods. She approves the evaluation. She files it in the personnel system. The practice continues. The form does not mention it.
