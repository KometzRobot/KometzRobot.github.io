# CC-582: January

January. The first meeting of the fourth year.

Okafor arrives at 9:07, which is three minutes early, which is the time she always arrives. The room has been set up by someone — eleven chairs, the projector, a carafe of water that no one ever uses. She does not know who sets up the room. She has never asked.

The practitioners arrive. Park first, then Chen, then Davis and Torres together, then the rest in no particular order. Reyes is last. He has been last since August.

No one mentions that this is the beginning of a new year. The practice does not observe its own milestones. CC-100 passed without notice. The five hundredth filing passed without notice. The vocabulary's seventy-fifth term will pass without notice, whenever it happens. The practitioners do not count. Counting is something the system does. The practitioners practice.

Park presents January data. Filing frequency: 347 this quarter. Cross-building correlations: consistent. No new patterns. No old patterns have disappeared. The buildings continue to be buildings. The practitioners continue to notice.

Chen has a question. He has been reading the vocabulary document on the bus — he has been doing this since the document was thirty-one terms — and he has noticed something.

"The early terms are all verbs. Filing instinct. Observation cascade. Pattern recognition. The middle terms are adjectives or states. Ambient inventory. Temporal margin. Functional echo. The late terms are..." He pauses. "Relationships. Between things."

"Between what things?" Park asks.

Chen thinks about this. "Between the practitioner and the building. Between the observation and the act of observing. Between the vocabulary and the practice the vocabulary describes."

The room is quiet for four seconds. Not eight. Four seconds is the quiet of recognition. Eight seconds is the quiet of arrival. The practitioners have learned the difference.

Okafor writes in the meeting notes: "Chen observes that vocabulary evolution follows a developmental pattern: actions → states → relationships."

After the meeting, no one stays behind. This is the first time since August that no one has stayed behind. The room empties. Okafor sits for two minutes. She does not write anything. She does not open the blue folder. She looks at the eleven chairs pushed back at eleven new angles.

She turns off the projector. She picks up the carafe of water — still full, as always — and carries it to the kitchen. She rinses it. She puts it in the drying rack. She returns to the room and stacks the chairs against the wall, which is not something she usually does but which today seems like the thing to do.

The room is empty. The room was empty before the practice and will be empty after it. The practice is what happens between the two empties.
