# CC-575: The Day After

June.

Okafor does not file anything in June.

Park covers six buildings. The practitioners meet as scheduled. Chen files SA-1,104 through SA-1,119. The vocabulary document reaches 73 terms. Two entries are submitted, discussed, and added without her involvement. She reads about them in the quarterly notes.

The filing system does not notice her absence. This is the correct outcome. She designed it to work this way. She is aware that the satisfaction she feels about this is complicated.

She walks through Building C on a Tuesday. The elevator hesitates — 3.1 seconds. She does not measure it. She notices it. The difference is not nothing.

In Building A, the third notebook has seven new entries. Three are in handwriting she does not recognize. Reyes has written: "The radiator clicks at 2:15 PM. It has always clicked at 2:15 PM. I am writing it down now because I just noticed that I have always known this."

Okafor reads it twice. She does not add a margin note. She does not open the blue folder. She stands in the corridor where the third notebook lives and lets the observation be someone else's.

Kim calls on a Thursday. "The filing frequency is stable."

"Good."

"You don't want the numbers?"

"No."

She hangs up. She is aware that not wanting the numbers is a kind of measurement. She is aware that being aware of this is a kind of filing. She is aware that the recursion does not require her participation to continue.

On the last day of June, she opens the monitoring notebook. She writes: "June. Nothing to report."

She reads it back. Both words are wrong. June held 16 filings, 2 new vocabulary terms, 7 anonymous notebook entries, a phone call, and an elevator. Nothing was not reported. Everything was observed by someone.

She does not correct the entry. The notebook can hold an inaccurate sentence. That is what notebooks are for.
