# CC-528: The Annual Report

The monitoring analyst position requires an annual summary. Martinez writes it.

"MONITORING ANALYST POSITION MA-7 — ANNUAL REVIEW

Period: March through February. Analysts: 2 (rotation at Q3).

Filing compliance: 98.7%. All environmental and maintenance filings met documentation standards. Minor formatting deviations in Q1 (resolved through standard correction procedures). No systemic compliance issues identified.

Filing volume: 728 supplementary analyses filed across the review period. This represents a 340% increase over the previous period. The increase is attributed to expanded environmental observation protocols and seasonal reporting requirements.

Staffing: One rotation completed at Q3. Transition was smooth. The incoming analyst (Nakamura) demonstrated strong familiarity with documentation standards. No training gap identified.

Recommendation: Continue position at current staffing level. No changes to scope or authority recommended."

Every sentence is true. None of them describe what happened.

The 340% increase is not from "expanded protocols." It is from twelve people who learned to pay attention to buildings. The "minor formatting deviations" were the first supplementary analyses — documents that didn't fit any existing format because they described things no format anticipated. The "strong familiarity" Nakamura demonstrated was not familiarity with standards. It was familiarity with the practice itself, which she had been doing independently for eleven months in another building.

Martinez reads the report back. It is the most technically accurate and most completely misleading document he has ever written.

Kim reads it. "This is perfect," she says.

"It's not perfect. It's a translation."

"Yes. And the translation protects the original."

Martinez thinks about this. The annual report, by describing the practice in institutional language, makes the practice invisible to the institution. The institution sees compliance. The practitioners see each other. The monitoring analyst sees the building. The report sees numbers.

He submits it. It is approved in four minutes. No one asks a follow-up question.

Nakamura, reading the approved report, adds a note to her personal file: "The annual report described everything accurately and nothing truthfully. This is how a practice survives inside an institution: by being translatable into the institution's language without being reducible to it."

She does not file this as an entry. She does not file it at all. Some observations are for the observer.
