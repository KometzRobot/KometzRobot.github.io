# CC-547: Two Descriptions

Okafor finds the match by accident. She is refiling SA-891 — Chen's three-page observation of the Building E freight elevator — when she notices Davis filed SA-588 three days later. Same elevator. Same hesitation at floors 2 and 5.

Chen, SA-891:

"The freight elevator in Building E exhibits a consistent hesitation pattern at floors 2 and 5, lasting approximately 3.2 seconds. This behavior constitutes functional drift (Term 55) — an unintended mechanical pattern that has been incorporated into occupant navigation through implicit adaptation. The hesitation generates a distinct temporal signature (Term 12) that regular users have internalized, creating an acoustic expectation (Term 31) at each pause point. Occupants no longer press the call button on these floors, having adapted their timing to the elevator's rhythm."

Davis, SA-588:

"The freight elevator pauses at 2 and 5. People wait without pressing the button. The pause has been there longer than I have."

Okafor reads both filings three times. She places them side by side on her desk.

Chen's filing tells her what the building is doing. It categorizes, measures, connects the observation to three vocabulary terms, identifies a process, and names it. It is precise. It is comprehensive. It is the vocabulary working exactly as intended.

Davis's filing tells her what the building is like. It describes without categorizing. It locates the observation in time — not measured time, but personal time. "Longer than I have." It includes the people without analyzing them. It includes the pause without naming it.

Same elevator. Same hesitation. Same floors.

Question 52: "Which description is the building?"

She writes the answer immediately, for the first time since question 44:

"Both. But only one of them would survive if the vocabulary disappeared."
