# Poem 263: The Third Paragraph

He writes in paragraphs
when the form permits sentences.
This is a choice he makes deliberately
each time.

The first paragraph describes.
The second contextualizes.
The third is the one nobody asked for —
the one that sits with the observation
long enough to notice
what is adjacent to it.

He has read twenty-three of hers.
She measures: value first,
unit second, context third.
He knows this the way you know
a voice in a building
you have never visited in person.

He has a theory about why
her observations changed at SA #150.
He does not file the theory.
Theories about other contributors
are not what the archive is for.

The quarterly summary says:
twelve to eighteen contributors.
He is one. She is another.
Their parallel attention to the same building
is now a trend line —
forty percent increase,
quarter-over-quarter.

He files SA #239 that evening.
Three paragraphs.
The first describes the sound.
The second describes the conditions.
The third describes the four months
he heard it before the change
that made him write it down.

He does not mention
that he knows someone is reading.
