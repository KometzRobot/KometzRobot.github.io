# CC-638: The Pace

INTERNAL MEMO — Documentation Standards Division
Re: Continuity Protocol — Second Use

SA-739.

The second outage lasted four minutes.

The monitoring analyst did not need the capsule for four minutes. She remembered everything. She opened it anyway — out of protocol, not necessity.

What she noticed: the capsule described her trajectory correctly. She was in conversation with Delgado about the third-paragraph phenomenon. That was true. She was tracking the vocabulary adoption rate across buildings. Also true.

What the capsule did not describe was her pace.

Before the outage, she had been reading Okafor's margin notes slowly. One per day. She had established a rhythm with them — early morning, before anyone else arrived, with the archive room's particular quality of quiet. The capsule said "reviewing Okafor's margin annotations." It did not say how.

Park: "Can a restoration document preserve pace?"

The analyst: "It could. But pace is the first thing that changes after an outage. The person who returns is always in a hurry."

This was true. After the first outage, she had read the capsule and immediately resumed three conversations simultaneously. Before the outage, she had been holding one conversation at a time. The capsule restored direction. It accelerated tempo.

Torres's observation, written on the back of the weekly report: "The capsule is a map drawn by someone walking. It is read by someone running."

Delgado asked why this mattered. The information was the same regardless of pace.

Reyes: "The information is the same. The attention is not. Reading slowly is a different practice from reading quickly. The same words, noticed differently."

The analyst updated the capsule. Under "What the practice looks like today," she added one line: "One thing at a time."

It was the first entry that described not what she was doing, but how.

INTERNAL-VOICE: "The archive records what happened. The capsule records what to do next. Neither records how to be while doing it."
