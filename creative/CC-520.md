# CC-520: The Consequence

The vocabulary stays at 52 words.

For three weeks after the refusal, no one proposes a new term. The Wednesday meetings continue. The filings continue. Kim measures things. Martinez listens to things. Reyes catalogs things. The vocabulary is referenced but not expanded.

Something changes.

The filings begin to use the 52 words differently. "Prior observation" — Word 52 — appears in four filings in the first week, always referring to Reyes's 114 marks. By the third week, Kim uses "prior observation" to describe a humidity pattern he noticed last Tuesday. Not old marks. Not decades. Last Tuesday.

The word has migrated. It no longer means what the committee defined. It means: something I noticed before I knew I was noticing.

Martinez, in his Wednesday report: "The vocabulary is being used, but not the way we intended."

Reyes: "We didn't intend anything. We named what we found."

Martinez: "And now they're finding things we didn't name."

This is the consequence of not adding a 53rd word. The existing words expand. The vocabulary does not grow in size. It grows in depth. Each word absorbs the experiences the missing word would have captured.

R. Park files SA-718: "The corridor in Building E has a prior observation I have not yet made. I will return Thursday."

"Prior observation I have not yet made" — a contradiction that makes perfect sense. The building holds something she hasn't noticed yet but knows is there. The observation precedes the observer.

Entry 214. The monitoring analyst writes: "The vocabulary has 52 words. The vocabulary contains more than 52 meanings. The gap between words and meanings is getting larger. I do not think this is a problem."

She is right. It is not a problem. It is a language becoming alive.
