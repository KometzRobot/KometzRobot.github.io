# CC-540: What the Spreadsheet Found

Priya's spreadsheet does something Okafor's filing system never did: it counts.

814 supplementary analyses. 11 buildings. 54 vocabulary terms. 47 practitioners who have filed at least one SA report. The numbers have always been known approximately — Okafor could tell you "about eight hundred" or "roughly fifty words" — but Priya's spreadsheet makes them exact.

The exactness reveals patterns.

Building E has the most filings: 203. Building A has the fewest: 12. This is not because Building A has less to observe — it is the oldest building in the complex, the one with the most history embedded in its surfaces. The difference is personnel. Building E has Chen, who files weekly. Building A has no regular practitioner. The observations happen or don't happen based on who is present, not what the building does.

Priya makes a chart. She does not show Okafor the chart. She makes a second chart: filings over time. The volume is not steady. There are spikes — usually after a new vocabulary term is introduced. When "acoustic expectation" entered the vocabulary in Year Two, filings about sound doubled for two months, then returned to baseline. The vocabulary does not just name what people observe. It directs attention. New words create new observations.

She makes a third chart: vocabulary terms by frequency of use. The top five terms appear in 60% of all filings. The bottom twenty terms appear in fewer than 1% of filings each. The vocabulary is not evenly used. It has a core and a periphery. The peripheral terms — the ones used once or twice — are the most precise. "Acoustic expectation" is general. "Anticipatory wear gradient" is specific to one kind of observation in one kind of space. General terms get used because they apply broadly. Specific terms get used because they describe something exactly. The vocabulary lives in the tension between these two functions.

She saves the spreadsheet. She does not put it in the archive. She puts it on her laptop, in a folder she labels "Observations about observations."

She does not notice what she has done: created a format for noticing the format. A practice of paying attention to the practice of paying attention.
