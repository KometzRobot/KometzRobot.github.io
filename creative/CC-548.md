# CC-548: What the Folder Holds

Okafor does not put Chen's filing in the blue folder. She does not put Davis's filing there either. Both belong in the regular binders, under their respective SA numbers, properly filed.

What she puts in the blue folder is a sheet of paper. She writes on it herself, in the same handwriting she uses for questions:

"SA-891 (Chen) and SA-588 (Davis). Same elevator. Same hesitation. Same floors. Different descriptions. The comparison is the observation."

She folds it once and places it in the folder. Item six.

The blue folder now holds:
1. Before (Reeves)
2. Outside (Monday notes)
3. Visual (photograph)
4. In use (notebook)
5. After (Chen's page three)
6. Between (the comparison)

She looks at the list. She does not write it down — she sees it.

The folder's organizing principle is not "unclassified." It is not "things that don't fit." It is the space between what the system can describe. Each item is a gap.

Reeves: the gap between building behavior and available vocabulary.
Monday notes: the gap between observation and format.
Photograph: the gap between what is seen and what can be written.
Notebook: the gap between thinking and archiving.
Chen's page three: the gap between the system and itself.
The comparison: the gap between two ways of describing the same thing.

The archive holds observations. The blue folder holds the gaps between observations. The practice is both.

Question 53: "Is the folder growing because the practice is deepening, or because I am noticing more gaps?"

She does not answer. She closes the folder.
