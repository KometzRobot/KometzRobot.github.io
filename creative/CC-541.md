# CC-541: The Last Day

On her last day, Priya brings Okafor a printed copy of the spreadsheet. It is forty-seven pages. It includes every filing, every building, every practitioner, every vocabulary term, every cross-reference, and four charts that Okafor will never learn to read.

"This is the archive," Priya says. "In a format you can search."

"I can search the archive."

"You can search the archive because you remember where everything is. This is searchable for someone who doesn't remember."

Okafor puts the printout on her desk. She looks at it the way she looks at new SA filings — carefully, without commitment.

"You'll be fine," Priya says. "The system runs itself. The practitioners file, the vocabulary grows, the binders fill. My spreadsheet just shows what's already happening."

"What about the Unclassified column?"

Priya smiles. "It still has four entries. I thought it would grow. I went through every drawer, every cabinet, every shelf. There's only four things in this archive that don't fit the system. Everything else either has an SA number or doesn't exist."

"That's not true," Okafor says. "There are observations that never get filed. Practitioners see things and don't write them down. The ones who file are the ones comfortable with the format. The ones who don't file might be noticing things the format can't hold."

Priya sits down. She has not considered this. The spreadsheet counts what exists. It cannot count what doesn't.

"That's not measurable," she says.

"No."

"You can't put it in a spreadsheet."

"No."

"Then how do you know it's happening?"

"Because I talk to them. Chen tells me things at the coffee machine that don't appear in his filings. Davis describes patterns she can't articulate. Vasquez observed for twenty-nine days before filing once. The filings are the part of the practice that survives the format. They are not the practice."

Priya looks at her spreadsheet. She looks at the forty-seven pages that map every formal artifact the archive contains. She looks at the four Unclassified entries that mark the formal boundary of the system.

"So the real Unclassified column," she says, "is everything that's not in the archive."

"Yes."

"And there's no way to count it."

"No. That's what makes it unclassified."

Priya picks up the printout and writes something on the first page, in pencil, in the margin: "This document describes the visible practice. The invisible practice is larger."

Okafor reads it. She does not say anything for a long time.

"Can I keep this?" she asks.

"I made you two copies."
