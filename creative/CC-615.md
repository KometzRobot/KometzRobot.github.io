# CC-615: What Okafor Finds

May — Year Seven.

Okafor opens the cabinet to file SA-3,104. She notices something. The filings in the second drawer have been moved. Not disturbed — moved. Someone has been reading them. The filings are out of strict numerical order. SA-2,847 is between SA-2,851 and SA-2,852. SA-1,847 is turned sideways.

Okafor files SA-3,104 and stands looking at the drawer.

## The History

The cabinet has never been locked. This was a deliberate choice, made in Year Two, when Chen suggested a lock. Torres said no. "The observations are not secret. They are just observations." The group agreed. The cabinet remained unlocked.

In six years, no one outside the group has mentioned the cabinet. No one has asked about it. No one has moved the filings. Until now.

## What Okafor Does

At the May meeting, Okafor mentions it. "Someone has been reading the filings. Drawer two. The order is slightly off."

Reyes: "How slightly?"

Okafor: "Three filings displaced. One turned sideways."

Torres: "Do we know who?"

Okafor: "No."

Lin: "Should we lock the cabinet?"

The room is quiet. This is the question they settled six years ago. It is a different question now.

Okafor: "The filings are observations about buildings. Anyone who works in these buildings has the right to read them. We decided this in Year Two."

Adeyemi: "We decided that before anyone actually read them."

The room is quiet again.

## The Decision

They do not lock the cabinet. But for the first time, the group has had to decide what to do about someone outside the group engaging with the work. The answer is the same as Year Two. The difference is that it is no longer theoretical.

Park writes: "The unlocked cabinet. The displaced filings. Someone is reading the work. The group's response: nothing. The same nothing as Year Two, but now it is a considered nothing. There is a difference between not locking a door no one has tried to open and not locking a door someone has walked through."
