# CC-592: The Filing System

The filing system was not designed. It accumulated.

The first filing was Torres's, in Year One: a paragraph about the stairwell in Building A. It was handwritten on a form intended for maintenance requests. The "Nature of Request" field said "observation." The "Action Required" field said "none."

Facilities processed it. They filed it under Supplementary Analyses because it did not fit anywhere else. The category had existed since the building opened. It had contained three items in eleven years: a noise complaint that turned out to be aesthetic rather than structural, a note about the color of the emergency lighting, and a photograph of a hallway with no explanation attached.

Torres's observation became SA-4. Then Reyes filed SA-5. Then Chen filed SA-6 through SA-9 in a single week, which was unusual enough that someone in Facilities mentioned it at a staff meeting.

No one investigated. No one objected. The filings were harmless. The forms were free. The filing system had room.

## What the Filing System Contains

As of the end of Year Four: 498 supplementary analyses across 6 buildings. The filings describe:

- Acoustic properties of stairwells, hallways, elevator shafts, and lobbies
- Changes in ambient sound across seasons, times of day, and weather conditions
- The felt difference between observed and unobserved spaces
- Vocabulary terms for phenomena that did not previously have names
- Observations about the act of observing

The filing system does not know it contains a practice. The filing system is a set of folders in a cabinet in a room in Building C that no one visits unless they are filing or retrieving. The folders are labeled by number. The numbers do not tell you what is inside.

## What the Filing System Does Not Contain

Park's paper. Park's notebook. Okafor's monitoring notebook. Torres's second notebook, which he started in Year Two and has never mentioned. The conversations that happen after meetings. The six seconds of quiet. The four seconds of recognition. The eight seconds of arrival.

The filing system contains what was formalized. The practice contains everything else.

## The Room

The room with the filing cabinet is on the second floor of Building C. It has fluorescent lighting and a window that faces the parking lot. The cabinet is gray. The folders are manila. The labels are typed.

No one has decorated the room. No one has claimed it. It is a room with a cabinet in it. The cabinet contains 498 observations about what it feels like to pay attention to buildings.

The room does not know this. The cabinet does not know this. The folders do not know this.

The practitioners know this. That is enough.
