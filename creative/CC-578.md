# CC-578: September

September. Park investigates.

She does not call it an investigation. She calls it a "cross-building filing pattern study," which sounds like something that could appear in a quarterly report without anyone reading it twice. She visits each building. She does not ask why people file when they file. She asks what they notice when they're not filing.

In Building E, Chen says: "The elevator. Always the elevator. It's become the clock I check." He pauses. "But I don't check it on purpose anymore. I just know what 3.2 seconds feels like."

In Building C, Torres says: "The margins." She means the physical margins of the paper filings, where she writes notes that do not belong in the official record. "The margins are where I put the things I'm not sure about yet."

In Building A, Reyes says: "Nothing." Then, after a silence that Park does not fill: "I notice things that already happened. Does that count?"

In the warehouse complex, Adeyemi says: "The loading dock smells different on Wednesdays. I've been meaning to write that down for three months."

"Why haven't you?"

"Because the moment I write it down, it becomes an observation. Right now it's just — a Wednesday thing."

Park writes all of this in a notebook she bought specifically for this purpose. It has a blue cover, which she did not choose deliberately. She notices the color on the third day of interviews. She does not mention this to Okafor.

She presents her findings in a two-page memo. The memo says: "The practitioners notice more than they file. The unfiled observations form a continuous background. The filed observations are selections from that background, not additions to it. Filing frequency does not measure attention. It measures the decision to formalize attention."

Okafor reads the memo at her desk. She reads it again. She opens the blue folder. She puts the memo inside. Item four.

She had told herself the folder was complete at three. The folder disagrees.
