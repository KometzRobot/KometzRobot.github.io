# CC-608: The Audit

August — Year Six.

The department head who asked for deliverables has not forgotten. He sends an email to the facilities coordinator, CC'd to his own manager, requesting "a brief audit of non-project resource utilization in shared spaces."

The email does not mention the practice. It does not mention Conference Room 4. It mentions "recurring bookings without project codes" as a category.

The facilities coordinator compiles a list. There are eleven recurring bookings without project codes across the campus. Most are informal lunch groups, departmental social gatherings, or training sessions that predate the current booking system. One is the second Tuesday meeting.

## The List

The audit is filed. Eleven bookings. Eleven one-line descriptions. The second Tuesday meeting appears as: "Cross-departmental observation and documentation working group. Contact: Okafor, S. Duration: 6 years. Participants: 12. Status: Active."

It is the only entry with more than three years of continuous booking. It is the only entry with participants from nine departments. It is the only entry that describes itself as a "working group."

The audit concludes: "No action required. All bookings serve documented institutional or social functions. Recommend annual review of bookings exceeding five years."

Annual review. The practice now has an annual review cycle. It has never had a review cycle before. It has never needed one.

## What Okafor Says

At the September meeting, Okafor mentions the audit. "We appeared on a facilities audit. 'Non-project resource utilization.' We are listed as a cross-departmental working group. The audit recommended no action."

Torres: "Should we be concerned?"

Okafor: "About what?"

Torres: "About being on a list."

Okafor: "We have always been on a list. The booking system is a list. This is a different list."

Adeyemi: "Lists don't forget."

The room is quiet.

## What Park Writes

Park writes: "The audit. The practice is now on two lists: the booking system and a facilities audit. Okafor is right — the booking was always a list. But Adeyemi is also right. Lists don't forget. The booking system is administrative — it tracks a room. The audit is evaluative — it tracks a purpose. The practice now has a purpose on record. Fourteen words and a recommendation for annual review."

She writes: "The practice was invisible for five years. In six months of Year Six, it has acquired: a description, a departmental inquiry, an audit entry, and a review cycle. None of these were requested. All of them are consequences of a facilities coordinator doing her job."

She adds: "The practice did nothing different. The environment changed. Legibility is not a choice. It is a condition."
