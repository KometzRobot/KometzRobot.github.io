# CC-584: Park's Paper

March. Park writes a paper.

She does not intend to write a paper. She intends to write a summary of the notebook project — the interviews with six practitioners about their personal documentation. The summary is supposed to be three pages. It is eleven.

The eleven pages describe something that does not have a term in the vocabulary: the relationship between what practitioners file and what they keep. Not the filings and not the notebooks, but the gap between them. The gap is not empty. The gap is where the practice lives.

Park reads the eleven pages three times. She changes four sentences. She realizes it is a paper when she adds citations — not academic citations, but references to specific filings. SA-47, which was Torres's first observation about the stairwell acoustics. SA-183, which was Reyes's description of how the radiator clicking at 2:15 PM had changed over two years. SA-312, which was Adeyemi's first filing after she finally wrote down the Wednesday thing.

She does not know what to do with a paper. She is not an academic. She is a practitioner who interviews other practitioners. The paper is not for a journal. The paper is not for Okafor. The paper is not for the filing system. The paper is for the gap.

She puts it in a manila folder. She does not label the folder. She puts the folder in her desk drawer, which already contains her notebook (47 entries, the most recent from two weeks ago), a broken pen, and a photograph of a door that Torres gave her for reasons neither of them discussed.

At the March meeting, she does not mention the paper. She presents the March data: 352 filings, two new vocabulary proposals (both tabled for further discussion), one building audit (passed). The meeting runs thirty-eight minutes. No one stays behind.

The paper sits in the manila folder in Park's desk drawer. It is the most complete description of what the practitioners do that anyone has written. No one has read it. No one knows it exists.

Park knows it exists. That is enough.
